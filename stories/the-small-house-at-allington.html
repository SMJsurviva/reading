The Small House at Allington

Anthony Trollope (1864) — retold in plain English, in its own Victorian England.
Chapter one — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. This is Anthony Trollope’s The Small House at Allington, retold in simple English but kept in its own world: the English countryside about 160 years ago. It is the story of two sisters in a small house — and above all of Lily Dale, who gives her whole heart and will not learn how to give only part of it.

Chapter One — The Two Houses

There were two houses at Allington, and this whole story happens between them.

The Great House was old and square and handsome, with tall windows and a garden that people drove miles to look at. Dales had lived in it for four hundred years. They were not a famous family. No Dale had ever done anything remarkable, and no Dale had ever done anything shameful, and in the English countryside that counts as a kind of greatness.

The squire of Allington was Christopher Dale, and he was exactly what the house had made him. He was past sixty. He had never married. As a young man he had loved one woman with his whole careful heart, and she had married a richer one, and he had closed that door quietly and never stood near it again. He was honest. He was fair. He was stiff as a gatepost. He loved his family the way he paid his taxes: fully, on time, and without one word more than was necessary.

The Small House stood a garden's length away, inside the same grounds. It had been built long ago for the mothers of squires. When a son took the Great House, his widowed mother crossed the garden, and that was the way of it for generations. It was not really small, either — it only felt small when the Great House was looking at it.

In the Small House lived Mrs Dale and her two daughters.

Mrs Dale had married the squire's youngest brother, Philip, who was the warm one of the family, and who died young, as the warm ones sometimes do. He left her very little money and two small girls. The squire did then what the squire always did. He did the right thing, coldly. He offered her the Small House, rent-free, for as long as she should want it. He sent vegetables from his garden and fruit from his wall and milk from his dairy, and hay for the girls' pony. He gave everything, in fact, except the one thing that would have made the rest easy to take. In eighteen years he had never once said: I am glad you are here.

So Mrs Dale had accepted the house for her daughters' sake, and she paid the rent no one asked of her. She paid it in pride. She did not dine at the Great House more than duty required. She asked for nothing, ever. The two houses were on perfectly good terms, the way two countries are on perfectly good terms when there is a treaty and no love.

The daughters minded all this much less than their mother did, because the garden between the houses belonged to their childhood, and you cannot be proud and stiff about the place where you learned to walk.

Bell was the elder, and she was steady. She was tall and fair and unhurried, and she had the kind of calm that is not dullness, though hasty people sometimes filed it there. When Bell was sure of a thing, she was sure of it all the way down, and no one could shift her — but it took her a long time to be sure, and she never pretended before she was.

Lily was nineteen, and Lily is the one this book is about. She was quicker than her sister, and browner, and not as tall. She teased everyone she loved, which was her way of saying so. She made a room feel better lit. If there was a weakness in her, it was this: when Lily gave anything, she gave the whole of it, and she did not know how to take part of it back. Some people learn that trick early. Lily was never going to learn it at all.

The gardens of both houses were kept by one man, a gardener called Hopkins, who had been at Allington longer than anyone could clearly remember. Strictly speaking, Hopkins served the squire. In his own understanding, the gardens were his, both of them, and the two families were people he allowed to walk there. He was rude to everyone equally, out of fairness, and he would have died for any of them, though he would have grumbled about it first.

The squire had a plan. He did not speak of it, because in his world arranging a thing quietly was very nearly the same as doing it. His heir was his nephew Bernard — the son of another dead brother — a captain of Engineers, sensible, presentable, and careful never to displease his uncle. The plan was simple. Bernard would marry Bell. The Great House and the Small House would then be one family again under one roof, everything joined, everything tidy, and nothing further would ever need to be said by anybody. The squire thought about this plan on most days. He had not thought it worth mentioning to Bell.

That was how matters stood at the end of June, when Bernard came down from London for his summer visit and brought a friend with him.

The girls were on the lawn between the two houses that evening, playing croquet, when the two men came across from the Great House in the long light. Bernard they knew as well as the garden itself. The other man was a stranger, and strangers were rare enough at Allington to make both sisters stand a little straighter over their mallets.

"My cousins," said Bernard. "Bell — Lily — this is my friend Mr Crosbie. We share London between us, and I have brought him down to show him that there are better places."

Adolphus Crosbie was tall, and he was handsome, and he was dressed the way most men would like to believe they dress. He was a clerk in a government office in London, which sounds a small thing; but there are clerks and clerks. Crosbie sat at the top of his office and was known in half the great drawing-rooms of London. He dined out six nights in seven, and the seventh he kept free in case something better was offered. He had eight hundred pounds a year and the manner of a man with three thousand. Everyone liked him. He found this only natural.

He bowed to the sisters and said that Bernard had promised him quiet, and that he could see the promise would be kept, and that after London a man wanted quiet more than he wanted anything. He said it charmingly. He said most things charmingly. Then he took a mallet, and lost the game to Lily with such good grace that it looked very nearly like a gift.

It was a pleasant evening. Even the squire, watching from his tall windows, thought so, in his silent way.

At supper in the Small House afterwards, with the candles lit and the windows still open to the warm dark, Lily gave her judgment.

"Mr Crosbie is a swell," she said.

"Lily," said her mother, though not very severely.

"Well, but he is. It isn't his fault. Bernard brought him, the way you'd bring a good hat. A swell is a man who shines, mamma, and Mr Crosbie shines. You keep expecting him to notice it, and he never does, and somehow that is the most polished part of the whole performance."

"I thought him very pleasant," said Bell.

"So did I," said Lily. "That's the shine."

But she said it laughing, and she had liked being beaten at croquet by nobody and winning against a swell, and when she went up to bed she was still humming.

Far away in London, at a cheap desk in a government office nothing like Crosbie's, there was a young man who would have walked every mile of the road to Allington to carry Lily's glove. She had known him all her life, and thought of him, when she thought of him at all, as a boy from Guestwick. His story is coming. He can wait a little longer. He was used to it.

And in the Great House the squire sat alone over his wine, one candle lit, and was pleased, somewhere far under the gatepost stiffness, that there had been laughter on his lawn. He would never have said so. That was the whole trouble with Allington, and it is fair that you should know it now, at the start: everything was there — the houses, the garden, the milk, the hay, the fruit against the warm brick wall — everything was always there, except the words.
