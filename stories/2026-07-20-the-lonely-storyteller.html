The Lonely Storyteller

2026-07-20 · A Famous Life · a true story · 3-minute read

Hans Christian Andersen was born in a small Danish town in 1805. His father was a shoemaker who loved to read him stories from *Arabian Nights*. His mother, however, could not read or write and worked washing clothes for others. When his father died, Hans was sent to a school for poor children. He also worked as an apprentice for a weaver and then a tailor to earn his keep.

At 14, Hans moved to Copenhagen, hoping to become an actor. He had a beautiful singing voice and was accepted into the Royal Danish Theatre. But his voice soon changed. Someone there suggested he try writing, and Hans took the idea seriously. The director of the theatre saw something special in Hans and even convinced the king to help pay for his education. However, his school years were very unhappy, and he was often discouraged from writing.

Despite these struggles, Hans wrote his first story at 17. He found early success with a short story and some poems. Soon, he received a travel grant from the king, allowing him to explore Europe. These travels inspired his first novel, which became very popular.

But it was his fairy tales that made him famous. Stories like “The Little Mermaid” and “The Emperor’s New Clothes” captured the hearts of people everywhere. He wrote 156 fairy tales that have been translated into over 125 languages. They are loved by children and teach valuable lessons. He met famous writers like Charles Dickens, but a five-week visit to Dickens' home ended their friendship, leaving Hans confused and sad.

Andersen was attracted to both men and women, and his private journals show a longing for love and companionship. He found joy in some relationships but also experienced much suffering when his feelings were not returned. Even though his stories were known around the world, the poor shoemaker’s son often felt lonely in his own life.
