A World of Touch: How Braille Traveled

2026-07-21 · How Things Came to Be · a true story · 3-minute read

Louis Braille, a Frenchman who lost his sight as a child, created a special way to read and write in 1824. He was only fifteen. He called it braille, and it used patterns of six raised dots. At first, it was like a secret code for soldiers to use at night. They could communicate without needing a light.

But Louis Braille made it better. His system used fewer dots than the old soldier's code, so it was easier to feel with one finger. He also made sure it could represent letters, numbers, and punctuation. He published his system in 1829. Later, he even added musical notes to it.

After Louis Braille died, his amazing system began to spread. People saw how helpful it was for blind and visually impaired people. Instead of just listening to information, they could read and write for themselves. This meant they could learn spelling and punctuation, just like sighted people.

At first, different countries tried to change braille to fit their own languages. For example, some in America tried to match English alphabet order. But soon, people realized it was better to have one main system. Most braille alphabets today follow Louis Braille’s original French letter order for the first 26 letters. This helped to make braille more consistent around the world.

Today, there are braille codes for over 133 languages. In English-speaking countries, there was a big effort to make braille the same everywhere. Now, a system called Unified English Braille (UEB) is used in many countries, like the United States and the United Kingdom. This way, braille users from different places can share and understand texts more easily. Even with new technology like audio readers, braille is still very important. It gives blind people a direct way to read and write, just like print does for those who can see.
