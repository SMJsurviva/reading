Why We Still Cry Wolf

2026-07-15 · Stories of the World · a true story · 3-minute read

Imagine a young shepherd boy standing on a hill. To pass the time, he shouts to the nearby villagers that a wolf is attacking his sheep. When they arrive, they find nothing. He laughs at his trick. He does this again and again. But one day, a real wolf arrives. The boy screams for help, but the villagers ignore him. They have heard his false alarms too many times. The wolf destroys the flock, and in some versions of the story, the boy suffers as well.

This simple story is one of Aesop’s Fables. It has been told for thousands of years, starting back in the Classical era. For a long time, it existed only in Greek. It did not spread across Europe until it was translated into Latin in the 15th century. Because it was not a single book, people gave the story many different names over the centuries. Some called it "The boy who lied," while others called it "The shepherd boy and the wolf." Eventually, the name we know today became the standard.

Why do we still talk about this boy after two thousand years? We use the phrase "to cry wolf" when someone gives a false alarm. Aristotle, an ancient philosopher, once explained the problem perfectly. He noted that if you tell lies, people will not believe you even when you finally tell the truth. This is not just a lesson for children. Today, experts study this behavior as "alert fatigue." This is a fancy way of saying that if people hear too many false warnings about danger, they stop paying attention. They grow tired of listening.

Interestingly, researchers have looked at how this story affects us. One study found that children who read the story were actually more likely to lie afterward, while those who read about George Washington and the cherry tree were more honest. Even so, the story remains a powerful way to talk about trust. Whether it is a shepherd boy in an old book or a person in modern politics, the lesson stays the same. If we warn people about dangers that aren't there too often, they will not be ready when a real danger finally appears.
