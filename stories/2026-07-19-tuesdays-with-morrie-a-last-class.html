Tuesdays with Morrie: A Last Class

2026-07-19 · Something Different · a true story · 3-minute read

In the late 1970s, Mitch Albom was a student at Brandeis University, where he studied sociology with Professor Morrie Schwartz. After graduation, like many students, Mitch lost touch with his old professor.

Life went on for Mitch. He became an award-winning sports columnist. But then, in 1995, something brought them back together. Mitch saw Morrie being interviewed on TV. The professor was talking about his experience living with ALS, a serious illness that affects how the body moves. Mitch was touched and decided to reconnect with his former teacher.

Morrie’s story was already getting attention. A newspaper article called “A Professor’s Final Course: His Own Death” had appeared earlier that year. The TV interviews had also highlighted Morrie’s courageous spirit. Around this same time, a newspaper strike freed up Mitch’s schedule, allowing him to travel every week to Massachusetts to visit Morrie.

These visits became special. Mitch and Morrie met every Tuesday at Morrie’s home. Mitch started to think of these meetings as a “last class” with his professor. They talked about many important topics: death, fear, growing old, greed, marriage, family, forgiveness, and the very meaning of life itself. Mitch also thought about his own busy life and remembered his college days.

Sadly, Morrie Schwartz passed away on November 4, 1995. Mitch decided to write a book about their Tuesday meetings. He hoped the book would help pay for Morrie’s medical bills, as Morrie had wanted to stay home during his illness rather than go to a hospital. This true story, first published in 1997, became *Tuesdays with Morrie*. It sold over 17 million copies worldwide and has been translated into more than 45 languages, making it one of the most loved books in the world.
