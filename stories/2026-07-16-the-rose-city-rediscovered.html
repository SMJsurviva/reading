The Rose City Rediscovered

2026-07-16 · Places · a true story · 3-minute read

Imagine a city born from rock, its buildings carved directly into towering, rose-red cliffs. This is Petra, an ancient city in what is now Jordan. Its early inhabitants, the Nabataeans, were skilled at living in the desert. They collected rainwater using dams and pipes, turning the dry landscape into a thriving oasis.

Petra became famous for its incredible rock-cut buildings and clever water systems. People even called it the “Rose City” because of the beautiful color of the sandstone from which it was carved. In the 1st century AD, Petra was at its busiest, with around 20,000 people living there. One of its most famous structures, Al-Khazneh, was built around this time. It might have been a tomb for a king.

But Petra's importance slowly faded. New ways of trading by sea meant fewer merchants came through the desert. Then, in 363 AD, a big earthquake shook the city, damaging many buildings and its crucial water system. Over time, Petra became quieter. In the early Islamic era, most people left, and only a few nomads remained.

For centuries, this amazing city was lost to the outside world. It became a hidden gem, known only to local people. But in 1812, a Swiss traveler named Johann Ludwig Burckhardt heard whispers of a “dead city” that held a prophet's tomb. He was very curious. Dressing like a local and speaking Arabic, he set out to find it. After just one day of exploring, he realized he had found the lost city of Petra.

Burckhardt's discovery brought Petra back into the world's eye. Today, it’s a symbol of Jordan and its most popular tourist spot, with nearly a million visitors each year, all coming to marvel at the city carved from rock.
