Better Than a Dog, Anyhow

A Famous Life · a true story · 3-minute read

In the summer of 1838, Charles Darwin had a problem. He was twenty-nine years old. He had just spent five years sailing around the world as a ship's naturalist, and his head was full of new scientific ideas. But one question was troubling him, and science could not answer it. Should he get married?

Darwin was a careful man. He wrote notes about everything he studied. So he did what he always did: he took two scraps of paper and made a list. At the top of one column he wrote "Marry". At the top of the other he wrote "Not Marry".

Under "Not Marry" he wrote his fears. Marriage would mean "less money for books", he wrote. And a "terrible loss of time".

Under "Marry" he wrote the good things. A wife would be a "constant companion and a friend in old age". Then he added five words that still make people smile today: "better than a dog anyhow".

The list said: marry. So Darwin talked to his father, and then went to visit his cousin, Emma Wedgwood. In November he asked her to marry him, and she said yes. They married in January 1839.

Emma knew that her new husband's scientific ideas might one day shake the world, and this worried her, because her own faith was deep. Soon after he proposed, she wrote him a letter. She thanked him for always being honest with her, and asked him never to hide his true thoughts from her, even if they were painful. They did not always believe the same things, but they promised to stay open with each other — and they kept that promise for more than forty years, through ten children and one of the most famous scientific lives in history.

The funny little list still exists. You can see it today, with "better than a dog anyhow" written in Darwin's quick, untidy hand.
