The Butterfly's Great Journey Home

2026-07-18 · The Natural World · a true story · 3-minute read

Imagine a tiny creature, born in Mexico or Florida, embarking on a journey that will take many months and many lives to complete. This is the story of the monarch butterfly, a famous traveler of North America.

Our story begins in late summer or autumn. Thousands of monarch butterflies in the northern and central United States and southern Canada feel a pull to fly south. They fly to warmer places like Florida and Mexico. These butterflies are special. They are the ones that will make the long trip. Their wings are black, orange, and white, making them easy to spot. They fly thousands of miles, much farther than most butterflies ever go.

But here's the amazing part: the butterflies that fly south in the fall are not the same ones that fly north in the spring. The journey home is a multigenerational effort. The monarch that left Canada in the autumn will not live long enough to see its old home again. Instead, it will lay eggs in the southern lands.

These eggs hatch into tiny caterpillars. The caterpillars eat milkweed plants and grow quickly, shedding their skin five times. Each time they shed, they get bigger and more colorful, with yellow, white, and black stripes. After about three to five days in each stage, they turn into a beautiful green pupa, also called a chrysalis. They hang upside down, then their skin splits, and the chrysalis appears. Inside this chrysalis, the adult butterfly forms over about 11 or 12 days.

When the new adult butterfly emerges, it pumps fluid into its wings to make them stiff and ready for flight. This new generation of monarchs will fly north, continuing the journey their great-grandparents began. They will lay their own eggs along the way. It takes four generations of butterflies to complete the entire round trip. Each new generation takes up the journey, flying closer to the original starting place until, finally, the great-great-grandchildren of the original travelers arrive back home in Canada or the northern U.S. It's a true family relay race across a continent.
