The Woman Who Told Stories to Stay Alive

Stories of the World · a true story · 3-minute read

This is one of the oldest and most famous story-frames in the world. It comes from the collection known in English as One Thousand and One Nights.

There was once a king who had been betrayed by his wife. His heart turned hard and cold. He decided that he would marry a new bride every day — and order her death the next morning, before she could ever betray him. This went on until his vizier, the royal minister whose sad duty it was to find the brides, could not bear it any longer.

The vizier had a daughter named Scheherazade. She was clever and well-read, and she had studied the books of history, poetry and philosophy. Against her father's desperate objections, she volunteered to marry the king.

But Scheherazade had a plan. On her first night, she began to tell the king a story — a wonderful story, full of adventure and mystery. And then, just as the story reached its most exciting moment, she stopped. It was dawn. The king wanted to know the ending. So he let her live one more day.

The next night she finished the story — and began a new one, always breaking off at a moment of suspense at daybreak. Night after night, story after story, the king postponed her death, because he simply had to know what happened next.

She kept this up for one thousand and one nights. By then, the king had changed. His anger had softened, he had come to love her, and he spared her life for good. She became his queen.

People have retold this frame for a thousand years, and it carries a quiet message that storytellers everywhere still believe: a good story does not just pass the time. Sometimes it can soften a hard heart — and sometimes it can save a life.
