The City That Stands on a Forest

Places · a true story · 3-minute read

Venice, in the north of Italy, may be the strangest great city ever built. It stands on 118 small islands in a shallow lagoon. Its streets are canals of sea water. Its buses and taxis are boats. And underneath it all, holding the whole city up, is a buried forest.

Venice began in fear. Around one thousand five hundred years ago, waves of invading armies swept into Italy, burning the rich Roman cities of the mainland. The people of those cities ran for their lives — and some of them fled out into the lagoon, onto low, muddy islands where nobody had ever wanted to live. It was a miserable place to build a home. But that was exactly the point. The shallow water and soft mud stopped horses and armies. The invaders could not follow.

The refugees stayed, and they solved an impossible problem: how do you build on mud? Their answer was wood. They cut millions of tree trunks — mostly alder, a wood that resists water — and drove them down through the soft mud like giant nails, until the piles reached a layer of hard clay deep below. On top of these wooden piles they laid their foundations, and on those foundations they raised a city.

Here is the surprising part: the wood has lasted. Buried in mud, with no air, the piles do not rot the way wood normally does. Palaces built five hundred or a thousand years ago are still standing today on their original tree trunks.

In time, the city of frightened refugees grew rich and powerful, and for centuries Venice was one of the great trading cities of the world. Its beauty is famous — the golden church of St Mark, the palaces along the Grand Canal, the quiet back streets of water.

All of it began with people who had lost everything, standing on the mud, refusing to give up.
