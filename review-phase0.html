Better Than a Dog, Anyhow

Monday — A Famous Life · ~318 words · FK 5.0 · source: Charles_Darwin

In the summer of 1838, Charles Darwin had a problem. He was twenty-nine years old. He had just spent five years sailing around the world as a ship's naturalist, and his head was full of new scientific ideas. But one question was troubling him, and science could not answer it. Should he get married?

Darwin was a careful man. He wrote notes about everything he studied. So he did what he always did: he took two scraps of paper and made a list. At the top of one column he wrote "Marry". At the top of the other he wrote "Not Marry".

Under "Not Marry" he wrote his fears. Marriage would mean "less money for books", he wrote. And a "terrible loss of time".

Under "Marry" he wrote the good things. A wife would be a "constant companion and a friend in old age". Then he added five words that still make people smile today: "better than a dog anyhow".

The list said: marry. So Darwin talked to his father, and then went to visit his cousin, Emma Wedgwood. In November he asked her to marry him, and she said yes. They married in January 1839.

Emma knew that her new husband's scientific ideas might one day shake the world, and this worried her, because her own faith was deep. Soon after he proposed, she wrote him a letter. She thanked him for always being honest with her, and asked him never to hide his true thoughts from her, even if they were painful. They did not always believe the same things, but they promised to stay open with each other — and they kept that promise for more than forty years, through ten children and one of the most famous scientific lives in history.

The funny little list still exists. You can see it today, with "better than a dog anyhow" written in Darwin's quick, untidy hand.

Question: What did Darwin write under "Marry" that still makes people smile today?
Answer (shown next day): That a wife would be a "constant companion and a friend in old age... better than a dog anyhow."

The Boy Who Wrote with Dots

Tuesday — How Things Came to Be · ~326 words · FK 6.4 · source: Louis_Braille

Louis Braille was born in 1809 in a small village near Paris. His father made leather harnesses for horses, and little Louis loved to play in the workshop. One day, when he was three years old, he was trying to make holes in a piece of leather with an awl — a sharp pointed tool. The awl slipped, and it stabbed him in the eye. The wound became infected, the infection spread to his other eye, and by the age of five he was completely blind.

In those days, most blind children received no real education. But Louis was clever and determined, and at ten he won a place at a school for blind children in Paris — one of the first schools of its kind in the world.

The school had a few special books with large raised letters that students could feel with their fingers. But the books were heavy and expensive, and reading them was very slow. Louis wanted something better.

The answer came from a soldier. A French army captain named Charles Barbier had invented a code of raised dots, so that soldiers could read messages in the dark, without lighting a lamp. Barbier shared his system with the school. The students found it interesting but far too complicated.

Louis took Barbier's idea and made it simple. He designed a small cell of just six dots — small enough to sit under one fingertip. Different patterns of dots stood for different letters. He worked on his alphabet for three years, and finished it when he was fifteen years old.

There is one more detail to this story. To write braille, you press dots into paper with a pointed stylus — a tool very much like the awl that took Louis's sight. The same kind of tool that closed the world to a small boy became the tool that opened it again, for millions of blind people, in almost every language on Earth.

Question: How old was Louis Braille when he finished his alphabet?
Answer (shown next day): Fifteen years old.

The Woman Who Told Stories to Stay Alive

Wednesday — Stories of the World · ~309 words · FK 6.0 · source: Scheherazade

This is one of the oldest and most famous story-frames in the world. It comes from the collection known in English as One Thousand and One Nights.

There was once a king who had been betrayed by his wife. His heart turned hard and cold. He decided that he would marry a new bride every day — and order her death the next morning, before she could ever betray him. This went on until his vizier, the royal minister whose sad duty it was to find the brides, could not bear it any longer.

The vizier had a daughter named Scheherazade. She was clever and well-read, and she had studied the books of history, poetry and philosophy. Against her father's desperate objections, she volunteered to marry the king.

But Scheherazade had a plan. On her first night, she began to tell the king a story — a wonderful story, full of adventure and mystery. And then, just as the story reached its most exciting moment, she stopped. It was dawn. The king wanted to know the ending. So he let her live one more day.

The next night she finished the story — and began a new one, always breaking off at a moment of suspense at daybreak. Night after night, story after story, the king postponed her death, because he simply had to know what happened next.

She kept this up for one thousand and one nights. By then, the king had changed. His anger had softened, he had come to love her, and he spared her life for good. She became his queen.

People have retold this frame for a thousand years, and it carries a quiet message that storytellers everywhere still believe: a good story does not just pass the time. Sometimes it can soften a hard heart — and sometimes it can save a life.

Question: Why did Scheherazade always stop her story at dawn?
Answer (shown next day): So the king would have to keep her alive one more day to hear the ending.

The City That Stands on a Forest

Thursday — Places · ~319 words · FK 6.7 · source: Venice

Venice, in the north of Italy, may be the strangest great city ever built. It stands on 118 small islands in a shallow lagoon. Its streets are canals of sea water. Its buses and taxis are boats. And underneath it all, holding the whole city up, is a buried forest.

Venice began in fear. Around one thousand five hundred years ago, waves of invading armies swept into Italy, burning the rich Roman cities of the mainland. The people of those cities ran for their lives — and some of them fled out into the lagoon, onto low, muddy islands where nobody had ever wanted to live. It was a miserable place to build a home. But that was exactly the point. The shallow water and soft mud stopped horses and armies. The invaders could not follow.

The refugees stayed, and they solved an impossible problem: how do you build on mud? Their answer was wood. They cut millions of tree trunks — mostly alder, a wood that resists water — and drove them down through the soft mud like giant nails, until the piles reached a layer of hard clay deep below. On top of these wooden piles they laid their foundations, and on those foundations they raised a city.

Here is the surprising part: the wood has lasted. Buried in mud, with no air, the piles do not rot the way wood normally does. Palaces built five hundred or a thousand years ago are still standing today on their original tree trunks.

In time, the city of frightened refugees grew rich and powerful, and for centuries Venice was one of the great trading cities of the world. Its beauty is famous — the golden church of St Mark, the palaces along the Grand Canal, the quiet back streets of water.

All of it began with people who had lost everything, standing on the mud, refusing to give up.

Question: Why did people first move onto the muddy islands of the lagoon?
Answer (shown next day): They were refugees fleeing waves of invasions — the water kept the invading armies out.

Two Sisters and One Promise

Friday — Family Stories · ~330 words · FK 6.0 · source: Marie_Curie

Before she was Marie Curie, she was Maria Skłodowska, a young woman in Warsaw with a problem that talent could not solve.

Maria and her older sister Bronia were both brilliant. Both dreamed of university. But Poland at that time was under Russian rule, and women could not go to university at all. In Paris, the universities were open to women. But the sisters came from a family of teachers, and there was no money to send them there.

So the two sisters made an agreement. Maria would stay in Poland and work, and send her wages to Paris to pay for Bronia's medical studies. Then, when Bronia was a doctor, she would help Maria in return.

Maria kept her side of the promise for six long years. She worked as a governess, teaching the children of wealthy families, and sent the money to her sister. It was lonely work, and her own dream had to wait. But she never stopped studying — at night, from books, alone. For a while she also joined Warsaw's secret "Flying University". This was an illegal night school. It moved from house to house to stay ahead of the Russian police — and it welcomed women.

Bronia became a doctor. And Bronia kept her side of the promise too. In 1891, Maria — now twenty-four — finally boarded a train to Paris. She lived with her sister, and she studied physics and mathematics at the Sorbonne.

You probably know the rest of the story. Maria became Marie Curie. She was the first woman ever to win a Nobel Prize, and the first person to win two. She is still the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences. Her daughter Irène would later win one as well.

But every part of that famous story rests on something small that happened first. Two sisters in Warsaw, with no money and no rights, decided to lift each other up — one at a time.

Question: What was the agreement between Maria and her sister Bronia?
Answer (shown next day): Maria would work and send money for Bronia's medical studies in Paris; two years later, Bronia would help Maria in return.
