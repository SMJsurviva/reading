The Small House at Allington

Anthony Trollope (1864) — retold in plain English, in its own Victorian England.
Chapters one to 4 — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. This is Anthony Trollope’s The Small House at Allington, retold in simple English but kept in its own world: the English countryside about 160 years ago. It is the story of two sisters in a small house — and above all of Lily Dale, who gives her whole heart and will not learn how to give only part of it.

Chapter One — The Two Houses

There were two houses at Allington, and this whole story happens between them.

The Great House was old and square and handsome, with tall windows and a garden that people drove miles to look at. Dales had lived in it for four hundred years. They were not a famous family. No Dale had ever done anything remarkable, and no Dale had ever done anything shameful, and in the English countryside that counts as a kind of greatness.

The squire of Allington was Christopher Dale, and he was exactly what the house had made him. He was past sixty. He had never married. As a young man he had loved one woman with his whole careful heart, and she had married a richer one, and he had closed that door quietly and never stood near it again. He was honest. He was fair. He was stiff as a gatepost. He loved his family the way he paid his taxes: fully, on time, and without one word more than was necessary.

The Small House stood a garden's length away, inside the same grounds. It had been built long ago for the mothers of squires. When a son took the Great House, his widowed mother crossed the garden, and that was the way of it for generations. It was not really small, either — it only felt small when the Great House was looking at it.

In the Small House lived Mrs Dale and her two daughters.

Mrs Dale had married the squire's youngest brother, Philip, who was the warm one of the family, and who died young, as the warm ones sometimes do. He left her very little money and two small girls. The squire did then what the squire always did. He did the right thing, coldly. He offered her the Small House, rent-free, for as long as she should want it. He sent vegetables from his garden and fruit from his wall and milk from his dairy, and hay for the girls' pony. He gave everything, in fact, except the one thing that would have made the rest easy to take. In eighteen years he had never once said: I am glad you are here.

So Mrs Dale had accepted the house for her daughters' sake, and she paid the rent no one asked of her. She paid it in pride. She did not dine at the Great House more than duty required. She asked for nothing, ever. The two houses were on perfectly good terms, the way two countries are on perfectly good terms when there is a treaty and no love.

The daughters minded all this much less than their mother did, because the garden between the houses belonged to their childhood, and you cannot be proud and stiff about the place where you learned to walk.

Bell was the elder, and she was steady. She was tall and fair and unhurried, and she had the kind of calm that is not dullness, though hasty people sometimes filed it there. When Bell was sure of a thing, she was sure of it all the way down, and no one could shift her — but it took her a long time to be sure, and she never pretended before she was.

Lily was nineteen, and Lily is the one this book is about. She was quicker than her sister, and browner, and not as tall. She teased everyone she loved, which was her way of saying so. She made a room feel better lit. If there was a weakness in her, it was this: when Lily gave anything, she gave the whole of it, and she did not know how to take part of it back. Some people learn that trick early. Lily was never going to learn it at all.

The gardens of both houses were kept by one man, a gardener called Hopkins, who had been at Allington longer than anyone could clearly remember. Strictly speaking, Hopkins served the squire. In his own understanding, the gardens were his, both of them, and the two families were people he allowed to walk there. He was rude to everyone equally, out of fairness, and he would have died for any of them, though he would have grumbled about it first.

The squire had a plan. He did not speak of it, because in his world arranging a thing quietly was very nearly the same as doing it. His heir was his nephew Bernard — the son of another dead brother — a captain of Engineers, sensible, presentable, and careful never to displease his uncle. The plan was simple. Bernard would marry Bell. The Great House and the Small House would then be one family again under one roof, everything joined, everything tidy, and nothing further would ever need to be said by anybody. The squire thought about this plan on most days. He had not thought it worth mentioning to Bell.

That was how matters stood at the end of June, when Bernard came down from London for his summer visit and brought a friend with him.

The girls were on the lawn between the two houses that evening, playing croquet, when the two men came across from the Great House in the long light. Bernard they knew as well as the garden itself. The other man was a stranger, and strangers were rare enough at Allington to make both sisters stand a little straighter over their mallets.

"My cousins," said Bernard. "Bell — Lily — this is my friend Mr Crosbie. We share London between us, and I have brought him down to show him that there are better places."

Adolphus Crosbie was tall, and he was handsome, and he was dressed the way most men would like to believe they dress. He was a clerk in a government office in London, which sounds a small thing; but there are clerks and clerks. Crosbie sat at the top of his office and was known in half the great drawing-rooms of London. He dined out six nights in seven, and the seventh he kept free in case something better was offered. He had eight hundred pounds a year and the manner of a man with three thousand. Everyone liked him. He found this only natural.

He bowed to the sisters and said that Bernard had promised him quiet, and that he could see the promise would be kept, and that after London a man wanted quiet more than he wanted anything. He said it charmingly. He said most things charmingly. Then he took a mallet, and lost the game to Lily with such good grace that it looked very nearly like a gift.

It was a pleasant evening. Even the squire, watching from his tall windows, thought so, in his silent way.

At supper in the Small House afterwards, with the candles lit and the windows still open to the warm dark, Lily gave her judgment.

"Mr Crosbie is a swell," she said.

"Lily," said her mother, though not very severely.

"Well, but he is. It isn't his fault. Bernard brought him, the way you'd bring a good hat. A swell is a man who shines, mamma, and Mr Crosbie shines. You keep expecting him to notice it, and he never does, and somehow that is the most polished part of the whole performance."

"I thought him very pleasant," said Bell.

"So did I," said Lily. "That's the shine."

But she said it laughing, and she had liked being beaten at croquet by nobody and winning against a swell, and when she went up to bed she was still humming.

Far away in London, at a cheap desk in a government office nothing like Crosbie's, there was a young man who would have walked every mile of the road to Allington to carry Lily's glove. She had known him all her life, and thought of him, when she thought of him at all, as a boy from Guestwick. His story is coming. He can wait a little longer. He was used to it.

And in the Great House the squire sat alone over his wine, one candle lit, and was pleased, somewhere far under the gatepost stiffness, that there had been laughter on his lawn. He would never have said so. That was the whole trouble with Allington, and it is fair that you should know it now, at the start: everything was there — the houses, the garden, the milk, the hay, the fruit against the warm brick wall — everything was always there, except the words.

Chapter Two — Apollo at Allington

Mr Crosbie had come to Allington for three days. He stayed a fortnight.

Nobody asked him to explain this, because in the country a pleasant guest is a kind of good weather, and you do not ask good weather why it is staying. There was croquet on the lawn every evening. There were walks to Guestwick and back that took twice as long as the road required. There was reading aloud in the Small House garden, where Crosbie read better than the book deserved and knew it. He shone all day, steadily, the way a well-kept lamp shines, and the two houses turned towards him without quite noticing that they had turned.

It was in the second week that Lily gave him his name.

They had been playing croquet, and Crosbie had made a shot that nobody could have made on purpose, and had then bowed as if he made it every day. Lily leaned on her mallet and looked at him.

"Bernard," she said, "you told us he was a clerk. You never said you were bringing down Apollo."

Everyone laughed, and Crosbie laughed too, and protested, and was in fact very pleased. That is the sort of joke that only works when a small part of it is meant, and everyone on the lawn knew a small part of it was meant. After that he was Apollo. Lily said it teasing, the way she said everything to people she liked; but a name is a door, and she had opened it herself.

Bell watched all this in her quiet way and said nothing. Being sure of things slowly was her gift, and she was not yet sure of Mr Crosbie. He was pleasant. He was very pleasant. She only wondered, in the bottom of her steady mind, what all the pleasantness was for.

Now for the boy from Guestwick, because his story starts long before Crosbie's and will outlast it.

His name was John Eames, and everybody called him Johnny, which tells you something straight away. His father had been a gentleman who farmed his own land and did it badly, in a warm-hearted, hopeful way, and died leaving very little behind but the good opinion of his neighbours. One of those neighbours was an odd old bachelor lord, Earl De Guest, who had valued Johnny's father more than either man ever said. Remember the earl. He farms in earnest, he is coming later, and there will be a bull.

Johnny's widowed mother lived in a small house in Guestwick on a small income, and when Johnny turned nineteen a word was said in the right place and he was made a clerk in the Income-tax Office in London. It was a real place on the ladder, and it paid him eighty pounds a year, which is to say: not enough. He copied letters at a cheap desk and was shouted at by a fat senior clerk, and once a year he came home on leave, browner about the wrists than when he left.

Johnny was at the age which has no good name, so I shall use the old one: he was a hobbledehoy. A hobbledehoy is a young man who is finished growing but not finished becoming — too old to be a boy, not yet fitted to himself as a man. His arms were longer than his sleeves. He said the wrong thing, heard himself say it, and then said another one. The Crosbies of the world are done early, like houses run up quickly for show. The Johnny Eameses are built slowly, and stand.

And Johnny loved Lily Dale. He had loved her since he was twelve and she was ten, entirely, hopelessly, and in perfect silence. She had known him all her life and thought of him, when she thought of him at all, as the boy from Guestwick. He had never told her. He had told the road between Guestwick and Allington, many times, walking it alone; the road kept his secret.

London, meanwhile, had done to Johnny the thing London does to lonely young men who are nobody in particular: it had got him into a scrape.

He lodged at a boarding house in Burton Crescent kept by a Mrs Roper, a decent widow always two bills behind her hopes. Her boarders were a mixed lot. There was Cradell, a clerk from Johnny's own office, silly in a friendly way. There was a Mr Lupex and his wife, who were vaguely theatrical and permanently short of money, and who filled the house with a feeling of borrowed things about to be sent back. And there was Mrs Roper's daughter, Amelia, who was thirty, handsome in a firm sort of way, and a great believer in things being settled.

One wet Sunday evening, being homesick and flattered — and flattery finds a hungry boy quickly — Johnny had said soft things to Amelia in the back parlour. Worse, being a clerk, he had written some of them down. He meant perhaps a tenth of it, for about an hour. Amelia had filed the whole of it, the way her mother filed receipts. Her letters to him now ended, every one, with the same sentence, underlined: Is your heart true to me? And Johnny, who could not afford his own umbrella, owned a millstone.

He did not love Amelia. He loved Lily Dale. He was ashamed of the one fact and hopeless about the other, and he carried both up to Guestwick that June, when his leave came round, along with a resolution.

The resolution was this: he would go over to Allington, and he would find one hour alone with Lily, and he would say the thing he had been not-saying for nine years. He had said it aloud to his shaving glass, and it had gone quite well.

He walked over on a golden evening, and heard the lawn before he saw it: the click of the balls, and laughter, one laugh above the others, which he had known all his life.

Lily was playing croquet against a tall stranger. She was accusing him of cheating, and the stranger was replying that Apollo does not cheat, he inspires the ball — and Lily was laughing, laughing entirely, the way she did everything.

"Johnny!" she called out, the moment she saw him, and came across the grass with both hands out, and was so plainly, warmly glad to see him that it hurt more than coldness would have. She brought him in among them all. "Mr Crosbie — my friend Mr Eames. Johnny, we have known each other since I was ten and he pushed me in the stream."

"I have regretted it since," said Johnny.

"You have not," said Lily.

"No," said Johnny. "I have not."

That was, in fact, the best thing he said all evening, and for one moment Lily's eyes rested on him as if she had heard something new in it. Then Crosbie said something graceful, and the moment went the way of such moments.

They gave Johnny a mallet. He played badly. His arms had never once been the right length in Lily's presence, and tonight they were worse. Crosbie was kind to him — which was the hardest part — kind the way a man is kind when he can afford it, passing him easy shots, praising the bad ones. Johnny took it and hated him quietly and was ashamed of that too, because a fair-minded boy cannot even hate in comfort.

He walked home in the last light. The resolution walked with him a little way, and then it lay down by the road, and he did not stop for it.

His leave had three more days to run. He spent them at his mother's, saying he was tired from London. Then he went back to the cheap desk, and Cradell's chatter, and Amelia's underlined question, and he wrote to nobody about any of it.

He had settled one thing, though, walking that road in the dark, and it was this: nothing was settled. Whatever the tall shining man meant to Lily Dale, Johnny Eames was not finished. He was a hobbledehoy; finished was the one thing hobbledehoys are not.

London took him back, and shut the door, and the summer went on shining at Allington without him — which is the way of summers, and one of the reasons this book was written.

Chapter Three — The Question by the Bridge

At the bottom of the garden, where the two lawns ran out into rougher grass, a little wooden bridge crossed the stream to the churchyard fields. It was nothing much to look at. It had a rail on one side only, and Hopkins repainted it white every third year, grumbling. Children had fished off it for four hundred years. It was the kind of place a house keeps without knowing why, until the day it finds out.

Crosbie's fortnight was over, and then somehow another week of it was over too, and at last there came a morning when his portmanteau was actually packed and the gig was ordered from Guestwick for the afternoon. London wanted him back. The office wanted him; three dinner tables wanted him; and he had said, more than once, laughing, that nobody at Allington would miss so idle a fellow.

He said it once too often at breakfast, and Lily did not laugh.

That morning the two of them walked down through the garden. Nobody arranged it — or everybody did; a house full of women can arrange a thing of that kind without one word passing, and Mrs Dale found herself urgently needed among the roses, and Bell discovered a letter that had to be written at once. So the garden emptied itself around Lily and Crosbie the way water empties around a stone, and they came, not quite by accident, to the bridge.

Lily stood at the middle of it, with her hand on the one rail, looking down at the water. Crosbie stood beside her and looked at her, and found that all his London manner had stayed up at the house.

It ought to be said plainly, because this book will have hard things to say of him later: at that moment there was nothing false in Adolphus Crosbie. The fortnight had surprised him. He had come down to be charming among rustics, and instead something in him had sat down in the sunshine of this girl and did not want to get up again. Men like Crosbie polish every feeling before showing it. What he felt on the bridge had no polish on it anywhere, and it frightened him a little, and he liked being frightened by it, the way a man likes deep water while the weather holds.

"Lily," he said. Not Miss Dale. She watched the stream and let the name stand.

"I am to go this afternoon," he said, "and I find I cannot do it — not in the usual way, with thanks and compliments, and see you next summer perhaps. I have grown too rich here to go back to being poor. Lily, will you be my wife?"

She looked up from the water then and straight at him, and she did not blush, or laugh, or make him wait — waiting is a coin for people who love a little. Lily paid in whole sums or not at all.

"Yes," she said. "You have my whole heart, Adolphus. I think you have had it for days. I only wanted you to ask for it."

And then, because she was still Lily: "You have been asking for it, you know. All fortnight. A swell asks with his shoulders."

He laughed, and took her hands, and the stream went on under the bridge, and the gig was sent back to Guestwick empty.

The news went through the two houses like weather.

Mrs Dale heard it in the rose garden, from the two of them together, and kissed her daughter, and gave Crosbie both her hands and her blessing. Her heart did one small thing sideways even as she did it — a mother's heart at such news is a divided kingdom, and joy governs it, but joy does not govern all of it. He was charming; he was a stranger; her Lily's whole self had just left home. She put the thought away like linen for winter and let the day be golden, and it was golden.

Bell embraced her sister and held her a moment longer than the embrace required.

"You are sure," she said. It was not quite a question, and Lily answered the part that was.

"I am sure all the way down," she said, which was Bell's own coin, borrowed for the occasion. "You will love him, Bell, when you know him. Nobody can know him and not."

"Then I will know him," said Bell, which was a promise with the smallest gate in it, though neither sister looked at the gate.

Hopkins was told by nobody and knew before dinner, in the way of gardeners. He said the young lady might do as she pleased, of course, and that the London gentleman had walked across his new-sown grass twice, and that he, Hopkins, said nothing; and he went off to the frames and said nothing so loudly it could be heard in both houses.

The squire was told by Bernard, up at the Great House. Christopher Dale stood by the tall window with his hands behind his back for a while.

"He has an income?" he said at last.

"Eight hundred, uncle. And his position is very good; he sits at the head of his office. Everyone in town knows Crosbie."

"Everyone in town," said the squire, as a man repeats a phrase in a foreign language. But he was pleased. Bernard could tell, over years of studying that face the way sailors study a grey sea, that his uncle was pleased. A Dale girl engaged, respectably, from his own lawn; the family enlarging itself; things going forward in an orderly manner. He sent down to the Small House, that evening, two bottles of a wine he never drank himself and the first of the wall peaches, and he shook Crosbie's hand at dinner and told him the office he served was a very proper office. From the squire this was very nearly an embrace. Nobody explained that to Crosbie, so it was wasted on him — the first thing Allington gave him that he did not know how to spend.

At the table there was candle-light and much laughing, and Lily called him Apollo before her mother, before everyone, no longer quite teasing. The name had changed in her mouth. It was daily use now, the private made public, a flag over taken ground. Crosbie heard it and shone; he was made for exactly such evenings; and if you had stood in the warm dark outside those windows you would have said — anyone would have said — there is the luckiest man in England, and he knows it.

He did know it. Hold that in his favour. Whatever page you are on when you judge him, remember that on this page he knew it.

He stayed two days more, and they were the best two days this book contains, so I will not hurry past them. Lily and her Apollo walked the churchyard fields and settled the future in the grand foolish way of the newly engaged — where they would live, and how, and which of the two of them loved the other first, a dispute Lily lost on purpose. She was not a girl in a story to herself. She was a girl to whom the one impossible thing had happened: she had given everything, and it had been wanted.

On the third morning the gig came again, and this time it could not be sent back. She walked him to the gate. He held her hand against his coat and said that September was not so very far, and that he would write every day the post ran — and he meant it; let that be written down too.

The gig took him up the Guestwick road and out of sight. Lily stood at the gate until the dust settled, and was not sad, or only in the way that perfect happiness is a little sad at the edges, because it has begun to have something to lose. Then she walked back down the whole green length of the garden — past her mother's roses, past Hopkins pretending not to see her, over the little bridge with its one white rail — and stood a moment where she had said yes.

"It cannot be altered now," she said to the stream, laughing at her own solemn face in the water.

She meant it for a vow, and it was a good one, and she would keep it. But words said over water carry, and this book has a long memory, and so, reader — you and I will hear that sentence again.

Chapter Four — Money

London received Crosbie back the way it receives all its favourites. It asked where he had been, did not listen to the answer, and gave him three invitations for the following week.

For a few days he was as happy as he had promised himself to be, and happier. He told the news at his club, and told it well. Men shook his hand. One or two of them, older men with married faces, shook it a moment too long. "Eight hundred a year, is it?" they said, in a tone he chose not to hear. He wrote to Lily every day the post ran, as he had sworn, and the letters were real letters — let that also be written down.

But a man cannot sit at the head of an office without knowing how to add. In the second week, alone in his rooms one evening, Crosbie took out — not paper; he was not yet so far gone as paper — the mental slate. Every gentleman owns one. It is where he does the sums he would rather not sign.

Eight hundred pounds a year. It sounds well, and for one polished bachelor it was well. Rooms, a good tailor, the club, cabs, the small constant silver rain of tips and gloves and theatre stalls. Eight hundred a year had made him the Crosbie that drawing-rooms knew. Now let there be a wife, said the slate. Let there be a house — a small one, in a street just barely worth naming. Two servants. Coals. A table fit to ask a friend to. Let there be, in time — and here the slate went respectfully blank, but did not go away — a family.

The sum came out the same every way he did it. Mr and Mrs Crosbie could live on eight hundred a year. Apollo could not.

He put the slate away and was ashamed. He told himself the fortnight-truth again: Lily was worth all the drawing-rooms in London. This was correct, and he knew it was correct, and he warmed himself at it. Only — and here is the exact place where the crack begins, so look closely — he now finished the thought differently than on the bridge. On the bridge, Lily was worth everything, full stop. In London the sentence had grown a second clause. Lily was worth everything — and besides, the uncle will surely do something.

Everybody in his set assumed it. A rich old bachelor squire. A favourite niece, married off his own lawn. Of course something would be done — something in the hundreds, settled properly. Crosbie had never quite asked himself whether the squire saw it so. At Allington he had been in love and above arithmetic. From London, Allington looked subtly different. It looked like a property, with people on it, one of whom owed him — no, not owed, he corrected himself. Would naturally wish.

So he wrote to Bernard. Bernard was his friend. Bernard was the heir. Bernard could sound his uncle, delicately, man to man, no harm done. It was, Crosbie told himself, only prudent; any man of sense would do the same. He posted the letter on his way to dinner, and was very agreeable that evening, and a little loud.

At Allington, Bernard read the letter twice, disliked his errand, and did it anyway. That was Bernard, from crown to boots.

He chose a Sunday, after church, walking back with his uncle across the fields. He produced it all very reasonably. Crosbie's excellent position; the realities of London; the natural question of a settlement. Not that Crosbie asked, of course — Crosbie asked nothing — but as a matter of business, between families —

The squire walked on for ten steps saying nothing, and the ten steps were long ones.

"He gets my niece," he said at last. "He was content with that in July. What has August taught him?"

"Uncle, it is not —"

"I know what it is." The squire stopped and faced him. His voice stayed level, which in Christopher Dale was the bad sign. Heat he could forgive in a man; he had none of his own. "I am asked a question through you, so that no one can be said to have asked it. Very well. Here is my answer, and you may deliver it whole. What I mean to do for Lily — if I mean to do anything — I will do in my own time, of my own motion, unasked. I will not be angled at, Bernard. The man who angles at me catches nothing. Tell your friend the girl says she loves him. He is richer than he was, if he knows his luck."

He walked on. Dinner at the Great House that day was silent even by its own standards.

Bernard softened the answer through three re-writings before it went back to London. No softening could change its figure. Nothing — or nothing promised, which to the mental slate is the same sum. Crosbie read the letter in his rooms and sat still with it a long while. What he felt, he was honest enough to name: a chill. A small cold place, no bigger than a shilling, somewhere under the shirt-front. He was engaged to a girl with no fortune, whose uncle would not be angled at. And September, which on the bridge had been a lovers' word, now stood in his diary as a date by which nothing would have improved.

He did not waver. Understand that: in August, Crosbie had not wavered. He burned the sums. He wrote Lily a letter fonder than usual that night — guilt writes fondly; it is one of guilt's few accomplishments. He stood, as he saw it, exactly where honour had placed him.

Only the room had grown draughty. And a few days later a large envelope arrived, sealed with the arms of the De Courcy family. The countess requested the pleasure of Mr Crosbie's company at Courcy Castle, for a fortnight in September. A fortnight among earls, on his way back from a betrothal to a country girl with no settlement. A wiser man — no. Wisdom is not the word; Crosbie was wise. A steadier man would have declined it. Crosbie turned the invitation twice in his hands. A man must keep up his connections, he told himself, now more than ever — for Lily's sake. For Lily's sake; the slate approved that clause. He accepted.

Put a mark by that letter. It cost him a shilling stamp, and it was the most expensive thing he ever bought.

The squire, meanwhile, was displeased, and one displeasure woke another, as they will in a man who keeps them all in one locked drawer.

If the London swell needed money before he would love Lily properly, then it was time — past time — that the other plan was made fast. The tidy one. The plan of his own quiet years: Bernard and Bell, the two houses joined, the name safe. He told Bernard so plainly. And he added what he had never put into words before — an income of eight hundred a year, settled on Bernard the day he married his cousin. The squire would angle for nothing. But he would pay, on delivery, for order.

Bernard, a soldier, went where he was sent. He proposed to Bell on the gravel walk on a Tuesday morning, and it must be said he did it honestly. No borrowed heat; no poetry that neither of them would have believed. They had always been good friends, he said. The marriage would please everyone and gather the family. He would make her a careful husband, and his uncle would be generous. It was a proposal like the minutes of the last meeting, read aloud, waiting only for a seconder.

Bell heard him all the way through, walking slowly, her eyes on the gravel. Being Bell, she did not pretend to need a week.

"You have asked me fairly," she said, "so I will answer fairly. No, Bernard. I am fond of you — as the family is fond, as cousins are. If I married where I was only fond, I should make you a false wife with a true face. You would never even know what was wrong with your house. You deserve better than a wife who is pleased with the arrangement. And so do I."

"My uncle," said Bernard, after a silence, "will be greatly disappointed."

"Yes," said Bell, gently — because it was true, and because it was, she noticed, the first entirely sincere sentence of the morning.

So the squire was twice refused in one month. Once by a man who wanted his money; once by a girl who did not. The drawer was locked on both, and he said almost nothing. The vegetables went down to the Small House as always. The milk went down morning and evening. The peaches ripened against the warm brick wall and were shared to the last one. Everything crossed that garden all August, everything, in the old full measure — except the words, which you knew already. And now there was a little less of the old grim warmth behind the no-words. The Small House felt it without being able to name it, the way one feels a door left open somewhere in a large house.

Down at the bottom of the garden the stream ran on under the little bridge, carrying its one sentence out to sea. In London a man at the head of his office did sums. And September came.
