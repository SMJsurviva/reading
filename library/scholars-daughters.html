The Scholar’s Daughters

After Jane Austen’s Sense and Sensibility (1811) — retold in old Korea.
Chapters one to 7 — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. This is Jane Austen’s famous novel Sense and Sensibility, retold in simple English — and moved from England to old Korea. The people and places have Korean names now, but the story is Austen’s: two sisters, one who hides her heart and one who cannot, and what happens to them when their family loses everything. It is best read slowly, a chapter or two at a time, like watching a drama.

Chapter One — The Promise

For more than a hundred years, the Yun family had lived in the big house at Pine Hill. The house stood on a low hill a day's ride from the capital, with tall pine trees behind it and rice fields below. People in the village called it a lucky house. For a long time, they were right.

Master Yun was a scholar — a quiet, kind man who loved his books, his garden, and his family. He had married twice. His first wife died young and left him one son, Ji-ho. His second wife, Lady Han, gave him three daughters: Seo-yeon, Mae-hwa, and little Dan-bi.

The two older sisters could not have been more different.

Seo-yeon, nineteen, was calm and careful. When there was trouble in the house, she was the one who thought first and spoke second. She felt things deeply — more deeply than anyone knew — but she kept her feelings folded away, like good clothes in a chest.

Mae-hwa, seventeen, folded nothing away. When she was happy, the whole house knew it. When she was sad, the whole village knew it. She loved poems, rain, plum blossoms, and playing her gayageum — a long Korean instrument with twelve strings — late into the night. "If you feel something," she liked to say, "why hide it?"

Dan-bi was twelve, and copied whichever sister she had spoken to last.

Ji-ho, the son, was almost forty. He lived in the capital with his wife, Lady Cho, and came home twice a year for the family rites. He was not a bad man. But he was a weak one, and he had married a wife who was not weak at all.

In the autumn, Master Yun fell ill. Doctors came from the village, then from the capital, and then it no longer mattered where the doctors came from. On his last night, he sent everyone away except his son.

"Ji-ho," he said, "the law is the law. The house, the land, the fields — all of it passes to you. Your stepmother and your sisters will have almost nothing. Promise me you will take care of them."

"I promise, Father," said Ji-ho. And at that moment, he truly meant it.

Master Yun closed his eyes, at peace. He had lived well, and he died believing his family was safe.

He was wrong about that.

Seven days after the funeral, a line of horses and carts came up the road to Pine Hill. Lady Cho had arrived — with her young son, twelve servants, and a face like the first day of winter. She did not ask which rooms were free. She took the best ones.

"From today," she told the servants, "you answer to me."

And just like that, Lady Han became a guest in the house where she had lived for twenty years. She said nothing. Her daughters watched her say nothing, and each sister learned a different lesson from it.

That night, in the warm inner room, Ji-ho spoke to his wife.

"I promised Father I would take care of them," he said. "I have been thinking. I will give them the three rice fields by the river. The rent from those fields would feed them well."

Lady Cho set down her tea very slowly.

"Three fields," she said. "Three fields. And what about our son? Those fields are his future. Would you take rice out of your own child's mouth and give it to your half-sisters?"

"They are still my sisters —"

"Half-sisters. And girls. Girls need very little — everyone knows that. They will all marry, and then their husbands will feed them. Your father was a good man, but he was old and sick when he asked you. He surely never meant fields."

Ji-ho looked at the floor. "Perhaps one field, then."

"A whole field, forever? And what if the harvest is bad? No — if you must give something, give a little money each year."

"Money each year, then."

"Every year?" Lady Cho's eyes went wide. "For how long? Lady Han is barely fifty. She could live thirty more years. Do you plan to pay for thirty years? People will laugh at you."

Ji-ho rubbed his face. "Then what should I do? I promised him."

"And you will keep your promise," said Lady Cho warmly, for she could be very warm when she was winning. "Help them pay for their move. Send them some rice at New Year, and dried fish from time to time. Fish is a fine gift. That is real kindness — kindness a family can afford."

Ji-ho was quiet for a moment. Then he felt something loosen in his chest, and he mistook it for wisdom.

"You are right," he said. "Father would not have wanted more than that."

He slept very well that night.

The sisters did not.

In the weeks that followed, life at Pine Hill grew small and cold. Lady Cho counted the rice jars. She moved Lady Han's things out of the great room "to keep them safe." She smiled at the sisters the way you smile at guests who are staying too long.

Seo-yeon stayed polite. She poured tea for Lady Cho, praised her son, and gave no one anything to complain about. "We have no money and no house," she told Mae-hwa quietly. "We cannot pay for pride."

Mae-hwa could not do it. She stopped smiling at meals. When Lady Cho said, at dinner, that girls who read too many poems make poor wives, Mae-hwa stood up and left the table. Later, her gayageum sounded through the house, playing something sad and furious, and she played it beautifully.

Their mother wanted to leave at once — that night, on foot if needed. It was Seo-yeon who made her wait. "Wait, Mother. We have nowhere to go. Something will come."

And something came.

One morning a letter arrived from the far south, from a fishing village by the sea. It was from Uncle Park, a cousin of Lady Han's — a magistrate, round and loud and cheerful, who wrote the way he talked:

"Cousin! I hear things I do not like. Listen: there is a small house near mine, empty since spring. It is not grand. The roof is honest and the well is sweet. The fish here are fat, the neighbors are loud, and my evenings are too quiet. Come and fill them. Come, and bring all three girls."

Lady Han read the letter three times and cried twice.

"The sea," said Mae-hwa slowly. She had never seen it. Something in her chest, shut since her father died, opened just a little.

"A magistrate's village," said Seo-yeon, already counting what a small house would cost. "We can live there. We can manage."

Dan-bi just asked if she could have a dog.

They packed what was theirs, and it was not much: clothes, books, the gayageum, and a few things of their father's that Lady Cho had not yet "kept safe." On the last morning, Lady Cho came out to watch them go, wearing her best sad face over her great relief.

"You must visit," she said, meaning: never.

"You are too kind," said Seo-yeon, meaning: I know exactly what you are.

The cart rolled down the hill. Dan-bi waved at the pine trees until they were out of sight. Seo-yeon looked back once, only once, and then set her eyes on the road ahead. Mae-hwa did not look back at all.

Somewhere ahead of them was the sea.

A question to think about: Ji-ho made a promise to his dying father. What did he actually give his sisters in the end — and who changed his mind?

Chapter Two — The Quiet Guest

The road south took twelve days.

Twelve days of ox-carts and river crossings, of inns with thin walls and thinner soup. Lady Han rode in the first cart and tried to look brave. Mae-hwa watched the mountains change shape and said very little, which worried everyone. Dan-bi counted magpies — she reached two hundred and six. And Seo-yeon held the money pouch, and felt it grow lighter at every inn.

On the third day, somewhere between two brown hills, Dan-bi looked up from her counting.

"Do you think Master Eun-woo will ever visit us?"

For a while, nobody answered. The cart creaked on.

Because one good thing had happened in those last cold weeks at Pine Hill, and his name was Cho Eun-woo.

He was Lady Cho's younger brother. He had come, at his sister's invitation, to admire her new house. In this, Lady Cho was disappointed, because Eun-woo turned out to be very bad at admiring things.

He was twenty-five, plainly dressed for a rich family's son, with a quiet voice and a way of standing a little apart, as if every room were slightly too full for him. At his first dinner he praised nothing — not the food, not the view, not his sister's newly arranged great room. Lady Cho's smile grew stiff. But when Lady Han came in late, tired from packing, it was Eun-woo who stood up first. It was Eun-woo who filled her cup before she asked, and moved the warmest cushion toward her without a word.

The family had been ready to dislike him. It lasted about half a day.

He mended the tail of Dan-bi's kite. He sat with Lady Han while she told long stories about her husband, and he never once glanced at the door. And on the fourth morning, he found Seo-yeon in the study, packing her father's books, and simply began to help.

They disagreed almost at once — about a poet, of course. Seo-yeon said the old poems were best, because they wasted no words. Eun-woo said the new poems were best, because they were still a little wrong, "and wrong things can still grow." They argued for two hours, very politely, missed lunch, and were both very pleased with the morning.

After that they were friends. Nobody used any other word for it. They were most careful of all not to use another word for it themselves.

Mae-hwa, of course, had to test him. One evening she put a book in his hands and begged him to read her favorite poem aloud — the one about the fisherman's wife who waits all winter by the sea. Eun-woo read it clearly, correctly, and with about as much feeling as a clerk counting bags of rice.

Mae-hwa sat through it the way some people sit through a toothache.

"How can you bear him?" she cried to Seo-yeon afterward. "That poem should be read like weeping! He read it like a tax report!"

"He reads quietly," said Seo-yeon. "Not everything loud is deep."

"And not everything quiet is deep, either!" said Mae-hwa — which was also fair.

One grey afternoon, in the bare winter garden, Eun-woo told Seo-yeon the shape of his life. His mother, the great Madam Cho, had planned it years ago: first place in the state examination, then a post at court, then a grand marriage, then power. His sister spoke of it at every meal. The word the family loved was "rise."

"And what do you want?" Seo-yeon asked.

He thought about it the way he thought about everything — as if the question deserved better than his first answer.

"A village school," he said at last. "Somewhere small. Teaching children their letters, and how to be decent to each other. My mother says small things are for small men."

"And what do you say?"

"As little as possible," said Eun-woo.

Seo-yeon laughed — a real laugh, her first since her father died. It surprised them both. And from the veranda, Lady Cho saw the two of them laughing together among the dead chrysanthemums, and her face went very still.

That night at dinner, Lady Cho talked at length about her brother's future. About the examination. About three great families in the capital — she named each one — whose daughters were beautiful, accomplished, and rich.

"Our mother has plans for Eun-woo," she said sweetly, to no one and to everyone. "Very high plans. It would be a pity if anything small got in the way."

The room went quiet. Seo-yeon poured the tea without spilling a single drop, which cost her more than anyone knew.

The next morning, Lady Han sent her answer to Uncle Park: they would come at once.

On the last morning, while Lady Cho was still arranging her face for the farewell, Eun-woo was already out by the carts in the cold, being useful. When the loading was done, he came to Seo-yeon and held out a small parcel wrapped in blue cloth.

Inside was her father's book of poems — the little worn one, his favorite, which Lady Cho had "kept safe" weeks ago.

Seo-yeon looked at it, and then at him. He did not explain how he had got it, and she did not ask.

"Some things should travel with their family," he said.

Then he bowed, and said, "I hope the sea is kind to you." He looked, for a moment, like a man about to say something else. He didn't say it.

"Will Master Eun-woo ever visit us?" Dan-bi asked again, on the ninth day of the journey.

"Perhaps," said Lady Han. "If his mother allows it."

"His mother," said Mae-hwa darkly, "sounds like winter in a silk dress."

Seo-yeon said nothing. She touched the little book, which rode inside her sleeve, and watched the hills go by.

On the twelfth day, near evening, the carts climbed one last hill and stopped.

Below them lay the sea.

It was bigger than anything they had ever seen. It was grey and silver and moving everywhere at once, and it went on to the very end of the world, and then it kept going. The wind that came off it smelled of salt and cold and somewhere else.

Mae-hwa stood straight up in the cart.

Lady Han found that she was crying, and didn't mind.

"Do dogs like the sea?" asked Dan-bi.

The village of Byeolpo sat around its harbor like guests around a table — low houses, brown nets drying on poles, boats leaning against the evening light. And on a low cliff at the edge of the village, behind a stone wall and one crooked persimmon tree, stood a small house with three rooms.

It was theirs. The whole of it would have fitted inside the great room at Pine Hill.

Lady Han looked at it for a long moment, and then she smiled anyway, which took some strength.

"Well," she said. "The roof looks honest."

They had not yet untied the first box when a voice came up the path like a festival drum. It was Uncle Park — round, loud, out of breath, followed by two servants carrying dinner.

"Cousin! You came! Look at you all — too thin, every one of you! Never mind. You're in Byeolpo now." He beamed at them. "Everything here can be fixed with fish."

That night, Seo-yeon lay in the smallest room of the small house, listening to a sound she had never slept with before: waves, arriving one by one in the dark, unhurried, like someone turning the pages of a very long book.

Far away, in a garden that no longer belonged to them, the chrysanthemums were under snow.

She let herself think about that garden for exactly as long as one wave took to fold onto the sand. Then she put the thought away, like good clothes in a chest, and slept.

Chapter Three — The Village by the Sea

In Byeolpo, everyone knew everything about everyone, usually before breakfast.

By their first market day, the whole village knew that a scholar's widow and her three daughters had come to live in the little house on the cliff — the one the fishermen called the Sea Cliff House. It was known that they were poor but proper. It was known that the eldest daughter did the accounts herself, that the youngest was hunting the village for dogs to pet, and that the middle one played the gayageum better than anyone had heard in years.

How the village knew this last thing before Mae-hwa had played a single note, nobody could say. But Byeolpo was rarely wrong, and it was not wrong this time.

The house was small, and money was smaller. Seo-yeon kept the ledger now, as she had kept it since her father died. Rice, firewood, lamp oil, salt — she counted everything twice and wrote it down in a neat, calm hand, and if the numbers frightened her, the numbers were the only ones who knew.

They could afford one servant. Her name was Sun-im, a fisherman's daughter of sixteen who could gut a fish in four seconds, carry water all day, and sing — loudly, constantly, and almost entirely on the wrong notes. Dan-bi adored her immediately.

Lady Han, meanwhile, made plans. A new room for guests, she thought, on the east side. A little pavilion in the garden, where Mae-hwa could play on summer nights. Perhaps a proper gate.

"Yes, Mother," Seo-yeon would say. "Next year."

It was always next year. They both knew it. It made them both feel better anyway.

Grief, they were discovering, did not come at the grave moments, when everyone stood ready for it. It waited behind small things instead — the smell of ink, a way some stranger in the market cleared his throat. It knocked them down one at a time, at odd hours, and the others poured tea and said nothing much, and the days went on. The days were kind that way. There were so many of them, and they kept coming.

Uncle Park could not bear the thought of them eating supper alone, and said so, often, at great volume. So two or three evenings a week, the ladies of the Sea Cliff House walked up the hill to the magistrate's house, where the food was excellent and the company came in three flavors.

There was Uncle Park himself, who laughed at his own stories, and had good reason to, because they were good stories.

There was his wife, Lady Min, who was beautiful, perfectly mannered, owned the finest silks in three counties, and spoke mainly in order to agree with people. Conversation with Lady Min was like resting.

And there was Lady Min's mother, Madam Oh — the widow of a rich merchant, up from the city for a long stay. Madam Oh wore six gold rings, laughed like a market bell, and said whatever came into her head, and her head was busy. Within an hour of meeting the sisters, she had asked them what sort of husbands they wanted. Receiving no answer, she promised to find them some anyway.

"It gives me something to do," she explained, comfortably. "I have married off my daughters, and a woman must have a project."

It was at one of these dinners that the sisters first met Commander Baek.

He was an old friend of Uncle Park's — a soldier, back from long years on the northern frontier, with land of his own at a place called Plum Valley, two days inland. He was past thirty-five. He had a still face, careful eyes, and a way of saying nothing that was somehow more polite than most people's talking.

That evening, Uncle Park begged Mae-hwa to play. She was rusty, she said; the sea air, she said; and then she played for an hour.

Uncle Park admired the music greatly, and loudly, and mostly while it was still being played. Lady Min agreed with each thing her husband said. Madam Oh beat time on her knee, incorrectly, with feeling.

Only Commander Baek listened to the whole song, from the first note to the silence after the last one, without moving at all. And when it was over, he said just one thing:

"Would you play the ending again?"

Which was, Seo-yeon thought, the only true compliment paid all evening.

Mae-hwa played the ending again — for the song's sake, she told herself, not for his.

Walking home under the cold stars, Madam Oh's campaign began.

"A commander!" she said. "Land at Plum Valley! Rich, sad, and unmarried — a waste of all three, and waste is a sin. Mae-hwa, my girl, you could do much worse."

"He is thirty-five if he is a day," said Mae-hwa. "Perhaps forty. And he wore a padded vest."

"It was a cold night," said Seo-yeon.

"A padded vest, sister." Mae-hwa said it with the complete certainty of seventeen. "A man in a padded vest has finished with love. He has folded it away with his summer clothes, and he is not getting it out again."

"Your father wore one every winter of his life," said Lady Han, mildly.

"Father was married. He was allowed."

A few days later, a servant came down from the magistrate's house with a flat parcel for Mae-hwa. Inside was a music score, copied out by hand — an old song for the gayageum that she had mentioned, once, in passing, at dinner: her father used to hum it, and their copy of the score was lost with the house.

Commander Baek had written it out from memory. The brushwork was small, exact, and patient. There was no letter with it — only his name.

"Hm," said Mae-hwa. "His hand is neat."

She played it through once that evening, while the lamps burned down. And that — his hand is neat — was all the praise Commander Baek received that spring.

The winter loosened. The rains came and went, the boats went out further, and the hills behind Byeolpo turned a sudden, impatient green. Mae-hwa grew restless. She had made friends with every hill in walking distance, and went out among them daily, in all weathers, coming home with wet hems, wind-red cheeks, and pockets full of things that had caught her eye.

"There's rain coming, miss," said Sun-im one grey afternoon, sniffing the wind like a fisherman's daughter. "Big rain. Stay home."

"It's only cloud," said Mae-hwa, and went out with Dan-bi to climb the high hill.

She was wrong about the rain. She was wrong, that spring, about a number of things.

Chapter Four — The Rain on the Hill

There is a kind of grey sky that only looks harmless to people who want a walk.

Under exactly that sky, Mae-hwa and Dan-bi climbed the high hill behind Byeolpo. The wind up there was glorious. It came straight off the sea with nothing to slow it down, and it made the long grass run in waves, so that the whole hill seemed to be going somewhere. Mae-hwa stood at the top with her arms out and the wind pulling her hair loose, and recited poems at the horizon, which accepted them.

Dan-bi, further down, was talking to a farm dog through a fence.

The rain did not arrive the way rain usually does, with warnings. It arrived all at once, cold and hard and slanting, as if the sky had been holding its breath for days.

The girls ran. The path down the hill was steep, and the grass was already slick, and Mae-hwa was laughing too hard to be careful — and then her foot went out from under her, and she was down, hard, with her ankle folded wrong beneath her.

She tried to stand. The ankle refused, with a hot white pain that made the hillside swim.

"Get help!" she shouted to Dan-bi through the rain — and then they both heard it: hoofbeats.

A rider came up the lower path with a hunting dog at his heels — a young man, as wet as they were and enjoying it considerably more. He took in the scene at a glance, swung down from the horse, and crouched beside Mae-hwa in the streaming grass.

"The hill is slippery," he said.

"I noticed," said Mae-hwa, through her teeth.

He laughed — a real laugh, quick and warm, snatched away by the wind. Then he turned to Dan-bi, very seriously, rain running off his chin.

"May I carry your sister down? I promise I've done this before. Sacks of rice, mostly."

Dan-bi, delighted beyond words, gave her permission.

He lifted Mae-hwa as if she weighed nothing at all and carried her down the hill, through the rain, along the cliff path, and in at the gate of the Sea Cliff House, where Lady Han met them at the door with a cry that brought the whole household running.

He would not come in — he was too wet, he said, and dripped on the doorstone to prove it. He set Mae-hwa down as gently as a teacup, bowed to Lady Han, and begged permission to call the next day, to ask after the ankle.

His name, he said, was Kang Do-hyun, of the Cedar House up the valley — his great-aunt's estate. Then he was back in the rain, and gone.

The house, it is fair to say, did not calm down for hours.

Mae-hwa lay on the warm floor with her ankle bound, her cheeks a colour that had nothing to do with the cold. Dan-bi told the story of the rescue five times before supper, and it grew each time.

"He lifted her like a queen!"

"Like a wet queen," said Mae-hwa, with dignity.

Uncle Park, arriving next morning with fish and information, supplied the rest.

"Kang Do-hyun! Ha! The best young man in three counties, and the best company in ten. Rides like a frontier scout. Sings like a boatman. His great-aunt is the old lady of the Cedar House — sharp as a fish-hook, that one — and he's her heir, the whole valley knows it. He comes down every spring. Well done, Mae-hwa! Best catch since my wife!"

Lady Min agreed that it was.

Madam Oh's rings clicked with joy. As far as she was concerned, the matter was settled; there remained only the pleasure of watching it happen.

Do-hyun called the next day, to ask after the ankle. He called the day after that, in case the ankle had worsened. By the fourth visit he had stopped inventing reasons, and nobody minded at all.

He and Mae-hwa fitted like two halves of a broken plate. The same poets. The same songs. The same way of talking with their hands, of jumping up mid-sentence, of caring far too much about things it was safest not to care about. One evening he took up the book on the floor beside her and read out — unasked — the poem about the fisherman's wife who waits all winter by the sea.

He read it the way Mae-hwa had always known it should be read: like weeping, but proud.

Mae-hwa shot her sister a look of pure triumph.

Seo-yeon smiled back, and thought, privately, that reading a poem beautifully was a skill, like archery — and proved about as much about a man's heart as archery did. Then she wondered if she was being unfair. Then she kept the thought anyway, folded away where she kept things.

By the second week, Mae-hwa and Do-hyun were finishing each other's sentences. By the third, they were correcting each other's favorite poems, which is a far more intimate activity.

If there was a shadow anywhere that bright spring, it was small, and easy to miss.

It looked like this: at the magistrate's table, when Uncle Park praised Kang Do-hyun's name for the third time in an evening, Commander Baek grew very interested in his cup of tea, and shortly afterward remembered a long ride he had to make in the morning, and left early. Only Seo-yeon noticed. She was the sort of person who noticed the people leaving a room as well as the ones filling it.

And once, walking on the shore path, Do-hyun himself said lightly:

"Commander Baek is the kind of man everyone praises and nobody talks to."

Mae-hwa laughed.

"That is unkind," said Seo-yeon.

"It is," Do-hyun agreed, cheerfully. "And everyone will forgive me for it. That's the trouble with me — I'm always forgiven." And he smiled so well, saying it, that both sisters forgot to hear it properly.

The weeks ran on. There were walks along the cliff with Dan-bi trailing behind as chaperone, paid in sweets. There were poems copied out and exchanged like state secrets. The whole of Byeolpo — which knew everything before breakfast — spoke of the two of them as a settled thing.

But no word had been said. No families had met. Nothing was promised.

"You give him everything so openly," Seo-yeon said one night, when the lamp was low. "Your time, your letters, your whole heart, where the village can see it. And nothing is settled, Mae-hwa. Nothing has been asked."

Mae-hwa lay back and smiled at the ceiling.

"If I feel something, why should I hide it?" she said — as she had said all her life. "He knows my heart, and I know his. Hiding it wouldn't make it any smaller. You'd have me keep love like money in a locked box, sister."

"I'd have you keep a little," said Seo-yeon, "in case of winter."

But there is no arguing with seventeen, in spring.

The plum trees flowered early that year, all along the valley, and everyone in Byeolpo agreed it was a lucky sign. Everyone, that is, except Commander Baek — but then, Commander Baek said nothing at all.

Chapter Five — Poems and Promises

The weeks that followed were the kind Byeolpo remembered for years.

Kang Do-hyun came every morning and most afternoons. He and Mae-hwa walked the cliff paths and the valley road and the long shore where the fishing boats came in. They sat at Uncle Park's table arguing about poems, both talking at once, while the fish went cold. Dan-bi went with them wherever she was allowed, which was most places, and accepted payment in sweets, and was a very bad chaperone, and did not mind at all.

Lady Han was making plans. She had not mentioned these plans aloud, because Seo-yeon would have asked her to stop. But they were forming, quietly and steadily, in the way her plans always did — built on warmth and hope and a certain comfortable disregard for anything that might stand in the way.

The village had an opinion. The village always had an opinion. On this subject its opinion was settled. It was so settled that people began to plan around it — speaking in knowing looks, arranging the future in small conversational ways — as if the only remaining work was for the two young people to look up from their poems long enough to notice.

Seo-yeon noticed something else.

Nothing had been promised. Nothing had been asked.

She was not the sort of person who said things loudly. She watched Do-hyun's face when he laughed, and she watched Mae-hwa's face watching him, and she thought: it may be exactly what it looks like. Or it may not. Both of those things were perfectly possible. Only one of them ended well. That thought she kept folded away, where she kept things.

One afternoon in early summer, Do-hyun arrived with a small wrapped bundle. He gave it to Mae-hwa in front of everyone, which was very like him.

Inside were gayageum strings — fine ones, wound in silk, the kind sold in capital shops at a price that was not nothing. Mae-hwa's second string had snapped the week before, mid-practice, and she had been playing around it with patient frustration ever since.

"Oh," she said, touching them. "These are very good strings."

Lady Han was delighted. Madam Oh's rings clicked. Uncle Park said that a man who remembered a snapped string would remember a great many things, and was very pleased with himself for saying it.

That evening, when the guests were gone and the house was quiet, Seo-yeon sat beside her sister.

"The strings are too much," she said.

Mae-hwa looked up. "They are not expensive."

"They are not about the price. A gift like that — thought through, intimate — says something has been decided. And nothing has been decided, Mae-hwa. Not formally. Not in any way that protects either of you."

Mae-hwa was quiet for a moment. She did not like this. It was true and she did not like it.

"You are saying I should return them."

"I am saying it would be the wiser course."

Mae-hwa said a number of things after that. They were all worth saying, and none of them were the point. In the end she wrote a note — friendly, she hoped, though Seo-yeon suspected it read a little cold — and sent the strings back.

Do-hyun received this without visible hurt. He came the next morning as he always came, and said nothing about the strings, and brought something instead that could not be returned: a poem, copied in his own hand from the Cedar House library, because copying a poem costs nothing and proves nothing and means everything to people who care about such things, which they both did.

Mae-hwa read it twice and forgave him in approximately thirty seconds. She did not mention this to Seo-yeon, as it would only have led to a conversation she could not yet win.

It was a week later that Do-hyun said, as they were setting out on the cliff walk: "My great-aunt is away in the capital this week. You should come and see the Cedar House while it is quiet. The plum trees in the upper garden are still flowering."

"I would like that very much," said Mae-hwa.

"When does she return?" said Seo-yeon.

"Not until the end of the month." He said it pleasantly, as if this answered the question behind the question.

It did not. But Mae-hwa had already agreed, and Dan-bi had already started walking, and Seo-yeon found herself moving with them, because the alternative was to stay on the road alone and explain herself to the next passerby.

The Cedar House was further up the valley than it appeared from the shore. It was old, and large, and very well ordered — the kind of house that has been run by the same methods for a long time and sees no need to change. The upper garden was famous across the county, and deserved to be. The plum trees were pale and late, blossoming against dark old wood, and there was a stillness to the place that surprised Seo-yeon — a dignity that went beyond the size of it.

Dan-bi found a large tortoiseshell cat sleeping on the garden wall and sat down at once to discuss matters with it.

Mae-hwa walked through the garden as if she had been there before. She had not been there before. She just moved that way.

They walked home in the late afternoon, the valley road warm and golden.

"We should not have come here," Seo-yeon said, when they were out of earshot of the house.

"His great-aunt was not at home."

"That is not what makes it improper, Mae-hwa."

Mae-hwa walked a little further before answering. "You think it looks as if we are taking something for granted."

"I think it could easily be seen that way. By him. By her, when she returns."

A pause. The sea was visible in gaps between the hills, flat and bright.

"And are we?" said Mae-hwa. "Taking it for granted?"

"I think," said Seo-yeon, "that nothing is settled. That is all I have ever said."

Mae-hwa did not answer. Dan-bi ran to catch up with them, calling back that the cat's name was General and she had decided this just now and it was final.

That night Mae-hwa did not play the gayageum. She sat at the window for a long time, looking toward the upper valley where the Cedar House stood among its plum trees, and Seo-yeon heard her from the next room and said nothing.

Nothing was promised. That was the trouble. That was also, in a way, the hope.

That was spring, and it lasted exactly as long as spring does.

Chapter Six — The Broken Morning

Summer came to Byeolpo the way summer always came — without announcement, simply there one morning, the light different, the sea a deeper blue, the air carrying something warm that had not been there before.

Do-hyun came with it. If anything, the longer days brought him more often, not less. The plum trees at the Cedar House were done flowering; the upper valley was green and loud with birds. Mae-hwa learned three new songs that month. Dan-bi acquired a strong personal opinion about fishing, which no one had asked for but which she was glad to share.

Seo-yeon watched, and said little, and kept her counsel.

The morning it happened, Do-hyun had come early. Earlier than usual, without the easy air he usually wore, which Seo-yeon noticed but Mae-hwa did not, because Mae-hwa was too happy to look for small signs.

A rider from the Cedar House came while they were at breakfast. He dismounted at the gate and handed Do-hyun a folded note and stood waiting, hat in hand.

Do-hyun read it. His face did not collapse or change dramatically — it only went still, the way a pond goes still just before the wind drops. He read it once more, folded it, and put it away inside his jacket.

"I have to go," he said.

"Today?" said Mae-hwa.

"Now. My great-aunt needs me in the capital."

"When will you come back?"

He looked at her. "I don't know," he said. "I will write when I can."

He took his leave of Lady Han with perfect courtesy. He thanked Uncle Park, who had appeared at the door with fish and an expression that was trying very hard to be helpful and was not. He said to Madam Oh that she was very kind. He said to Mae-hwa that she should keep playing in his absence, which was not the thing she needed him to say, but was the thing he said.

Then he was gone. The rider followed him up the road, and the dust settled, and the morning looked exactly the same as it had before.

Mae-hwa stood in the courtyard for a moment.

"He'll write when he reaches the capital," she said, to no one in particular. "He said so."

"He said he would write when he could," said Seo-yeon, carefully.

"That is the same thing."

Seo-yeon did not say that it was not.

The first week was not terrible. Mae-hwa played the gayageum for long hours, which she did when her mind was busy, and claimed it was for practice, which it partly was. She checked the road at intervals she did not notice she was checking. When Uncle Park came for dinner, she asked if he had heard any news from the capital, in a tone that was meant to sound idle and did not quite manage it.

Uncle Park had not heard anything. He offered fish.

The second week, the letters they expected did not come. Mae-hwa began to leave gaps in sentences that she would once have filled immediately. "He is probably very occupied" was one. "The capital is a complicated place" was another. Seo-yeon agreed with all of them, which was the kindest thing she could do.

At night she could hear Mae-hwa playing — low and slow, not the songs she had played when Do-hyun was there, but older things, sadder in their bones.

The third week, Byeolpo began to wonder out loud.

Madam Oh said — tactfully, for Madam Oh — that the Cedar House had connections in Hanyang, and sometimes great-aunts had plans. Lady Min agreed, very gently, that sometimes things were arranged quickly. Uncle Park said that young men were unpredictable, and when he said it he looked at his feet, which meant he knew something and would not say it.

Seo-yeon did not contribute to any of this. She had her own thoughts and they were not cheerful, but they were also not yet confirmed, and unconfirmed fears were best kept folded away.

Commander Baek came to dinner one evening. He did not mention Do-hyun, which was unusual given that everyone else mentioned Do-hyun. He ate quietly. He answered when he was spoken to. When Lady Han, who was not always careful, said that she hoped Do-hyun would have good news when he returned, Commander Baek said: "I hope so too, Lady Han," in a voice that meant nothing at all.

Seo-yeon heard it as meaning a great deal, and kept this to herself.

The gap where Do-hyun's visits had been was not loud. It was very quiet, which was worse. Mae-hwa did not talk about missing him. She talked about everything else, a little too much, in a way that left no room for silence.

"He will write," she said, one evening, late, when the lamp was nearly out.

Seo-yeon looked at her sister's face in the low light.

"He may," she said. She wanted to say more and did not.

"You think I am foolish."

"I think you are seventeen."

Mae-hwa was quiet a long time.

"That is not an answer."

"No," said Seo-yeon. "It is not."

She put out the lamp. Through the thin wall she heard the sea, constant and unhelpful, which was the sea's way.

Summer went on. Byeolpo learned to not mention Do-hyun when Mae-hwa was present, which was the kindest thing the village knew how to do. Mae-hwa accepted this without speaking of it, which was the bravest thing she knew how to do.

No letter came.

Chapter Seven — The Visitor with the Silk Knot

Late summer arrived. Then early autumn. The persimmon tree in the courtyard turned slowly orange, and the sea on cold mornings had a grey edge that it had not had in July, and Mae-hwa played the gayageum every day and did not say his name, and life at Sea Cliff House continued, because life does.

It was in early autumn that Cho Eun-woo arrived.

Lady Han had written to Ji-ho in the spring, in better days, wondering whether Lady Cho's younger brother might come south for a short visit. She had framed it as hospitality — it was natural to return a little kindness to Lady Cho's family, she had written to Seo-yeon, though Seo-yeon understood well enough what the real motive was. Lady Han was kind, and she also believed that the company of new people was good for people who were waiting for letters that did not come.

He arrived with a servant, two trunks, and a box of books.

He was not what Lady Han had expected. She had taken Lady Cho's description — handsome, accomplished, great prospects — and found a young man who was all of those things in a quieter way than she had imagined. Not cold. Not unpleasant. But subdued, in the way of a room where a window had been left half-shuttered. He asked questions and listened to the answers. He did not recite things unless someone asked.

Madam Oh liked him, which meant he passed the test that mattered most in Byeolpo.

Uncle Park declared that a man who brought books south in the summer was either very studious or avoiding something, and that both of these were fine.

Eun-woo seemed grateful for this judgment and did not clarify which applied.

On the first day, Seo-yeon sat beside him on the veranda and they talked about the village. He wanted to know what it was like — not what the magistrate would report, but what she saw every day. She said the children were better than their parents thought. She said the sea was louder in winter, which surprised her, though perhaps it shouldn't. She said the persimmon tree was old and the fruit was hard, but the birds did not seem to mind.

He listened to all of it.

"There is a village school?" he asked.

"A small one. Two rooms."

"What does the teacher teach?"

"Reading. Some calculation. What the examination demands."

He was quiet a moment. "Not what the children might want to know."

"There are many things wanted that the examination does not cover," she said. "The examination takes some notice of this, I am told, but not much."

He smiled — a real one, not the polite kind. "I think I would like to teach somewhere like this," he said. "A village school. Something that mattered to the people in front of you."

"Your mother would not be pleased."

He looked at her, a little startled.

"Forgive me," she said. "I shouldn't have—"

"No," he said. "No, you are right. She would not." He picked up his tea. "I find it difficult to be wrong and also right at the same time. Perhaps you know how that feels."

She thought that she knew how it felt very well, but she did not say so.

On his second day, before the morning meal, he came to find her in the courtyard. He was carrying a thin, worn book with a cloth cover. He held it out without preamble.

"I found this among the things Lady Cho sent for safekeeping after Pine Hill. It has your family seal on the binding."

She took it.

It was her father's poem book — the one she had last seen in Lady Cho's hands on the day of packing, being moved to "keep safe." She had not asked after it. Asking would have been acknowledging that she was afraid it was gone.

"You asked for it back?" she said.

"I mentioned that it likely belonged to the family." He said it carefully, as if not to make too much of it. "She had set it aside."

Lady Han, if she had seen, would have said: a very good young man. Seo-yeon said: "Thank you, Master Cho." She meant it in a way that was hard to say plainly.

He nodded once and left her with it.

She noticed, then, what she had half-noticed before. He had a small knotted token tucked into his jacket — two colors of twisted silk, the kind of small thing made by careful hands for someone to carry. She had seen him take it out twice now, turn it between his fingers without thinking, and put it away. He did not know she was watching. He had not noticed doing it.

She did not ask about it.

He was quiet at meals, though not unpleasantly so. Mae-hwa was kind to him, in the careful, surface-warm way she was kind to most visitors now. Dan-bi talked at him about fish and Admiral the cat's recent opinions, which Eun-woo received with more patience than they deserved.

He left after four days.

At the gate, with his horse ready, he turned back.

"You are very sensible, Lady Yun," he said. "I mean it as the highest compliment."

"Thank you," she said.

"I hope—" He stopped. "I hope things go well for your family. I mean that also."

He mounted and rode north, and the dust settled, and the sound of hooves faded into the valley.

Lady Han said he was a very nice young man and it was a pity he seemed so sad.

Uncle Park said he had eaten all the fish, which meant his heart was sound.

Mae-hwa said nothing, which these days was how she said most things.

That evening Seo-yeon sat by the window with her father's poem book in her lap. She did not read it. She only held it, feeling the worn cloth cover, the slight softness of pages handled many times by hands she remembered.

She thought about what it cost to do the right thing when no one would notice. She thought about it for a long time. She was not sure the thought was only about him.

Through the window the persimmon tree stood dark against the evening sky, its fruit heavy and orange and quietly ready.

Outside, the sea came in. The sea always came in.
