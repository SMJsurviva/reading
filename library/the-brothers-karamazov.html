The Brothers Karamazov

Fyodor Dostoevsky (1880) — retold in plain English, in its own 1870s Russia.
Chapters one to 3 — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. This is The Brothers Karamazov by Fyodor Dostoevsky — many people say the greatest novel ever written — retold in simple English and kept in its own world: a small Russian town about 150 years ago. It is the story of a terrible father and his three sons: a soldier, a scholar, and a young monk. It is about money, love, God, and a murder. The storyteller is a man of the town who saw it all happen, and he likes to talk — let him. He knows where he is going.

Chapter One — A Certain Family

I had better begin with the family, because everything that happened, happened to them; and I had better admit at once that I am not a clever storyteller. I am only a man of our town who saw a good deal of it, and heard the rest, and has never been able to forget any of it.

Our town is small. It stands a long way from anywhere important. We have a river, a market square, a prison, and — a mile outside town, behind pine trees — a monastery. Remember the monastery. A great deal of this story walks in and out of its gates.

And now the family.

Old Fyodor Karamazov was a landowner of our district, and he was famous in his day — not for anything good. He became truly famous later, because of his dark and terrible death, which I shall come to in its proper place. For now I will only say what kind of man he was, because his three sons cannot be understood without him.

He was the kind of man who is worthless and clever at the same time. People call such men fools, but that is wrong. Fyodor was not a fool. He began with almost nothing, ran other men's errands, ate other men's dinners, played the clown at other men's tables — and died worth a hundred thousand roubles. He had a gift for money the way some men have a gift for music. And with it, all his life, he had a hunger for pleasure that he never once tried to control, and a strange need to make himself shameful in public. I will have more to say about that need. It is the key to him.

He was married twice, and he had three sons.

His first wife, Adelaida, was a rich young woman from a fine family. Why did she marry him? Our whole town asked it, and I can only give the answer people gave then: she did it to be daring. It was a time when young ladies wished to show they had ideas of their own. Running away with the shameless hanger-on Fyodor Karamazov was certainly an idea of her own. She was sorry almost at once.

Fyodor grabbed her dowry — some twenty-five thousand roubles — and set about enjoying himself. There were scenes in that house I will not describe. In the end Adelaida ran away to Petersburg with a poor teacher, leaving behind her little son Dmitri, who was three years old. She died there, in a corner somewhere, of hunger or fever. When the news reached Fyodor, they say he ran into the street shouting — some said with joy, some said he wept. Perhaps both are true. A man can be glad to be free and weep for himself at the same time, especially a man like Fyodor.

And little Dmitri? Here is a thing you must know about Old Fyodor: he forgot him. He did not hate the child. Hating takes attention. He simply forgot that the boy existed, the way you forget an umbrella.

The one who remembered was Grigory, the old servant. Grigory was a stern, honest, stubborn man who served Fyodor all his life — why, God alone knows; perhaps someone must. He took the child to his cottage, washed him, and fed him. If Grigory had not existed, I do not know how any of the three boys would have survived their childhood. After a while the mother's relatives took Dmitri away, and he was passed from house to house like a parcel.

He grew up believing one thing: that his mother had left him an estate, and that when he came of age he would be rich. He left school early, joined the army, became an officer, fought a duel, was demoted, rose again, and spent money — always, everywhere, money that he did not yet have. From time to time his father sent him small sums, and the boy signed papers he did not read. By the time this story begins, Dmitri was twenty-eight: a soldier, strong as a bull, open-handed, quick to burn, honest in his own stormy way — and quite sure his father had cheated him of thousands. He came back to our town to have it out. That return is where all our trouble began.

But I am running ahead.

Fyodor's second wife, Sofya, was an orphan, raised in the house of a general's widow — a rich, severe old lady who kept the girl like a bird in a clean cage. Sofya was gentle, quiet, and lovely, and she had nobody in the world. Fyodor stole her. There is no other word; he carried her off without permission, and the girl went because a bird will fly through any open door, even a bad one.

He did not beat her. He did worse: he made his house a shameful place around her while she watched. I will not describe it. She was timid by nature, and in that house she fell into a nervous illness. Village women who have it are called shriekers. She would tremble and cry out and know nothing for hours. She bore him two sons all the same — Ivan, and then, four years later, Alexei, whom everyone has always called Alyosha. When Alyosha was in his fourth year she died. He remembered her all his life — not as a story, but in one picture: a summer evening, a slanting ray of sun across the room, the icon in the corner with its little lamp, and his mother holding him up toward it with both arms, sobbing, praying for him as if she wished to give him away to God before the house could touch him. That ray of evening sun stayed in him. Remember it, as you remember the monastery.

And Fyodor forgot these two boys exactly as he had forgotten the first. Again it was old Grigory's cottage that took them in; then the general's widow came in fury, took them away, and raised them; then she died, and a kind guardian saw to their schooling. So all three brothers grew up apart — from their father, and from one another. Dmitri hardly knew the younger two existed.

Ivan grew up proud and brilliant. By thirteen his teachers were saying he had a rare mind. He learned early that he was living on other people's charity, at other people's tables — he was his father's son in nothing, but he had the same sharp eye for how the world is arranged. At the university he refused all help and earned his own bread writing for the newspapers. Then he wrote an article that made him, for a season, famous. It was about the church courts, and it was so cleverly written that the church party read it with joy and the party against the church read it with equal joy, and each side was sure the author was theirs. A few readers — very few — finished it with a cold feeling, and wondered what the author himself believed, and whether he believed anything at all. Keep those readers in mind. They were on to something.

And Alyosha — Alyosha is my hero, and I am almost afraid to introduce him, because he was not remarkable in any of the usual ways, and I fear the reader will ask why a quiet boy of twenty deserves a whole book. I will say only this and let the story do the rest. Wherever Alyosha went, people loved him. He never pushed for it; he arrived at a school or a house full of strangers, and within the month he belonged to everyone there. He never remembered an insult. He never wanted anyone's money, and when he had any, he gave it away or lost it. When his own father, drunk at dinner, wept over him with sticky sentimental tears, Alyosha did not sneer at him — and Fyodor, who expected sneering the way other men expect good morning, never got over it. He loved the boy, in his fashion. Even he.

In the year our story begins, Alyosha came back to our town. He was twenty. He came, he said at first, to find his mother's grave. His father could not tell him where it was — he had forgotten that too. Old Grigory knew. Grigory had paid for the stone himself, and he led the boy to it, and stood aside with his hands folded while Alyosha stood a long time looking down and said nothing.

Soon after that, Alyosha went out to the monastery behind the pines and asked to stay. He had met the man who lived at the heart of it: the elder Zosima, an old monk of whom half our province said he was a living saint, and the other half said nothing, because holiness annoys people. What passed between Zosima and Alyosha belongs later in this book. It is enough for now that the boy found the door he had been looking for all his life, and walked through it.

So there they were, in the summer this story opens — all three brothers in our town at once, for the first time in their lives. Alyosha in the monastery. Ivan, newly arrived, staying — to everyone's surprise — in his father's house, the two clever men circling each other with courtesy. And Dmitri, in rented rooms, demanding his inheritance, sending his father letters that got angrier by the week.

It was Ivan who proposed, half smiling, that the family quarrel be settled peacefully — before a holy man. Let everyone meet in the elder Zosima's cell, he said, and talk it out under that gentle eye. Old Fyodor agreed with delight, which should have warned them all.

"Excellent," he said. "We shall be reconciled. In a monastery. I shall behave beautifully."

Our town still talks about how beautifully he behaved.

Chapter Two — The Meeting at the Monastery

The meeting was set for a warm, bright morning at the end of August.

They came in two carriages. In the first rode old Fyodor and Ivan, the father talkative and delighted with himself, the son silent. With them came a relative I must introduce, though he would rather I did not: Miusov, a cousin of Dmitri's dead mother — a polished gentleman of fifty who had lived half his life in Paris, believed in progress, despised the church, and had somehow agreed to attend a family peace conference in a monk's cell. He had come, he told himself, as an observer. He spent the whole day suffering.

Dmitri had been sent word of the hour. He was late.

I should say something about the place they were entering. Our monastery is not grand, but it is old, and people came to it from a thousand miles away — because of one man. The elder Zosima was sixty-five, small, and so weak by that summer that he could barely walk. An elder, I should explain, is a monk to whom other people bring their souls the way you bring a broken thing to the one man in town who can mend it. Peasant women waited at his door in crowds. Ladies came in carriages. Learned men came to argue, and went away quiet. He had a worn face, full of small wrinkles, and eyes of which I can only say that people remembered them years afterward, and were better for remembering.

This was the man in whose small cell the Karamazov family assembled to discuss money.

It went wrong from the doorway.

Old Fyodor entered the cell, looked around at the holy pictures, at the quiet monks, at the dying old man rising to greet him — and felt what he always felt in respectable places: that everyone was waiting for him to disgrace himself. And when Fyodor felt that, he could never bear the waiting. He disgraced himself at once, to get it over with.

He bowed too low. He called himself, loudly, a buffoon and the father of a buffoon. He told an anecdote that was not true and not decent. He asked pardon so noisily that the asking was worse than the offense. Miusov turned crimson and stared out the window. Ivan watched his father with a cold, curious little smile, as a man watches weather. Alyosha, who stood behind the elder's chair, could not lift his eyes at all.

The elder Zosima looked at Fyodor for a while, and then said, gently: "Do not be so ashamed of yourself. That is the root of it all."

Fyodor stopped as if struck. "Meaning what, holy father?"

"You play the fool," said the old man, "because you are ashamed, and the shame hurts you, so you rush to be shameful first — as if to say, you cannot laugh at me, I have already laughed. But a man who is always performing forgets, in time, which part of him is the performance. Do this one thing, and I ask nothing else of you: do not lie to yourself. The man who lies to himself comes at last to a state where he cannot tell the truth inside him from the lie, and then he cannot love, and then he cannot be loved, and to fill the emptiness he turns to pleasures — and he ends by being exactly what you called yourself at the door. And it is not true yet. It is not yet true."

There was a silence in the cell of a kind I cannot describe. For one moment — those who were there swear to it — old Fyodor's face was the face of a man looking through a window at the life he might have had. It lasted the length of a breath.

"Father," he cried, kissing the elder's hand with tears, "it is exactly as you say! All my life I have been ashamed, and lied first!" And even as he said it, delighted with the truth of it, he was turning the truth into a performance, and by the next sentence he was play-acting his own repentance, and the window had shut again.

It was into this room, an hour late, that Dmitri walked.

I have said he was a soldier, twenty-eight, strong and open-faced. He came in quickly, bowed to the elder with rough, real respect — he crossed himself, which surprised some — and asked pardon for his lateness. Then he took a chair opposite his father, and the business of the day began, and I will make short work of it, because it was never really about the sums.

Dmitri believed his mother's estate had owed him a fortune, and that his father had paid it out in scraps and kept the rest. Fyodor had papers — he always had papers — showing the scraps added to more than the fortune, so that Dmitri, far from being owed, was in his father's debt. There were figures: three thousand here, six thousand there. Miusov attempted a summary and made everything worse. The truth, which everybody in the room understood and nobody said, was this: the father was cheating the son within the letter of the law, and had been doing it for twenty years, because he had never once thought of Dmitri as a son. Only as a claimant.

But it was not the money that broke the meeting. It was the other thing, the thing fathers and sons should never fight over.

For Fyodor, seeing the morning slipping away without a disaster, made one. He brought up — in a monastery cell, before monks — the name of a certain woman of our town. Grushenka. I shall have much to say of her later; for now it is enough that Dmitri, a man betrothed to another woman, had lost his head over her — and that his father, sixty years wicked, had lost whatever he had in place of a head over her too, and had promised her money, and considered himself, God help us, a rival. He now accused his son, in the sweetest voice, of dishonoring a respectable girl to chase a shameless one — he, the father, having chased her first.

Dmitri came to his feet white as paper.

"Why is such a man alive?" he said, in a low voice, to the whole room. "Tell me — can he be allowed to go on dishonoring the earth?" He said other things too. It was ugly, as only family things are ugly. Miusov was on his feet; the monks were stone; Fyodor was shouting delightedly about disrespect; Ivan alone had not moved, watching, watching.

And then the thing happened that our town has never stopped discussing.

The elder Zosima rose. Alyosha, terrified, put out a hand to steady him, but the old man stepped away from the chair, walked to Dmitri, and knelt down before him — down to the floor, until his forehead touched the boards at the young man's feet. It was no fainting fit; it was a full, deliberate bow, to the earth. Then he rose, with difficulty, and said, mildly, "Forgive me. Forgive us all," and blessed them, and it was understood the meeting was over.

They went out stunned. Dmitri, who had stood through the bow like a man struck by lightning, rushed away with his face in his hands. Fyodor went quiet — for a while. Miusov said afterward that it was theater; he had recovered his opinions by the gate. But he had not said so in the cell.

Alyosha could not rest until he had asked. That evening, alone with the elder, he begged: "Why did you bow to my brother? What did it mean?"

The old man was silent a moment. Then he said: "I bowed to the suffering that is coming to him. Yesterday it was on its way. Now I have seen its face." And he would say nothing more about it, but sent Alyosha to bed with this instruction, which the boy did not understand either, and which decided the rest of his life: "When the time comes, be near your brothers. Both of them."

Chapter Three — The Old Buffoon

You might think the morning could not get worse. The people who were there thought so too, which shows how little any of us knew Fyodor Karamazov.

Here is what should have happened. The Father Superior — the head of the monastery, a stately, courteous old man — had invited the whole party to dinner after the meeting. It was an honor, and the dinner was ready: fish soup, good bread, pears, the monastery's own tea. After the scene in the cell, of course, the family could not decently sit down to it. Everyone understood that. Even Fyodor understood it. He announced, at the elder's door, that he was too ashamed to eat, begged everyone's pardon one last noisy time, and drove off toward town.

And halfway down the road, he stopped the carriage.

I promised in the first chapter to give you the key to this man, and here is the place to use it. Sitting in the carriage, Fyodor asked himself why he was going home, and found the answer waiting for him, as it had waited all his life. He was going home because he was ashamed. And at once the old mechanism turned over. They think I am past disgracing myself further, he thought. They are at dinner now, being gracious, being relieved. Impossible to fall lower, they are thinking. Well — and a kind of joy lit in him, the only joy he really trusted — we shall see about lower.

Understand this if you can, because the whole book stands on it: there are men in whom shame does not stop the fall. It greases it. Ordinary men do a shameful thing and pull back from the edge. Fyodor did a shameful thing, heard the gasp, and knew he was, for that one moment, the most important man in the room. No virtue of his had ever made anyone gasp. So he had made an instrument of his own disgrace, and played it for forty years, and by now he played it the way a drunkard drinks — with loathing, and with need. It was sad. I insist on that word. Our town found him funny; I ask you not to. Nothing about that carriage turning around on the monastery road was funny.

The carriage turned around.

They were at the soup when the door of the Father Superior's dining room opened and Fyodor Karamazov walked back in.

"I forgot my hat," he said into the silence. And then, having no hat there to forget: "No. That is a lie. I came back on purpose. Reverend fathers, do not be alarmed — I have only come to say what no one else will say in this pious place."

What followed took perhaps six minutes, and our town fed on it for six months. He accused the monastery of taking money from the peasants and calling it piety. He accused the monks of dining on fish soup — waving at the actual soup — while preaching fasting to the poor. He demanded to know what a monastery was for, in this day and age; he answered himself before anyone else could; the answer was not polite. He worked his way to the elders, and to Zosima by name — Zosima, who had spoken to him that morning more kindly than anyone had spoken to him in thirty years, and had been kissed and wept on for his trouble. That is worth stopping on for a moment. You might think the one man who had reached him would be the one man he spared. It was the opposite, and if you have followed me so far you will understand why: the kindness was the offense. Zosima had seen through him and had not despised him, and Fyodor could forgive anything but that. So the sneers grew cheaper and the voice grew louder, and it was all untrue, and the worst of it was that he did not believe a word of it himself; you could hear that he did not; the lies came out dressed as boldness and shivering underneath.

Miusov fled — actually fled, knocking over his chair, declaring to the corridor that he was done with this family forever. The Father Superior stood through it all with his head slightly bowed, and when the storm paused for breath he said, mildly, that no one at his table would answer railing with railing, and that dinner remained served if his guest wished to sit. That mildness finished Fyodor off worse than any shout. A performer needs a scandalized audience; forgiveness empties the theater.

He had one shot left, and he used it at the door. He turned, hunted the room with his eyes, and found Alyosha, who stood frozen against the wall.

"Alexei!" he called, and the joking voice had something ragged in it now. "You — come away from here. For good. Pack your things today. Your father commands it. No son of mine will stay among these people. I revoke everything. Come home!"

And he was gone. Ivan, who through the entire scene had eaten his soup without once looking up, laid down his spoon, went out, and got his father into the carriage the way one handles a piece of luggage: without anger and without gentleness. The last the monks saw of the Karamazovs that day was the old man's face turned back toward the refectory window, shouting something nobody chose to hear.

By nightfall the whole town had the story, and by Sunday the story had grown a tail: what he was supposed to have said, what the Superior was supposed to have answered, which chair Miusov was supposed to have broken. Our town loved a scandal the way a cat loves a saucer, and this one was rich cream, and I confess I heard my share of it eagerly enough at the time. The monastery said nothing at all, which the town found very dull of it. Only a few people asked the question that seems, now, to be the only question worth asking: what it must be like to be that old man — to have played the fool at sixty-five in front of the one audience on earth that would not laugh, and to ride home knowing it. Nobody asked him. He would have made a joke of it.

Now, three small things from the wreck of that afternoon. I ask you to keep each one, because every one of them matters later.

The first. Alyosha did not go home that day. But the command sat in him like a splinter, and the elder Zosima, when he heard of it, did not say what Alyosha hoped he would say. He said: "Perhaps your place will soon be out there, among them. Do not be afraid of the world, Alexei." It was the second time in two days the old man had frightened him.

The second. On the drive back to town, Fyodor, slumped and spent and suddenly honest the way such men are honest only when exhausted, said to Ivan, apropos of nothing: "You despise me. You have your mother's eyes and you despise me with them." Ivan said nothing at all. And the old man laughed, and patted his son's knee, and said something so strange that Ivan repeated it to Alyosha weeks later: "Good. Despise away. At least you are a Karamazov. It is the ones who love that frighten me."

And the third. That evening a servant brought cognac to Fyodor's study — a soft-footed young man with flat, careful eyes who had stood by the sideboard through a hundred of the old man's dinners, hearing everything, saying nothing. His name was Smerdyakov, and the time has come, in the next chapters, to tell you who he was. Our town, which laughed all winter about the dinner at the monastery, would give a great deal now never to have heard the name.
