The New Headmaster

After Anthony Trollope’s Barchester Towers (1857) — retold in old Korea.
Chapters one to 10 — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. This is Barchester Towers by Anthony Trollope, one of the best-loved comic novels in English — retold in simple English, and moved from an English cathedral town to a Confucian academy in old Korea. It is a comedy about a school, a scheming secretary, a kind old man, and the question of who really gives the orders. One thing to know: Trollope’s storyteller likes to talk to you directly. When the book says “you”, it means you. That is part of the fun.

Chapter One — The Two Couriers

Old Master Gwon was dying. The whole town knew it, and the town was a little ashamed of how interesting it found the news.

He had been headmaster of Crane Rock Academy for forty years. The academy stood on the hill above the river town of Baekcheon, behind a gate pavilion that carried a signboard in the king's own script, and it was old, and famous, and sure of itself. Great scholars had taught in its lecture hall. Boys came from three provinces to study there, and went away believing, most of them for the rest of their lives, that no other hill in the country stood quite so high. At the top of the hill was the shrine hall, where the tablets of the old sages were kept, and twice a year the whole county came up in its best clothes to bow before them. The headmaster of Crane Rock was, after the magistrate, the first man in that country. There were people in Baekcheon who would have told you, quietly, that after was not quite the right word.

For forty years Old Master Gwon had worn this greatness so lightly that no one had ever managed to be afraid of him. He corrected a boy's essay the way a grandfather mends a kite. He was now past eighty, and he was dying as he had lived: slowly, gently, and giving as little trouble as he could manage.

His son sat by the bed.

Scholar Gwon was fifty, broad, handsome, and used to being obeyed. He was the academy's senior lecturer, and for the last ten years, while his father faded, he had run Crane Rock in everything but name. He ran it well. He was proud, he was worldly, and underneath both he was a good man — though the goodness lay deep, and he did not encourage digging. Everyone in three provinces assumed that when the old master died, the son would be master in his place. The son assumed it too. He wanted it. I will not pretend for him that he did not, because he tried pretending it to himself that autumn, and it went badly.

For here was the difficulty. Crane Rock held a royal charter, and the master of a chartered academy is confirmed by the court. And the court, that autumn, was a pot coming to the boil.

You must know that the great men of the capital were divided, in those days, into two parties. The Old Rites men held that the country stood upright because of ceremony, poetry, music, and the correct performance of ancient things, and that a man who got the rites right could be trusted to get the rest right too. The Plain Learning men held that ceremony had grown fat, that poetry banquets were idleness in silk, and that what the country needed was granaries, arithmetic, and early rising. Each party was certain the other would be the ruin of the kingdom. Each party was, at any given moment, either in power or describing power as a burden it was well rid of.

The Chief State Councillor was an Old Rites man. He had studied under Old Master Gwon fifty years before, and he would have confirmed the son's appointment between his soup and his rice. But everyone knew the old councillor's government was falling. It had been falling all year, grandly and slowly, like a pine leaning further after every storm. The only question in the capital was when. And so the only question in Baekcheon — asked in the market, at the ferry, over fences, everywhere except out loud on the academy hill — was which would come down first: the government, or the old man.

There were two couriers on the road that autumn, though only one of them was a man on a horse. The first was whichever rider would someday gallop from the capital with word that the State Council had fallen. The second was death, coming up the hill to Crane Rock at its own pace, on no horse at all. Everything hung on which arrived first. And the man for whom it hung heaviest sat at his father's bedside, holding his father's hand, doing sums he despised himself for doing.

If his father died while the old council stood, the mastership was his. If the council fell first, the Plain Learning men would give Crane Rock — Crane Rock! — to some safe man of their own. He knew it. He could not stop knowing it. He would look at the old man's sleeping face and love him, wholly and truly; and inside that love, uninvited, the sum went on running: how many days has the council left? how many has he?

One evening it grew too heavy to carry. The old man was asleep. The lamp was low. Scholar Gwon looked at his father and caught the thought at last in the open — caught himself, for one bare instant, wishing the count of days would hurry. He had not wished it. He had almost wished it. A man learns something about himself at such a moment, and it is rarely a thing he wanted to know. He went down on the floor beside the bed and pressed his forehead to the boards, and asked — of Heaven, of his father, of whoever keeps the accounts of such things — to want nothing at all. He stayed there a long time. He was a proud man, as I have said, and I ask you to remember, whatever he does later in this story, that he once knelt on a cold floor and was honestly ashamed.

Most evenings Old Scholar Ahn came up the hill and sat in the corner of the sickroom with his geomungo. Ahn was the gentlest man in the county, and the dying master's oldest friend. He had once been keeper of the Hall of Rest, the old boatmen's house down by the river — how he came to give that up is a story this book will have to tell later, and it does the town no credit. He played low, old tunes, very softly, and the dying man liked the sound. When Ahn was troubled he had a habit of pressing the strings without sounding them, so that his fingers said what he did not. That autumn his fingers were seldom still.

The end came before dawn, on the first morning of frost. The old man woke, and his mind was clear, and he looked at his son for a while without speaking.

"You have been a good son to me," he said. "Better than I have told you."

Then he closed his eyes, and by the time the frost was off the roof tiles he was gone.

His son wept like a boy. And while he wept he wanted nothing — not the academy, not the appointment, not one thing in this world that he did not already have and was not already losing. If you would weigh a man, weigh him then. The sums came back later, because grief is deep but it is not busy, and the living have letters to write. Within the hour, one was written — to the Chief State Councillor, in the son's best hand, sealed with the academy's seal — and the academy's fastest rider went down the hill with it before the funeral white was hung on the gate.

The rider did everything right. He changed horses where he should, crossed both rivers without waiting, and reached the capital on the evening of the third day. He was two days too late. The State Council had fallen; the old councillor had surrendered his seals; the letter found him in a plain coat in a quiet house, a private old man. He read it twice, and grieved for his teacher, and wrote a kind answer — and a kind letter from a man who has just lost his power is worth exactly the paper it stands on.

The Plain Learning men now held the council, and they knew precisely what Crane Rock was: the brightest jewel in the other party's cap. They had no intention of leaving it there. Before the first snow they had put forward their man, and the appointment was sealed.

His name was Pyo. He was a capital scholar of respectable learning and no enemies — a thing most easily achieved by having no opinions, and Master Pyo had spent thirty careful years achieving it. He had held quiet posts and left them quietly. The new men wanted the academy in safe hands, and the safest hands, they reasoned, belong to a man who can be told what to do. In this they judged him rightly. They were mistaken only about who would be doing the telling.

For Master Pyo was married. The capital knew Madam Pyo. The capital had been making a certain joke about her for twenty years — though never twice in her hearing.

There would also come a secretary: a smooth young scholar named Seol, who had fixed himself to Master Pyo's fortunes the way a burr fixes itself to a traveller, and who will get all the attention he deserves in this book, which is more than he ever deserved elsewhere. The character of his name, he liked to explain, meant snow. People who had heard him talk for a whole evening privately preferred another character, which sounds just the same, and means tongue.

Scholar Gwon bore the news as a proud man bears such things: correctly. He praised the wisdom of the court in the tone a man uses to praise the weather during a flood. Only once, standing with Old Scholar Ahn under the bare gingko by the gate, did he say what he meant.

"Forty years," he said. "And they hand it to a man who has never kept a school in his life."

"It is still your father's academy," said Ahn gently. "A signboard cannot change what the hill is."

"No," said Scholar Gwon, looking up at the gate his grandfather had repaired and his father had walked under every morning for forty years. "But we shall see what it can change."

The new master would come in the spring, when the roads opened. Baekcheon settled in to wait, and told itself it was waiting for a headmaster. In fact it was waiting for three people, and the headmaster was the least of them.

Chapter Two — The New Master Comes

They buried Old Master Gwon on the east shoulder of the hill, where he could keep an eye on the academy, and the whole county walked up behind him. Then winter closed the roads. Baekcheon was left alone with its grief and its curiosity, which is an uncomfortable pair to spend a winter with.

Through those months Scholar Gwon ran the academy, because somebody had to, and he always had. He kept the winter session. He read the students' essays. He settled the bursar's accounts, the cook's quarrel, and the matter of the leaking store-room roof. He did, in short, the whole work of the master of Crane Rock. He did it at the master's desk, in the master's room. He did it excellently, and he did it for the last time. Every man on the hill knew all four of those things. Nobody said one word. There is a kind of tact that is crueller than rudeness.

The new master came up from the capital in the third month, when the plum blossom was out along the river road, and Baekcheon turned out to watch. It watched respectfully, in its clean clothes, the way a town watches anything it has already decided to have opinions about.

First came a rider carrying the letters of appointment in a lacquered box. Then came Master Pyo's palanquin. Then came Madam Pyo's palanquin, following her husband's — which is the correct place for a wife's palanquin, and the last time in this history that anything belonging to Madam Pyo came second. Then a cart of books. Then three carts of household goods. Then came a fourth cart, packed tight with wooden crates. Within a day the town knew what was in them, because ferries and markets keep no secrets. The crates held printed tracts: advice, in large clear characters, on frugality, plain food, early rising, and the proper government of a household. There were a great many crates.

Master Pyo stepped down at the gate pavilion. He turned out to be a neat, grey, worried man of about sixty, with a habit of glancing to his left before he spoke. He looked up at the royal signboard for a long moment, and said, at last, that it was very fine. No one could quarrel with this. That was the point of it. In thirty years of public life, Master Pyo had never once said a thing that anyone could quarrel with. He did not mean to begin on a strange hill.

The next morning he was installed. He bowed in the shrine hall, before the tablets of the sages — correctly, unhurriedly, and with real reverence. The old lecturers watched the way hawks watch. They could not find a fault anywhere. Whatever else was going to be said about the new master, and a great deal was, the man knew how to bow. If the dead keep watch from the east shoulder of the hill, Old Master Gwon saw the thing done properly. One hopes it comforted him.

On the third day the new master received his lecturers in the outer hall, and made a speech. It was a good speech, in the sense that there was nothing in it. He honored the memory of Old Master Gwon. He trusted in the guidance of the sages. He hoped to be worthy of the hill. The lecturers listened and bowed. They had just begun to feel something dangerously like relief. Then Master Pyo paused, and glanced to his left.

To his left was the wall of the inner room, and the paper door in that wall stood open exactly one inch.

From behind the door there came a small, dry cough. It was not a cough that had anything to do with a throat.

"There will also," said Master Pyo, "be improvements."

He did not say what they would be. Perhaps he did not yet know. The lecturers walked down from the hall in perfect silence. Lecturers notice everything. Every man of them had noticed the door. Not one of them ever mentioned it to another. Within the month, the whole county knew.

The visiting that the master did not do, the secretary did. In his first week, Scholar Seol called upon every lecturer at Crane Rock, in strict order of seniority. He was charming to each of them for exactly as long as courtesy required, and then a little longer. He had one great trick, and it never failed him: he complimented every man on the one thing that man was proudest of and vainest about. He praised old Lecturer Heo's commentary on the Odes, which no one living had read. He praised the bursar's memory, the porter's gate, and the cook's pickled radish. This looks like luck. It is not luck. It is research, and Seol had done his research the way other men do their mourning: thoroughly.

He bowed a shade too deeply, and came up a shade too slowly. He handed papers with both hands, as a courteous man should, and the papers came away from his hands, somehow, very slightly damp. He smiled a great deal. He was young, he was smooth, he was undoubtedly clever. And when he had gone, the skin on the back of your neck went on being aware of him for some minutes. The lecturers compared their impressions afterwards, and made a curious finding. Each man had liked Seol a little less than the man before him had. There were eleven lecturers. It added up.

Scholar Gwon paid his call of courtesy on the fifth day, in his best coat and his best temper, both of them stiff. He had prepared himself for an enemy. He found, instead, a mild grey man who agreed with him.

Gwon praised the traditions of the academy. Master Pyo agreed. Gwon observed that in old institutions, sudden change was dangerous. Master Pyo agreed with real feeling; he had a horror of sudden change. Then he glanced to his left, where the door stood open its inch. And after a short delay — the way thunder follows lightning — he added that of course, where improvement was needed, improvement must come.

Scholar Gwon walked down the hill more troubled than any quarrel would have left him. You can wrestle a man. Nobody has ever wrestled fog.

Old Scholar Ahn made his visit too, carrying a jar of quince preserve. The old boatmen at the Hall of Rest had sent it up for the new master. Ahn had told them it would be a kind thing to do, and the twelve of them would have carried it up the hill in procession if he had let them. Master Pyo was more at ease with Ahn than with anyone he had yet met in Baekcheon. Frightened men always were. He kept him a full hour, talking about nothing, gratefully. When Ahn left at last, Master Pyo stood in the doorway looking after him, like a man watching the last boat of the evening go out.

That same week, the first of the improvements arrived. It was posted at the gatehouse in the master's own hand: from this day, the academy's gate would be barred at dusk.

It was a small thing, as first things are. The students grumbled for a day and invented a window. The old porter had kept that gate for thirty years, on the ancient principle that rules are for strangers. He read the notice twice. Then he looked up the hill toward the master's residence — toward the inner court of it — and said nothing at all, thoughtfully.

And now the spring rite was coming: the great day of the academy's year, when the whole county climbs the hill to bow in the shrine court. By forty years of custom, when the ceremony is done, the master gives the lecture. The lecturers waited for that lecture the way farmers wait for the first warm day. It would tell them, at last, what kind of year they were in.

Then the order of the day was posted, and the lecture was not assigned to Master Pyo at all. After the rites, the notice said, the discourse would be delivered by the master's secretary: Scholar Seol.

The academy read the notice before breakfast. The town had it by noon, and the county by nightfall. Nothing so interesting had happened in Baekcheon since the winter the river froze over in a single night. That was forty years ago, and fewer people had been angry.

Chapter Three — The Lecture

The spring rite at Crane Rock was old, and it was beautiful. This book intends to laugh at a great many things on that hill. It is not going to laugh at this one.

At first light the county began to climb: farmers in clean white clothes, boatmen up from the river, mothers with children scrubbed into astonishment. Old men made this climb twice a year, and meant to go on making it until the hill itself told them to stop. The shrine court had been swept until it shone. In the hall, in the smell of cedar and incense, stood the tablets of the old sages. Before them, slowly, with music, the ancient offerings were made, exactly as they had been made every spring for two hundred years. Master Pyo performed his part correctly and with real reverence; bowing, as we have said, was the thing he could truly do. If you had stood at the back of the court among the farmers, you would have felt the county's heart turn over quietly in its proper place. You would have said the academy had found itself a worthy master.

Then the ceremony ended, and mats were laid on the stone for the discourse. And we may stop being solemn, because Scholar Seol has risen to his feet.

He began beautifully. No one ever denied that Seol could speak. It was the one gift Heaven had honestly given him, and he kept it polished. His voice was low, clear, and humble, and it reached the last row of the court without ever seeming to try. He praised the hill. He praised the spring. He praised the late Old Master Gwon, at length, and with perfect sincerity — in the manner of a man praising last year's harvest, as a fine thing, and a finished one.

Then he took up his text. He took the saying of the Master, the one every man in that court had known since boyhood. Where substance outweighs ornament, we get a rough countryman. Where ornament outweighs substance, we get a mere clerk of forms. Only where the two are balanced do we find the gentleman. It is a great saying, and it is built like a pair of scales. Scholar Seol read out one pan of the scales. The other he set down, very quietly, on the ground behind him.

Ornament, said Scholar Seol, had outweighed substance. He said it generally at first, and sorrowfully — a fault of the whole age, and no one's in particular. Then he became less general. Poetry banquets, he said, and paused. Poetry banquets were idleness dressed in silk. Music was sugar: pleasant, and no man was ever fed by it. And then he came to the spring poetry contest of Crane Rock. The contest had been held on this very hill for eighty years. It was the pride of the academy — the one day of the year when the county's sons competed at making something beautiful. The spring poetry contest, said Scholar Seol, gently, regretfully, might be thought by serious men an ornament the age could no longer afford. What the times asked of scholars was granaries, account books, and early rising. And those who clung to the old ways — his voice here grew tender as a nurse's — clung, perhaps, not to their fathers' virtue, but to their own comfort in their fathers' clothes.

All of this was said at the one gathering of the year where no man could answer it. You do not shout at the spring rite. You do not stand up in the shrine court, before the tablets and the county and the children, and quarrel. Decorum held the whole old party pinned in their seats like beetles on a collector's card. And the smoothest thing in the whole smooth performance was this: Seol knew it. He had counted on it. And he never once, while he used it, let his voice climb out of the register of humility.

Old Lecturer Heo's knuckles went slowly white around his book. The bursar stared at the ground between his feet, as if he had dropped something precious into it. Scholar Gwon sat with his eyes fixed on the great roof beam of the shrine hall — the beam his grandfather had raised. His face was so calm that men on the far side of the court found themselves watching it instead of the speaker, the way one watches a kettle.

Seol finished with modesty, thanked the hill for its patience, and sat down.

The county went down the mountain talking, which is what counties are for. The party of the old ways went down in silence. It was the kind of silence that would have frightened a wiser man than the one who had caused it.

They gathered in the gatehouse without anyone having called a meeting; such meetings call themselves. Old Scholar Ahn was there too, deeply unhappy. He was casting about for the kindest available explanation, which was his way of looking for his hat.

"He is young," Ahn offered at last.

"So is a mosquito," said Scholar Gwon.

He stood in the middle of the gatehouse floor, and the room went quiet around him.

"We were told this morning," he said, "on our own hill, at our fathers' rite, over our fathers' tablets, that our fathers were idle men in silk. Very well. It was said where it could not be answered. It will be answered where it can."

That night the lamps burned late in Scholar Gwon's house, and letters went out of it. Several went to the old families of the county. One went a great deal further — to the capital, to a great old man of the national academy who had been his father's first famous pupil. The old man was past fighting anybody himself. But he had a lifetime's weight to lend, and a long memory for where to lend it. The letter was a long one, and most of it does not concern us. But one sentence in it mattered more than all the rest of the ink together, and the sentence was this: Send me Scholar Mun.

Who Scholar Mun was, and what he was worth, this book will show in its proper place. It is enough to say here what happened when the old man in the capital came to that sentence. He sat back, and stroked his beard, and said "Ah." It was the "Ah" of a man watching a good player, at long last, pick up his strongest stone.

At the master's residence that evening, it was agreed that the day had gone extremely well.

"It went well," said Madam Pyo.

"It went well," said Master Pyo, after a short delay.

Madam Pyo had a list, and the spring poetry contest stood near the top of it. She retired that night in the deep calm of a woman who has seen her duty done by proxy, and found the proxy satisfactory.

The proxy, alone in his own room, was also pleased with himself. But his pleasure was of a more professional kind. While he spoke, Seol had watched the court the way a boatman watches water. The white knuckles and the stone faces he had expected. Those belonged to the old men, and the old men were the past. What interested him were three or four of the younger lecturers, seated low in their rows, who had not looked angry at all. They had looked thoughtful. Seol wrote their names on a slip of paper before he slept. It took him some time to sleep, because he was happy. A war needs two parties. He had spent the morning recruiting his.

So Crane Rock had received a new master in the winter, and by the middle of spring it had received a war. Baekcheon, which had been bored since the funeral, was deeply, guiltily grateful.

Chapter Four — The Family from the Lake

While the men were declaring war, Madam Pyo was reading the account books.

She read account books the way other women read letters from home: closely, with feeling, and twice. Within a month of her arrival she knew the academy's accounts better than the bursar knew them. The bursar discovered this in a single interview, conducted through a paper door open one inch. The interview took several years off the far end of his life. And in the third ledger, under her evening lamp, Madam Pyo found the entry she had been born to find.

It was a stipend, marked out for a visiting lecturer. It had been paid in full, every year, for twelve years. And in twelve years, the lecturer it maintained had visited the academy exactly never.

The lecturer's name was Scholar Im, and he was living two hundred li away, beside a lake.

He had gone to the lake twelve years before, for his health. His chest was weak. This was the family's official doctrine. Scholar Im had published it himself in the year he discovered the lake, and nobody had examined it since — least of all a physician. The lake air had certainly agreed with him. In twelve years, the weak chest had not interrupted his fishing for one single morning. He fished at dawn. He studied his collection of inkstones in the afternoon. In the evening he rested from both. He was a large, calm, handsome old man with the most magnificent beard in three provinces. And he had discovered early the great secret of life: a man who does absolutely nothing, slowly enough, and with enough dignity, will generally be described as wise.

Madam Pyo's letter went out over the master's name and under the master's seal. It observed that the age of ornament was over. It invited Scholar Im to resume his duties at Crane Rock in person, or else to resign his stipend — whichever his health advised. It was a beautifully made letter. It was courteous in every line, and there was no soft place in it anywhere, like a well-built wall.

At the lake, Scholar Im read it twice, and passed it without comment to his eldest daughter. This was what he did with everything that looked like work.

Im Cho-hui was thirty-five and unmarried. She had kept the family's accounts, appearances, and secrets since she was nineteen. She was quick, warm, and deeply capable. She was honest in everything — except on her family's behalf, where she was a diplomat first, and took a diplomat's view of facts. She read the letter once and understood the whole of it: the stipend was the household's rice. "We are going," she said. And they went, the entire household. The alternative to going was economy, and no Im had practiced economy in living memory.

Her brother took the news beautifully. He took all news beautifully. Im Beom was thirty-two, and the most agreeable failure in the country. He had attempted, so far: the military examination, at which he fell off the horse; the practice of medicine, in which his patients showed a strong instinct for recovery, exercised chiefly by staying away; and the Daoist arts of long life, which he gave up because — the phrase is his own — it took too long. There had also been a scheme for growing ginseng. The capital still discusses that scheme, in low voices, the way men discuss a flood. Each of these he had taken up with his whole heart, for a season. Each he had laid down without one hour of bitterness, which is, if you consider it, a kind of achievement too. Everybody liked him. He owed money in four provinces, all of it to people who liked him. Told that the family was moving to Baekcheon, he said it was the best of news. He had been wanting a change of air. Also, he understood there was money in mulberries.

And then there was the second daughter.

Everything about Im Wol-ha arrived ahead of her: the story, the warnings, and the calling card. The card was a small work of art on thick paper, and it announced Lady Wol-ha of the Jade Terrace. Where the Jade Terrace was, nobody knew. Underneath, in smaller characters, the card mentioned that her infant daughter descended from the royal house of Silla. No one had ever verified this. No one had ever volunteered.

The story, told plainly — and she never told it plainly — was this. At eighteen she had been the beauty of the eastern provinces. Against every advice offered by every person who loved her, she married a garrison officer of splendid looks and no character whatever, and went north with him to his posting. Four years later she came home. She came home with an infant daughter, and without her husband. And she came home carried; for her legs, which had walked her into that marriage, did not come back from it. Her family asked once what had happened. The look she gave them lasted the family a generation. "There was an accident," she said. The subject closed. It has stayed closed, and it will stay closed in this book as well.

Another woman would have gone into a back room, and the world would have arranged to pity her. Wol-ha considered pity, as a career, for the better part of one afternoon, and declined the position. Heaven had taken her legs. Very well: Heaven, being just, must owe her everything else, and she had spent the ten years since collecting the debt. Since she could not walk, she would never again be seen attempting anything so ordinary. She received the world reclining, on a long, low couch of dark wood and brass. Her brother had designed the couch in one of his enthusiasms. It was agreed on all sides to be the single complete success of Beom's career. She lay arranged among her cushions like the best line of a poem; and the world, which ought to have known better, came to be received. The men of the world especially. She collected men's hearts the way her father collected inkstones: expertly, one at a time, with real appreciation, and with no intention of ever using them for anything.

The family came into Baekcheon on a market day. Cho-hui had planned for a quiet one; Wol-ha had had a private word with the bearers. The couch came up the whole length of the market street at shoulder height, through the dried fish and the brassware and the spring onions. Baekcheon stopped trading. She was carried past perfectly still, faintly smiling, looking at no one, missing nothing. By evening the town had reached its verdict, up and down every lane. The verdict was that Baekcheon had never seen anything like her in its life, and could not wait to see her again.

They took the old magistrate's house by the willows, at the foot of the academy hill. On the first evening, Scholar Im walked down to look at the river. He looked at it for a long while. Then he delivered his one public comment upon their new life.

"The fishing," he said, "appears adequate."

Up on the hill, Madam Pyo received the news of the family's arrival with grim satisfaction. The idle had been summoned, and the idle had come. Let the county take note that the age of ornament was over. She had brought the Ims to Baekcheon to be an example. In this she had judged rightly, as she generally did. An example is precisely what they were going to be. Her mistake was only in the matter of what the lesson would turn out to be about — and she had a busy season in front of her before she discovered it.

For Madam Pyo was, at that moment, planning a reception. There was to be a housewarming at the master's residence, for the lecturers, the magistrate, and every respectable family in the county. It would open her husband's rule. The word the town used privately was not "husband's," and it was not quite "rule," but the town knew what it meant. The invitations went down the hill on thick, plain, frugal paper. At the willow house, Cho-hui read theirs and accepted for the entire family, in her best hand, before her father could think of a chest complaint.

The housewarming was set for the first warm evening of the fifth month. It deserves a chapter of its own. It is going to need one.

Chapter Five — The Housewarming

The reception was to be plain. The invitations said so. The food said so. The courtyard, swept and bare, said so twice. Madam Pyo intended the evening as a lesson: the master's house, under new rule, would show the county what serious living looked like.

In one thing only did the hostess step aside from her own doctrine. For the honor of the master's house — the phrase was hers — she wore her grandmother's ceremonial skirt. It was deep blue silk, two hundred years old, embroidered at the hem with a line of flying cranes. It was the one splendid thing she owned, and she wore it the way a general wears his one medal. It is important that you know about the skirt. The skirt is going to matter.

The county came. The county would not have missed it for a flood. The magistrate came. The lecturers came, all eleven. Scholar Gwon came in his stiffest coat, for a war does not excuse a man from his enemy's parties; in Baekcheon it rather requires them. And Old Scholar Ahn came, bringing his daughter.

About the daughter, a little must be said now, because a great deal will have to be said later. Ahn Yeon-u was twenty-four. Three years before, she had married a young censor from the capital — a sharp, honest, reforming young man. How the daughter of the gentlest man in the county came to marry that man, of all men, is a story tangled up with her father's, and it will be told in its place. He had died of a fever within two years of the wedding. He left her a baby son, and, from his merchant grandfather, a fortune. She had come home to her father's house with the baby; and Baekcheon approved of her grief, and was frankly interested in the fortune. She was quiet. She watched things. What she watched, she generally saw.

Scholar Seol had, of course, already learned the size of the fortune, down to the last bag of rice. Learning such figures was one of his gifts. At the reception he contrived to be seated where he could be useful to her, and he was useful all evening — usefully near, usefully attentive, usefully grave about the great wrong once done to her father, of which he promised to say more when the company was quieter. The widow thanked him. She was fair-minded; and fair-mindedness, in this world, is a door left unlatched.

The Im family arrived one hour late. Cho-hui had fought hard to prevent this. Wol-ha had personally arranged it.

They came in procession: Scholar Im, serene and magnificent; Cho-hui, apologizing beautifully to exactly the right people; Beom, delighted with everyone he saw; and then, through the gate, at shoulder height, the couch.

A reclining lady presents a seating problem that the book of rites does not directly address. The stewards conferred in whispers. The hostess was sent for. Before any ruling could arrive, Wol-ha settled the matter herself, with one small motion of one small hand: the bearers set the couch down in the one spot in the courtyard from which everything could be seen, and past which everyone must walk. It was the spot the hostess had reserved, in her mind, for herself. But there is no ceremony for removing a lady who cannot walk. Wol-ha's whole public life was built upon that fact.

The evening then did what evenings do. Master Pyo drifted to the couch and was asked gentle questions, and heard his answers agreed with before he had finished giving them, and found this restful beyond anything in his recent life. Beom told the magistrate about mulberries. The lecturers ate plain food and counted the cranes on the hostess's hem. And Seol came to the couch to detach the master from it, and stayed to be examined himself. Wol-ha looked at him the way a jeweler looks at a stone that is offered as jade. She asked him three soft questions. In the asking, she learned more about him than Madam Pyo had learned in two years, and what she learned amused her. She decided to keep him.

It happened at the turning of the evening.

Madam Pyo rose to lead the ladies in — and at that same moment Wol-ha, wanting a better view of the rising moon, signalled her bearers to shift the couch. The couch swung. The brass corner-fitting — her brother's one triumph — caught the ancient blue silk at the hem, and held it, and the hostess walked on.

The sound was not loud. There are sounds that do not need to be loud. A hand's-breadth of two-hundred-year-old embroidery came away, and three flying cranes stayed behind on the brass hook, and Madam Pyo stood in the middle of her own courtyard in a torn skirt.

"The silk!" said Madam Pyo. And then, in a voice the whole county heard and never forgot: "My silk!"

Master Pyo arrived at a hover, and hovered. Beom was first to actually help, kneeling at once, gathering the hem with real tenderness, and offering, as he worked, the name of a man in the capital who mended silk so finely that you could never find the seam. He did not add that he owed this man money. Even Beom had evenings of judgment. And Wol-ha apologized. She apologized slowly, beautifully, and at length, with her head tilted; and her apology was somehow, unmistakably, worse than the accident.

Madam Pyo withdrew to change. The party, deprived of its hostess, relaxed so noticeably that the relaxation itself was a kind of comment. When she returned — in plain grey now, plainness worn as armor — she found her reception had become a warm, laughing, happy thing that no longer belonged to her. Her husband was at the couch. Her secretary was at the couch. Half the county was within hearing of the couch, and the couch was holding court.

She stood at the edge of her own party, and opened, in her heart, a ledger. Into it she entered the entire family Im, in fresh ink, under an old heading.

The county walked home under the moon, and agreed that it was the best evening the hill had given in years. "Though not," as the magistrate's wife observed, "for the reasons the hostess would have chosen."

And Scholar Seol walked back to his rooms having done, without noticing it, the first careless thing of his careful career. He had let the mistress of the house watch him enjoy himself, for one hour too long, at the foot of the couch. Careers have died of an hour. His had begun to.

Chapter Six — The Widow's Tea

Baekcheon spent three days discussing the skirt. Then it needed something new, and Scholar Seol gave it something new. He began calling at Old Scholar Ahn's house in town.

Before we watch him do it, you must know a little more about the widow, and about the husband she had lost.

Her husband had been a censor: a young man paid a small salary to point out large faults. He had been good at it — honest, fearless, and inconvenient, which are the three qualities the post requires and the three least likely to make a man rich. It was he, four years earlier, who had written a famous petition about the Hall of Rest — the petition that cost her father the keepership. That story is coming in the next chapter. Keep it in mind.

How the daughter of the gentlest man in the county came to marry the man who had, with entire honesty, wrecked him — that is one of those things that happen because people are people. The short version is that the young censor came to apologize, and stayed to be forgiven, and the forgiving got out of hand. Ahn had loved him, which surprised nobody who knew Ahn and astonished everybody else. Between two honest men, a great public wrong can shrink, in time, into a small family joke. Then the young man died of a fever in the second winter of the marriage, and the joke went quiet, and the widow came home to her father's house with the baby.

That was the household Seol began to visit.

He came about nothing. He was scrupulous about that. He came to return a borrowed book; he came with a question about calligraphy; he came with a message from the master that any servant could have carried. He admired the baby, at a safe distance, in measured terms. And on the third visit, over tea, he lowered his voice, and spoke of the Hall of Rest.

A wrong had been done to her father, said Seol. All Baekcheon knew it, and all Baekcheon had let it stand. But times had changed on the hill. Old wrongs could be righted under new rule. He himself — he said this with his eyes down, a man embarrassed by his own influence — was not without a voice in certain rooms, and he intended to spend that voice where honor pointed. He asked for nothing. He promised nothing exact. He was grave, and warm, and his hands, as he took the teacup, were as damp as ever.

The widow did not much like him. She was ashamed of the not-liking, because here was a man trying to restore her father, and what kind of daughter counts the dampness of such a man's hands? So she thanked him with both hands and with real feeling — the feeling being for her father, though a listening man could take it however he pleased. Seol took it however he pleased.

Fair-mindedness, as we have said, is a door left unlatched. Seol could smell an unlatched door through a stone wall.

When he had gone, the house was quiet a while. Old Scholar Ahn sat with his geomungo across his knees.

"He is very kind," said the widow, in the voice of a woman testing a plank before she steps on it.

"Yes," said her father. His fingers were on the strings, pressing them, one after another, without making any sound at all.

By the end of the week, Scholar Gwon knew of the visits, because his wife was the widow's elder sister, and sisters write letters. His roar could be heard through two walls. Seol! In that parlor! Drinking that tea! Sitting across from that fortune with his damp hands folded! The elder sister wrote back counselling calm. Scholar Gwon dictated three replies and sent the fourth. This was not yet the family tribunal — that comes later, and it will be worse — but the alarm had been lit, and alarms, once lit in that family, were never wasted.

And here, reader, before the rumor gets ahead of the facts, this storyteller will keep the promise made at the start of our journey: no secrets between us. So let it be said now, plainly. Yeon-u will not marry Scholar Seol. She was never, for one single hour, in any danger of marrying Scholar Seol. Her family will come to believe otherwise; they will believe it with great energy and at great length; and you and I, knowing better, will be able to enjoy their suffering in comfort, which is one of the chief pleasures of reading a book instead of living in one.

But to understand what Seol was really selling in that parlor, you must first understand the thing itself — the Hall of Rest, the best thing in Baekcheon, and the scene of the most reasonable injustice ever done there.

That story now. It deserves a chapter, and it has earned one.

Chapter Seven — The Hall of Rest

Three hundred years ago there was a ferryman at Baekcheon called Hong.

He began with one boat. He ended with the ferry rights, the fish market, and a warehouse on each bank. He grew rich the way rivers grow: quietly, and by never stopping. And when he died, having no sons, he left the whole of it in trust. There was to be a house by the water, and in the house were to live twelve old boatmen of Baekcheon — men worn out by the river, with no family to keep them — fed and warmed to the end of their days. The academy was to steward the trust, and appoint a keeper, who would live beside the old men and have a stipend for his trouble.

For three hundred years there were always twelve old men, because the river never stops making them. And the rents of the trust lands grew, as rents do; and the trouble of pouring tea for twelve old men stayed the same size; and so the keeper's stipend swelled, century by century, into a sum that nobody had ever exactly decided and everybody had stopped noticing. By the time of our story, the keeper of the Hall of Rest was paid rather better than a county magistrate — for kindness, in effect, at the rate of statesmanship.

Old Master Gwon had given the post to his friend Ahn, and for nine years Ahn was the best keeper the Hall had ever had. He did not merely pour the tea. He knew twelve life stories by heart, and the names of the boats in them. He wrote letters for men whose hands had gone stiff. And in the evenings he played the geomungo; and the old men, who had spent their whole lives being shouted at over water, sat in the sound like men sitting in the sun.

Then the young censor wrote his petition, and the petition was arithmetic. Ferryman Hong had left his fortune for old boatmen. The old boatmen received their rice and their firewood, as always. Where, asked the petition, does the rest go? — and it printed the rest, in figures, and the figures were large.

The petition was honest. That was the terrible thing about it. Ahn read it; and being Ahn, he checked the arithmetic himself; and the arithmetic was correct. Everyone told him it meant nothing. The old master told him the stipend was the custom of three hundred years. Scholar Gwon told him, at volume, that a gentleman does not resign because a boy in the capital can divide. The young censor himself, appalled at where his own honesty had landed, half-retracted, and was gently thanked, and got nowhere. Ahn resigned.

"The money was wrong," he said. "It was wrong while I had it. It can be right for the next man."

Then he apologized to the twelve old men, as if he had wronged them; and moved his geomungo into town; and in due course gave his daughter in marriage to the man who had cost him everything — which tells you most of what this book will ever need you to know about Old Scholar Ahn.

The post had stood empty ever since. The old master could never bring himself to fill it while his friend lived in town on half of nothing. The trust drifted; the stipend piled up unclaimed; the twelve old men poured their own tea, and were poorer company for one another than they had been, for the music was gone.

Now there was new rule on the hill; and the keepership of the Hall of Rest was suddenly the ripest plum in the county; and two hands reached for it at once.

Madam Pyo had a candidate. His name was Scholar Ma, of Poor Field village: a country teacher, poor as a winter field, honest as noon, and the father of fourteen children. Fourteen. The number is counted nightly by Mrs. Ma, on her fingers, by lamplight, and it always comes out the same. The Ma family's need was real, and it was enormous, and Madam Pyo's patronage of it was perfectly sincere. Let no one say she did no good. She did good the way she did everything: on purpose, at volume, and with the account kept.

Seol had a different candidate. His candidate was the old one.

He went to the house in town, and sat again in the parlor, and laid the restoration before Ahn like a gift on a tray. The Hall could be his again. The wrong could be righted. Only — and here Seol's voice took on its softest regret — new rule brought new duties. The keeper would read aloud to the old men each morning from the printed tracts, for their improvement. The keeper would submit his accounts each week to the master's secretary — that is, to Seol. And the evenings of music — Seol grieved over this most of all, with his hand upon his heart — the evenings of music would naturally be given up. These were serious times. Music, he did not need to add, was sugar.

There was a long quiet in the parlor. Ahn's fingers found the strings of the geomungo across his knees, and pressed them, and made no sound.

"I am too old to learn new songs," he said at last, "and too fond of the old ones."

He did not say no. He said he would think. Thinking, for Ahn, was a form of suffering, and Seol went down the steps well pleased with the suffering he had arranged.

And that same afternoon — the very same, the walk between the two conversations being four streets — Seol told the widow, gravely and warmly, that he was fighting to restore her father freely, without one condition attached, as honor demanded; though certain persons on the hill — he did not name the inner room, and his eyes named it for him — certain persons wished to hang chains on the gift. He asked her to say nothing yet. Hope, he said, should not be woken until it can be fed. The widow, who had watched her father's soundless fingers for four years, could have wept; and very nearly liked him.

So there were now two promises abroad in Baekcheon that could not both be kept, and both of them wore Seol's face. A man may ride two horses while the horses walk.

Baekcheon's horses were about to trot.

Chapter Eight — Scholar Mun

Scholar Gwon was losing his war, and knew it, and did the one wise thing an angry man can do. He sent for help.

Help had a name, and the name was Mun. You will want to know him, reader, for he matters more than anyone we have met so far except the widow; and this storyteller, who has promised you no suspense, will say plainly why he matters. But first, the man.

Scholar Mun was forty years old, and famous, in the way scholars are famous: three provinces knew his name, and no innkeeper did. He held a lectureship at the national academy in the capital. He had written a commentary that older men quoted and younger men fought about. He could unpick a bad argument the way a sailor unpicks a knot — patiently, without cutting anything. And he was, at forty, still unmarried; still poor, because he had never once in his life asked for anything at the right moment; and still, in all matters that lay outside a book, approximately eleven years old.

He and Gwon had been students together. They had shared a room, a lamp, and thirty years of arguing, which between two men of that sort is friendship of the strongest kind.

One thing more you must know about him, because it is the key to the whole man. At twenty-five, Mun had nearly left the road. There was in those days a teacher in the far south — a brilliant, dangerous, warm-hearted man — who taught that the sages' truth was not to be found in books at all, but in the heart, directly, as fire is found in flint. The orthodox called this heresy, and worse, foreign heresy. Young Mun, drowning in commentaries, had felt that teaching pull at him like a river current. He had packed, in fact, to go south. What un-packed him — whether argument, or fear, or one long night he never described — he told no one. But he came back to the orthodox road the way a man comes back from a cliff edge: permanently, and without ever again mocking those who fall. It left a mark on him, that near-fall. It left him gentle where zealots are hard, and hard where comfortable men are soft. And it left him with a private conviction, never spoken: that he had used up his life's one great feeling on a doctrine, at twenty-five, and would not be asked for another.

He was wrong about that. This is the part the storyteller promised to say plainly: he was wrong.

Gwon's letter reached him in the capital in early summer. It was seven pages long. Its argument may be shortened without loss: a damp-handed nobody is eating my county, come at once. Mun read it twice, and smiled once. Then he began — being Mun — to weigh the question on its merits, as if merits were what his friend had written seven pages about.

But it happened that the mountain school above Baekcheon stood vacant. It was the smallest post in the academy's gift: a schoolhouse on the high road, one room for teaching and one for living, a stipend that a careful man could nearly live on, and a roof with opinions of its own about rain. To the county it was nothing. To Gwon it was a border fort: whoever held the mountain school held a seat, however small, in the academy's councils, and a reason to be in Baekcheon for the fighting season. He begged Mun to take it.

And Mun — famous Mun, capital Mun, who had refused fatter posts the way other men refuse bad fish — accepted. The capital was astonished. The capital said so, at length, in letters. Mun answered none of them. The truth, which he did not examine closely, was that he was forty, and tired of winning arguments in rooms where nothing was at stake. And something in the phrase one room for teaching had sounded, to a man who owned four thousand books and no chairs, like rest.

So he came down in the sixth month, with his books in three carts and his belongings in one basket, and took possession of the mountain school. He spent his first afternoon in it placing basins under the leaks, with the concentration of a man annotating a text. The roof, he wrote to a friend, promised to keep him humble. It kept its promise all that summer.

Scholar Gwon gave a dinner to welcome him. And at that dinner our two people met, and it went as badly as a meeting can go without anyone raising a voice.

It was not the widow's fault. It was not quite Mun's either. The fault, if we must lodge it somewhere, belongs to the family alarm, which had been ringing for a month by then; for this dinner fell, you will note, in the very thick of the Seol business — the visits, the tea, the letters between sisters. The house was full of the suspicion nobody spoke. When Yeon-u was announced, Gwon's wife glanced at her husband. Gwon arranged his face the way large men do, which is to say visibly. And the widow, who watched things, saw it all, and understood that in her sister's house she was now a question. She sat down at that table armored, therefore, from the first cup.

And Mun? Mun had been told about her. Gwon, in the manner of friends, had supplied everything a man least needs before meeting a woman: the fortune, in figures; the dead husband; and the family fear, presented as settled fact, that she was halfway to being caught by the worst man in the province. Mun despised gossip and had memorized every word of it.

So when they were seated near each other, he was stiff. Stiffness, in a shy scholar, has a way of dressing itself as judgment. He asked her one question about the capital and did not follow her answer, because he was composing his next remark, which is a thing that happens to men who read too much. She concluded he was proud. He quoted, in the general conversation, a line about widows of antiquity — meaning nothing by it, meaning it for Gwon, meaning it about a point of ritual scholarship — and heard it land, one beat too late, as a remark. The table went briefly and horribly attentive. The widow smiled the smile she kept for public use, and asked him whether the mountain roof still leaked. Mun, wretched, said that it did. She said she was glad the school had at last a master who could weather it. No one at the table could have sworn whether that was kindness or a knife. The widow herself could not have sworn it.

They parted with perfect courtesy, each certain of having been disliked.

He walked home up the mountain road in the dark, calling himself, in the classical language, several kinds of fool. She rode home thinking that the famous Scholar Mun might at least have had the manners of the unfamous kind. Her father alone, who had sat quietly at the foot of the table all evening, formed a different view of the matter; but Ahn's views ripened slowly, and he took this one home with him, and said nothing, and pressed no strings.

And here ends the first part of our story. The board is set; it is worth one look before the play. On the hill, the inner room rules, and the paper door has nearly given up closing. In the town, two promises walk the streets wearing one face. At Poor Field, fourteen children are counted nightly by lamplight. By the river, twelve old men pour their own tea, and the music is still gone. And on the mountain, under a mended-enough roof, sits the one man in this book who wants nothing — which, in Baekcheon, in that summer, made him the most dangerous piece on the board.

The rains were ending. The horses, as promised, were about to trot.

Chapter Nine — Two Promises

Master Pyo had now been headmaster of Crane Rock Academy for half a year, and it is time to say, in fairness, what the post had given him. It had given him a signboard with royal calligraphy over his gate. It had given him a residence with inner and outer courts, and eleven lecturers who bowed to him. And it had given him not one single hour, day or night, in which he was master of anything.

He minded this less than you would think. A man can grow used to weather. What he could not grow used to was being asked to decide things; for a decision, in that house, was a walk between two cliffs, and the cliffs had opinions.

The keepership of the Hall of Rest now had to be decided.

Madam Pyo required it for Scholar Ma of Poor Field. She said so at breakfast, in the tone she used for settled matters, which was most matters. The candidate was deserving; the children were fourteen; the promise — she used the word plainly — the promise had been given, by her, on the master's behalf, weeks ago. Nothing remained but the paper.

And yet the paper did not come. For on the other side stood Seol, and Seol had been at work on his master in the only room in the residence where the mistress did not sit.

You will remember what Seol wanted: Ahn restored, with conditions; the widow grateful; the fortune, in due season, folded into the household of the man who had managed it all. But Seol had a second want now, newer and hotter. It dated from the housewarming — from the hour at the couch, and certain remarks the inner room had since let fall about it. Seol had begun to want something for its own sake: to win against Madam Pyo. Openly. Once. A man who has been a secretary too long stores such wants the way a jar stores summer vinegar.

His method was patient. Each evening, over the papers, he dropped one small seed. The county says the master is generous — the county says it, I only report it. Drop. There is talk that the Ma appointment is the mistress's; a master's first appointment ought to be his own, or men will draw conclusions. Drop. Old Ahn is loved. The man who restores him will be loved. Ought that man to be anyone but the master? Drop, drop. Master Pyo listened, and hovered, and felt — dimly, deliciously, dangerously — the outline of an opinion forming in him, his own, or nearly.

The thunder, as always, arrived after the lightning; but it arrived.

One morning, alone in the outer court — Madam Pyo being engaged with the linen — the moment came. Master Pyo told Seol that on reflection, and on the merits, and in view of the feeling of the county, he was minded to restore Old Scholar Ahn to the Hall of Rest. Seol bowed low, to hide his face. Before noon he was in town, in the widow's parlor, announcing — gravely, joyfully, with his hand on his heart — that the thing was done. The master had decided. Her father would be restored, freely, as honor demanded. He said nothing of tracts, or weekly accounts, or the giving up of music. Those conditions were for a later conversation, when gratitude should have set like plaster. He asked, once more, that the family say nothing public yet. Then he walked back up the hill through the summer heat, and his hands, for one hour of his life, were dry.

That same afternoon, Madam Pyo sent word to Poor Field that the keepership was settled on Scholar Ma, and that the family might begin to pack.

I pause, reader, to defend the master, since no one in Baekcheon will. Master Pyo did not think of himself as having promised the post twice. Thinking of it would have required holding both conversations in his mind at once, and his mind, in matters of conflict, was like a small ferry: one passenger at a time. With Seol before him, the case for Ahn was the whole world. With his wife before him — but his wife had not been before him; that was the beauty of it; that was, if we are honest, the entire plan. He had signed nothing. He had merely been minded, aloud, in two directions. Many men govern whole provinces this way for years.

The two promises met, as promises do, in the servants' quarters.

Madam Pyo's maid heard it from the widow's cook's sister, and carried it to the inner room with the speed of good news. The mistress heard her out. She did not cry aloud. Those who wish to imagine Madam Pyo at this moment should imagine not fire but frost: the courtyard went quiet around her the way a pond goes quiet in the eleventh month, from the edges inward.

She went to her husband's study. She did not send; she went. The paper door slid open its accustomed inch. Then, for the first time in the residence's history, it slid the whole width. She came in through it, and stood before his desk, and asked him one question. Was it true.

What Master Pyo said is not recorded. What he attempted is generally agreed to have included the phrases feeling of the county, on the merits, and — bravely, fatally — a master's first appointment. At this last, Madam Pyo recognized the workmanship. It bore its maker's mark as plainly as a stamped ingot. She stopped listening to her husband at once, having heard, through him, all she needed of Scholar Seol.

"Send," she said, "for the secretary."

And here occurred the event which this chapter exists to relate. Seol came; and Seol did not submit. In the inner court of the residence, before the master's desk and the mistress's face, the secretary bowed his lowest bow. He regretted that in the matter of the Hall of Rest he had acted on the master's spoken instruction, which it was not a secretary's place to question. Should the instruction change, none would obey more swiftly than himself. But the instruction, as given, had been the master's. Every word was true, and every word was war. He kept his eyes down, and his hands were folded, and they were damp again, for the hour was upon him at last, and he found — as he had always suspected — that he enjoyed it.

Madam Pyo looked at Seol a long moment, the way one looks at a housecat found at the throat of a hunting dog.

"The academy," she said at last, "has been misinformed," and swept out; and the paper door, sliding shut behind her, closed the whole way, flush to the frame, with a small clean sound like a verdict.

War between the mistress and the secretary was now open, and the whole hill knew it by nightfall. What the hill could not know was the order of battle. The mistress held the household, the purse, and the master's fear. But the secretary held the master's vanity, which is the stronger fortress, being garrisoned by its owner.

Between them, hovering, stood Master Pyo, twice promised, half proud, and wholly miserable. And down in the town and out at Poor Field, two households — one with a geomungo, one with fourteen children — went to their beds happy, each believing the Hall of Rest was theirs. The reader, who knows better than to believe anything yet, is invited to sleep as soundly.

Chapter Ten — Mrs. Ma Walks to Town

Poor Field village lies three hours' walk from Baekcheon, on the wrong side of a hill that grows stones. The Ma family had farmed and taught there for four generations, each poorer than the last, and each, it was said in the village without unkindness, better loved.

Scholar Ma taught the village school. He was a small, worn, upright man who knew the classics soundly and had never once been anywhere that mattered at the moment that mattered. His wife had brought him, in twenty years, fourteen living children and a genius for stretching things — rice, cloth, firewood, hope — further than things go. You have heard that she counted the children nightly on her fingers by lamplight. This is reported in Baekcheon as a joke. It was not a joke. Twice in twenty years the count had come up short by one, and those two numbers were counted now with the rest, every night, at the end. She was not a woman anyone had ever accused of softness. She was the hardest-working and hardest-headed woman in three villages, and she loved her husband the way a roof-beam loves a roof: invisibly, and bearing everything.

To this household, in early summer, had come Madam Pyo's message: the keepership of the Hall of Rest was settled on Scholar Ma, and the family might begin to pack.

You must try to feel, reader, what that message meant at Poor Field, before you can feel what came next. It did not mean comfort. It meant existence. It meant rice without arithmetic. It meant the two eldest sons kept at their books instead of hired out at planting. It meant a dowry — a small one, a real one — for the eldest daughter, who was nineteen and had begun to be quietly unmentioned in her parents' evening talk, the way the poor unmention what they cannot mend. Mrs. Ma heard the message standing in her kitchen doorway. She said, "The mistress is gracious." Then she went in and sat down, which she had not done in daylight for some years.

They began, as instructed, to pack. There was not much, and fourteen children can wrap it quickly. The village made a small feast of farewell — a real feast; no wine need trouble us; the Ma family's celebrations ran to rice-cakes and noise. The school children cried. Scholar Ma made them a small speech about diligence, and cried himself.

And then the second rumor came up the stone hill, as second rumors do, wearing the friendly face of a neighbor's cousin. The appointment was off. The master had promised the Hall elsewhere, to Old Ahn himself, at the urging of the secretary. All Baekcheon knew it. The mistress had been beaten in her own courtyard.

Scholar Ma received this ruin with the classics. Heaven's decree, he said, was not to be quarreled with; they had been poor before, and were experienced at it; he would write, respectfully, to inquire — in a month, perhaps, when the great house had leisure. He then went to the schoolroom and taught the afternoon lesson with perfect steadiness, being that kind of man.

His wife was in the yard, hanging the wash. She finished hanging it. Those who knew Mrs. Ma could have read the signs in that: the pegging was even, and rather fast. Then she came in, took off her apron, and put on her market clothes and her good shoes — meaning the pair with the mended soles rather than the pair past mending. She told her eldest daughter what the little ones ate and when. Then she said she was walking to Baekcheon.

"To do what?" asked her husband, appalled, half-risen among his students.

"To count," said Mrs. Ma, "out loud."

Three hours, the wrong side of a stony hill, in the sixth-month heat. She did it in something under three, dust to the knee, and arrived at the academy's outer gate toward the end of the afternoon, and asked the gateman, civilly, for the mistress of the residence.

The gateman was civil back — to her clothes. The mistress, he regretted, received the county's ladies on the third day of the month. Petitions to the master's office went through the secretary. He recommended the secretary. He indicated, helpfully, the road back down the hill.

Now, a wife of the gentry would have had no move left, for a wife of the gentry has dignity to protect, and dignity is heavy baggage at a gate. Mrs. Ma had walked three hours precisely because she carried none. She did not argue with the gateman. She did not go to the secretary, whose name she had begun, on the walk, to use as a private curse. She sat down on the gate-step, in the shade of the royal signboard, took out her mending — she had brought mending; she had fourteen children; there is always mending — and said she would wait for the mistress's convenience, today, or tomorrow, as the house preferred.

A poor woman mending socks under a royal signboard is a small sight. It is also, to any great house with sense, a siege engine. Servants passed, and looked. A lecturer passed, and looked twice. The story of who she was, and how many children, and what had been promised, and what was now said, went over the wall faster than any petition had ever gone through the secretary; and it went, within the hour, where all household news went — to the inner room.

Madam Pyo came out to the gate herself.

Understand her rightly, reader, this once. It was not shame that brought her out, nor kindness only. Mrs. Ma at the gate-step was, to Madam Pyo, reinforcement arriving on the very morning of her war — proof, seated and sewing, of everything she held. Her promise was the true one. The secretary's meddling starved fourteen children to fatten one intriguer's schemes. Heaven, whatever the outer court imagined, kept accounts her way. But it was not only that, either. She stood at her gate looking down at the mended shoes and the road-dust, and something in the mistress of Crane Rock, some flint that her doctrine and her ledgers were built to protect, struck honest fire. She had never in her life been poor. She recognized it, at sight, as the enemy.

"You have walked from Poor Field," she said.

"I have, madam."

"You will not walk back tonight." And to the gateman, in a voice that reorganized his opinions about clothes: "The teacher's wife will take rice in the inner court."

What passed in the inner court between those two women, no man heard, and this storyteller will not invent it. But it is recorded that Mrs. Ma walked home the next morning by the cool of the day, with a fee for the eldest sons' school papers folded in her sleeve. She told her husband only, "The mistress keeps her promises." And from that day Madam Pyo carried the Ma family no longer as a patronage but as a flag.

Master Pyo was informed at supper that the matter of the Hall of Rest was to be reopened. He looked once at his wife's face. He then discovered that on reflection, and on the merits, and in view of the feeling of the county, he had always been chiefly anxious to do right by the deserving poor. The thunder, this time, arrived almost with the lightning. The Hall was Ma's again — for that week, at least, and in that court.

But the outer court had a resident too; and the secretary, hearing by nightfall how the day had gone, was not dismayed at all. Sieges, he reflected, work both ways; and he had engines of his own, and one of them, reclining on a couch at the lake house, owed him nothing and amused herself terribly. It was time, Seol decided, drying his hands upon his sleeves, to call in the county's other queen.

How that call was paid, and what it cost every single person concerned, the next chapters must tell.
