Brigadier Gerard

After Arthur Conan Doyle’s Gerard stories (1890s) — retold in plain English.
Chapters one to 7 — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. These are the adventures of Brigadier Gerard, written by Arthur Conan Doyle — the creator of Sherlock Holmes. Gerard is a French cavalry officer in Napoleon’s army, about two hundred years ago. He is brave, kind, lucky, and completely sure that he is the finest soldier in France. He tells his own stories as an old man, and he never notices when the joke is on him. Each chapter is a complete adventure.

Chapter One — How I Won My Medal

You wish to know how I won my medal. Everyone does, sooner or later. Old men ask me in the café. Young officers ask me respectfully, as is proper. Ladies used to ask me, and I would tell them the story slowly, because they deserved my best.

I always tell the truth. I won it by being the bravest man in the Emperor's army. The Emperor himself said something of the kind on the day he gave it to me — though he said it in his own way, and the Emperor's way with words was not always kind. Great men are like mountains, you understand: magnificent from far away, and very cold when you stand close.

Judge for yourself. Here is the story.

In the first weeks of the year 1814, France was a wounded lion, and the hunters were in the field. Russians, Prussians, Austrians — they had crossed our borders from three sides, more of them than there were rabbits in Champagne. Our army by then was boys and grandfathers, plus the last of the real soldiers, of whom I was one. We had been beaten several times that winter. I say this plainly, because it was in no way my fault.

I was a colonel of the Hussars of Conflans — the finest light cavalry in the world, which is to say, in France. We had two hundred men left, and I was the best of them, as any of the two hundred would have told you, once they had stopped pretending otherwise.

One freezing night, an order came down: Colonel Gerard to headquarters. At once.

I brushed my uniform, curled my mustache, and went. It is not every night that the Emperor sends for the best officer in his army, and I wished to look like the man he thought I was, which I was.

He was in a farmhouse full of maps and candle smoke, a small man in a plain grey coat, with his chief of staff at his elbow. Another officer was already waiting: Captain Charpentier of the dragoons — a big, slow, brave man who thought the way an ox walks: one heavy step at a time, arriving eventually.

The Emperor looked at the two of us for a long moment. There was something in his face that I took, at the time, for admiration.

"Gentlemen," he said, "I have chosen you two out of the whole army."

I bowed. I remember feeling briefly sorry for Charpentier, who was only there, I supposed, in case my horse went lame. But some missions are too large for one man's glory. The overflow must go somewhere.

There were two letters, the Emperor said, with the same words in each: for his brother, in Paris. We would ride that very night, by two different roads, through country that was crawling with Cossack patrols and Prussian horsemen. The letters must go through.

"Whatever happens," he said, "the letters must go through." And then, more quietly: "If you are taken — well. You are soldiers. These things happen."

There was a look in his eye when he said that. I did not understand it then. I understood it later. Wait, and you will understand it too.

He asked if my horse was fresh. My horse! I had Violette — the fastest mare in six brigades, a little chestnut lady who loved me nearly as much as I deserved, which is a great deal to ask of any horse. I told him that France herself was not fresher. He looked at me for a moment and said, "No. I imagine not." The Emperor was often brief with me. Great men fear, I think, to let their warmth show.

I rode out at midnight, with the letter sewn inside my boot, the moon on the snow, and glory ahead of me somewhere in the dark.

The first trouble was a Prussian picket at a bridge. They shouted a challenge. I answered in my best German — which consists of the word "Ja," said with confidence — and by the time they had finished thinking about it, Violette was over the bridge and gone. Pistols went off behind me. A ball passed through the top of my hat. I have the hat still. I show it to visitors.

The second trouble was a village full of Cossacks. The wise road went around the village. But it was long, and I was in a hurry, and besides, it was not magnificent. I went down the main street at a full gallop, sabre out, shouting my own name so that they would know whom to blame. I went through them like a needle through cloth. A lance ripped through my cloak, and I kept the cloak, too. My rooms, you will gather, are something of a museum.

The third trouble came near dawn, when I was half-asleep in the saddle. At a crossroads, quite suddenly, there were Russian hussars on every side of me — a dozen, then twenty, then more. Even the bravest man can be arrested by arithmetic.

They took my sabre, and my money, and my good opinion of that particular morning. They did not take my boots — which shows you they were young at this work — and they shut me in a barn to wait for an officer who spoke French.

I did not wait with them. Some hours into the night, my guard began to nod over his pipe, the way sentries do in the third hour. And outside in the yard, Violette whinnied. She was calling me. What could I do? A lady was waiting.

There was a loose plank; there was a quiet minute; there was a sentry who woke up too slowly, in the hay, some time after I had gone. I regret his headache to this day. I regret nothing else.

I came into Paris on the evening of the second day, having slept four hours out of forty-eight, been shot at, stabbed at, captured, and escaped — and I placed the Emperor's letter in the minister's own hands. I was frozen, starving, and filthy, and I was still — I say it with all modesty — the finest-looking soldier in that courtyard.

Ten days later, when the army had shifted and I had rejoined my hussars, I was summoned to the Emperor once more. I confess I had given some thought to what he might say, and to how I would refuse a promotion twice before accepting it.

He was reading when I came in, and he let me stand while he finished — which is a thing great men do, to remind you that you are furniture. Then he put down the paper and looked at me with eyes like two grey stones.

"Colonel Gerard," he said. "How, exactly, did you reach Paris?"

I told him. Modestly. It took some time.

While I spoke, his face did not move at all, and the chief of staff behind him wore the expression of a man watching a house burn down. When I had finished, the Emperor was silent for a moment.

Then he told me — in a voice you could have skated on — the thing that you, my friend, have perhaps already guessed.

The letter was false. Every word of it. It had been written to be captured — so that the enemy would read it, and believe he meant to fall back on Paris, and hurry their armies the wrong way, while he swung behind them and cut their supply road. He had needed two brave officers who would be certain to be caught, and he had chosen us for it. Charpentier — "who is a sensible man," said the Emperor — had been captured before midnight, as arranged.

"And you—"

He did not finish the sentence. Perhaps there were no words in French for it.

The room was very quiet. I confess it: for one entire minute, I felt like an ordinary man. It had never happened to me before, and I have taken care that it should never happen again.

It was the chief of staff who leaned down and murmured something — and I learned afterward what it was. The Prussians had taken Charpentier's copy after all. The trick had worked in spite of me. No harm was done, except to three Cossacks and one sentry's head.

The Emperor's face changed slowly, the way ice decides, in the end, to melt. And then he laughed — once, short, like a door shutting — and he opened a drawer, and took something from it, and came around the table to me.

He pinned the medal on my chest with his own hands. And he said, to the whole room, in that voice that could make a hundred thousand men cheer:

"Gentlemen — this officer has the thickest head and the stoutest heart in my army."

People have repeated that sentence to me all my life, thinking to tease me with it. Let them consider two things.

First: the Emperor of the French never said such a sentence about any other man. I have asked; he did not.

Second — and I invite you to notice it — both halves were superlatives.

That is how I won my medal. I have others. But that is the one I polish first.

Chapter Two — The Castle of Gloom

In the summer of 1807, the Emperor signed a peace with the Czar on a raft in the middle of a river — which gives you some idea of how things were managed in those days. No desk in the world was large enough for two such egos; a river between them was the only solution.

I was not on the raft. I had been sent east, into Prussia, with thirty hussars and an order I did not like.

A Prussian colonel named Streele was to be transferred from our keeping to a fortress called Gloom — and its name, I may tell you, was not a mistake. It stood in a pine forest on a hill above a grey lake. The pines were black. The lake was grey. The walls were built from dark stone that seemed to absorb the light. Even in July, there was ice at the margins of the water, and the guards at the gate wore the expressions of men who had said goodbye to joy some years ago.

I had met Streele once, when he was captured. He was a lean, grey man with good manners and tired eyes. I did not know what he had done. When I asked, I was told it was not my concern. In my experience, when someone tells you a thing is not your concern, it is about to become very much your concern indeed.

We rode for three days. Streele rode without complaint, which I respected. On the first night, by the fire, he said to me: "You are a French officer. You have a reputation for honour."

"I have a reputation for many things," I said, with my usual modesty. "Honour is not the one that keeps me awake."

"Whatever you have been told about me," he said, "I am not a spy. I am a soldier. A prisoner of war. I should be housed accordingly."

I had no good answer to that. My orders were clear. I followed them — though I rode the last miles a little more slowly than necessary.

The commandant of the fortress was a man called Gruber. I disliked him at once. There are men who love their work a little too much, and Gruber was one of them. He had small eyes and a loose mouth that smiled when smiling was not called for. He received my papers, looked at Streele, and said, "Good. We have been waiting for this one."

I did not like the way he said it.

I handed over Streele in the courtyard. The Prussian looked at me once, as they led him through a low door into the dark. He said nothing. I have thought about that look many times since.

My orders were to return immediately. Instead, I took my time with the horses. There was a groom in the stables, a Silesian boy who had been there three years and talked because he had no one else to talk to. I am an excellent listener when I want information.

He told me a great deal. About the sounds at night from the lower levels. About men who arrived and were never seen again. About the supplies that came in — not food, but other things: instruments, cords, a particular kind of basin that the boy described with his eyes on the floor.

I put my horse back in her stall. Then I went to find the commandant.

I want to be clear: I had no orders that covered what I did next. It was irregular. But I am an officer of France, and there are things we do not do to prisoners of war, and I have never needed a written order to know which things those are.

I told Gruber that my orders had been amended — verbally, by a general whose name came to me conveniently in that moment. I was to inspect the facility and report on its condition. I used the word "report" with care, to imply paperwork, and Paris, and offices full of men who read paperwork.

He was not a clever man. I am a very convincing one.

They opened the lower levels.

I will not describe in detail what I found. I have seen the field after a great battle; this was worse, because it was deliberate. I will say only that there were three men in conditions no treaty permits, and that Gruber, explaining them, used the word "necessary." He used it three times. On the fourth, I interrupted him.

"You will release these men today," I said.

"On whose authority?"

"Mine," I said. "The authority of Colonel Étienne Gérard, of the Hussars of Conflans, who judges that the conduct of this facility violates the conventions governing prisoners of war."

He laughed. He had thirty soldiers and I had thirty hussars, and he thought numbers were what mattered.

My men were in the courtyard.

By the time the matter was resolved, Gruber had a bruise on his chin, three men in French custody had blankets around their shoulders, and I had an interesting letter to write to Paris.

I will not claim it ended perfectly. I was reprimanded for exceeding my orders. Gruber, I was told, was not a subject for French interference. The report I wrote was read, filed, and no doubt misfiled shortly afterward.

But those three men were free. And Streele — who was exchanged the following spring, as these things go — sent me a short note some years later. He had heard what happened. He thanked me.

I have the note still. It is the only letter in my collection I did not write to myself.

Chapter Three — The Fox

I have fought in Spain, in Poland, in Egypt, and in Russia, and I will say this of the English: they are a brave people, and a proud people, and a people who have made the raising of very large dogs into something approaching a religion. I have no quarrel with the English. My quarrel was, on one occasion, with their fox.

It was in 1811. I had been captured — I will not go into the circumstances, as they reflect no credit on the men who captured me and none at all on the horse I was riding at the time. She was not Violette. Violette was wintering at Badajoz. The horse that threw me into the hands of a British patrol was a borrowed animal, and I borrowed nothing from that day forward.

As an officer of field rank, I was placed on parole at a house in Devon — a large estate, green and grey and dripping with rain. My hosts were a family called Pomfrey. They were perfectly civil. Colonel Pomfrey had his own stories from India, and we exchanged them politely, each of us believing the other's to be considerably exaggerated.

I was permitted to ride within a radius of five miles. I was permitted to attend church, which I did once for the architecture. I was, in the English phrase, "at liberty," which is what they call captivity when they are embarrassed by it.

In November, the foxhunting began.

The Pomfreys were kind enough to include me. I borrowed a horse — a proper English one this time, large and slow and very serious about hedges — and followed the party out on a grey morning, into fields where the frost was still white on the grass and the hounds were arguing about something at the bottom of a hill.

I understood the broad principle: there was a fox. The fox would run. We would follow. This sounded simple enough, and I said so to young Pomfrey, who was riding beside me.

He gave me an uncertain look. "Yes," he said, "that's essentially it."

"And when we catch the fox," I said, "what then?"

There was a pause. "Well," said young Pomfrey, "that rather depends on the hounds."

In France, we do not hunt for pleasure in quite this way. A man on a horse pursues something, he closes with it, he resolves the matter. I assumed this was the English version of the same principle, with dogs as an additional complication.

The hounds found the scent. The horn blew. Everyone moved.

Now: I was a colonel of hussars. Light cavalry is, above all things, about speed and the decision to commit before the moment passes. When the horn sounds and the enemy is in view, you do not wait to discuss it. You go.

I went.

I had some advantages. I was thirty-five years old, in condition, and my horse — despite his views on hedges — was a powerful animal once pointed in a direction. Also, I had spent twelve years in an army where the man who hesitated was the man who died, and this had given me, I confess, a very direct approach to obstacles.

There were three hedges. I went over them. There was a ditch. I went through it. There was a gate, which I found closed and elected to ignore. Behind me, I was aware of a general noise of horns and shouting, which I took for encouragement.

The fox was ahead, a small red shape in a large grey field. It was running well, I will grant it that. Better than most of the infantry I had faced in Germany. But I was mounted, and it was not, and what followed was, from a military standpoint, inevitable.

I caught the fox near a stone wall. It had turned to face me — small, wet, furious, in the manner of a trapped soldier who has run out of options — and I dismounted and picked it up, smartly, the way you seize a dispatch before someone can burn it.

There was a pause in the shouting behind me.

I walked back to the field, holding the fox at arm's length — it had opinions about this, which it expressed clearly — and presented it to Colonel Pomfrey with the compliments of the French cavalry.

He stared at me for a long time. The huntsman stared. Young Pomfrey had ridden to the back of the crowd and appeared to be examining his saddle.

"You caught it," said Pomfrey at last. "With your hands."

"Oui," I said. "It did not require the horse."

He looked at the fox. He looked at me. He looked at the assembled riders, some of whom were laughing in the quiet English way, which consists of saying nothing and going very red.

"In future," he said carefully, "we will explain the local customs more fully before you join us."

He released the fox himself. It shot into the brambles like a bullet and was gone in four seconds, which is about as fast as I have ever seen anything move that was not Violette.

There was a supper that evening at which I was something of a celebrity, though I was not entirely certain whether I was being celebrated or displayed. The English are hard to read in these situations. They applaud your energy with one side of the face and deplore your methods with the other, which requires a very mobile face indeed.

I consider it a successful campaign. The objective was reached, the quarry was secured, the party was enlivened, and the fox — I am glad to say — escaped. A fox that runs as well as that one did deserves its freedom. It had earned it the same way I had earned my medal: by not knowing when it was beaten.

Chapter Four — The Ajaccio Brothers

There is a question I am sometimes asked by people who did not know the Emperor: was he a good man? I always answer the same way. He was a great man. These are not the same thing, and anyone who has worked near greatness will tell you so.

His family was another matter entirely.

In the winter of 1808, I was stationed in Madrid for three weeks — not because I was needed there, which I was not, but because King Joseph required a bodyguard who would cut a fine figure at formal dinners, and someone at headquarters had remembered that I was available. I put on my dress uniform and reported to the palace, which was occupied, at that moment, by four of the Bonaparte brothers and one cousin, and which smelled of argument.

You know, perhaps, that the Emperor's family gave him more trouble than any of his enemies. His enemies he could fight. His brothers he had to support — which is harder, if less dramatic.

Joseph was the King. He was a thoughtful man, Joseph, with the eyes of a poet and the authority of a man who knows everyone knows his brother gave him the job. He was kind to me. We had long conversations at dinner, and he was genuinely interested in the men under my command, which is rarer in a king than you might think.

The other brothers were more complicated.

Lucien had quarrelled with the Emperor about a wife and was not speaking to anyone as a result. He spent his days writing poetry and conducting private wars with the palace cook. The poetry, I was told, was better than the cook's situation, though I did not read it.

Louis had been made King of Holland, which he found unsuitable and said so repeatedly to anyone who sat still long enough. He spent his holiday in Madrid complaining about the Dutch, the rain, and his feet, in that order of importance.

Then there was the cousin — I will call him Fabien, because his real name was longer and Corsican and had too many vowels for a soldier to use in an emergency — who was twenty-two, handsome, quick-tempered, and deeply in love with a Spanish girl whose father did not want French soldiers anywhere near his house, his family, or his country.

Fabien had solved this problem by climbing in through the window twice a week. The father had complained to Joseph. Joseph had complained to his aide. His aide had complained to the officer of the guard, which was me.

"Stop him," the aide said.

"Stop him from climbing in the window?" I said.

"From climbing in the window," confirmed the aide. "Before her father does something that will cause a diplomatic incident."

I thought about this. "Would it not be simpler to let him use the door?"

The aide's expression told me that simplicity was not the approach we were taking.

I found Fabien in the courtyard at ten in the evening, dressed in a dark coat and carrying a rope ladder in a way that suggested he had used it before. He was twenty-two and Corsican, which meant he regarded any instruction from anyone outside his immediate family as roughly equivalent to the opinion of a passing dog. He was also, I noticed, wearing the Emperor's name like a personal shield, in the way young men do when they have a famous relative and no better argument.

"The Emperor," he told me, "would understand."

"The Emperor," I said, "is in France. King Joseph is here, and he asks that you use the front door, like a man, and call at a reasonable hour."

"Her father will not—"

"Her father," I said, "is a proud man in a city full of French soldiers. He will receive a formal call from an officer in full uniform because it is the right thing, and he will do it, because doing the right thing costs him nothing and refusing it costs him everything." I looked at the rope ladder. "That, however, costs everyone."

He argued. He appealed to Corsican tradition, which I gathered permitted and possibly encouraged rope ladders. He appealed to love, which I concede is a stronger argument but not an infinite one. Eventually he appealed to my experience of such matters, which was, I admitted, considerable.

"Did you never," he asked, "do anything stupid for a woman?"

I considered the question seriously, as it deserved.

"I did everything correctly," I said, "and they were stupid anyway." I took the rope ladder. "Tomorrow morning. Uniform. Front door. I will come with you. Nobody refuses a colonel of hussars."

He looked at me for a long moment, calculating, the way young men do when they are deciding whether to trust someone who is not family.

"You will come," he said. "In uniform."

"The full dress," I said.

He handed me the rope ladder.

The call next morning was a success, in the way that French military diplomacy is usually a success, which is to say that everyone was polite, nothing was resolved, and I ate excellent pastries. The father was stiff. Fabien was nervous. I was magnificent. The girl appeared briefly, looked at Fabien, and went red, which was all the information I required.

They were married the following autumn, once the political situation had simplified enough to permit it. I received a letter. Fabien wrote that he had told his wife the story of the rope ladder, and that she had laughed for a long time, and that he was grateful to me for making it into a story rather than an incident.

I wrote back that it had always been a story. Most things are, if you arrive early enough.

The rope ladder, for what it is worth, I kept. It hangs in my study between the hat with the bullet hole and the cloak with the lance-tear, and visitors always ask about it last, because it requires the longest explanation.

I give it gladly. I have all the time in the world for a good story. At my age, stories are what remain when everything else is gone.

Chapter Five — The Wager

There are men who will tell you that pride is a vice. I have always found it a most convenient virtue, since it does the work of ten ordinary virtues and asks for nothing but admiration in return. I offer, as evidence, the affair of the wager at Wesel, which very nearly cost me my neck and taught me a great deal about the true value of my own reputation — which is to say, it taught me that my reputation was worth considerably more to other men than it was to me.

We were in winter quarters, the year I will not trouble you with, in a dull garrison town on the Rhine where the chief entertainments were drinking, dueling, and inventing reasons to do either. I had been there three weeks and had grown, I confess it, a little restless. A man of action, confined to a card table, is like a sabre left too long in its scabbard: he does not rust, exactly, but he loses his edge for want of use, and I did not intend to lose mine.

It was in this frame of mind that I fell into conversation, one evening, with a hussar of the Guard named Duplessis — a tall, cold, handsome man with the manners of a cat and, I am sorry to say, something of a following among the younger officers, who admired him in the way young men admire anyone who says little and looks bored while doing it.

"They tell me, Gerard," he said, in the tone of a man remarking on the weather, "that you are the finest horseman in the light cavalry."

"They tell you the truth," I said, for there was no sense denying it.

"They also tell me you have never ridden the Wesel road at night, in winter, along the river path, where the ice comes to the very edge of the track and a man who trusts his horse too far finds himself in the water before he has time to be surprised."

I confess my interest was engaged. "I have not had the occasion."

"I have ridden it," he said, "twice. Slowly, and by daylight, both times, and I do not mind telling you I was glad to arrive." He looked at me with those cold, amused eyes. "I would not care to try it at the gallop, in the dark, for any sum you cared to name."

You will understand, I think, that there was only one possible answer to a sentence like that, and I gave it. Within the hour it was arranged: I would ride the river path from the mill to the chapel, a distance of some four miles, at full gallop, at midnight, alone, for a wager of two hundred francs and — though nobody said this part aloud — the small, permanent, delicious satisfaction of having done what Duplessis said could not be done.

I should tell you that Violette was not with me that winter — she was resting a strained tendon in Paris, may every hour of her rest be blessed — and I rode instead a big raw-boned gelding named Marat, borrowed for the purpose from a captain of dragoons who wept, actually wept, when I brought him back the next morning covered in mud to the ears. But that is later in the story.

At midnight I was at the mill, in my greatcoat, with perhaps a dozen officers gathered to see me off, their breath smoking in the cold, their faces full of the particular excitement men feel when they are about to watch someone else take a risk. Duplessis was among them, saying nothing, which I took at the time for admiration and understand now was something closer to guilt.

I will not pretend the ride was comfortable. The path was exactly as bad as promised — ice to the left, black water beyond it, a track no wider than a good dining table, and Marat, who did not know me and did not love me, taking every turn as if he meant to test my faith in Providence rather than confirm it. Twice I felt his hind legs slide toward the river and twice I hauled him back by main strength and the sort of language a priest would not enjoy. But I have never in my life abandoned a wager once accepted, and I did not abandon this one. I came into the chapel yard at a hand gallop, half-frozen, entirely alive, and considerably more pleased with myself than the circumstances strictly warranted.

It was three days later that I learned the truth, from a lieutenant who had drunk more than his discretion could support.

The wager had not been about horsemanship at all. Duplessis, it transpired, owed a great deal of money to a merchant in Wesel, a hard, patient man who had begun to speak of magistrates. Duplessis needed, urgently, to be seen losing two hundred francs to someone in a public and memorable fashion, so that when the merchant's agent came asking after his finances, the whole garrison would swear that Duplessis had two hundred francs to lose on a foolish bet, and was therefore a man of substance and not a man who owed money he could not pay. My gallop down the river path — which I had genuinely believed to be a contest of nerve between two proud men — had been nothing more than a very expensive piece of theatre, staged for an audience of one merchant's agent, who had indeed been present that night, disguised, I was told, as a groom.

I sat with this information for some time before I decided how I felt about it.

You may imagine that I was angry. I was not, or not for very long. I considered the matter from every side, as a fair man should, and I arrived at this conclusion: I had ridden the Wesel road at night, at the gallop, on a strange horse, on ice, and I had done it magnificently, and that fact remained entirely true and entirely mine, whatever purpose Duplessis had smuggled in beside it. He had used my courage to solve his difficulty with the merchant. He had not created my courage, nor diminished it, nor even understood it, particularly well, since a man who merely wanted witnesses could have chosen any fool with a horse, and had instead — whatever his private reasons — chosen the one officer in the garrison who could actually be relied upon not to break his neck in the attempt. That, I decided, was itself a kind of compliment, buried inside the insult, the way a good egg is buried inside a bad omelette.

I did take the two hundred francs. A wager is a wager, and I am not a philosopher about money.

I did also, I confess, arrange a small return. At the next regimental dinner, in front of the same dozen officers who had watched me ride out, I proposed a toast to Captain Duplessis, "the most careful man in Wesel, who never risks a sou he cannot afford to lose" — which was true in every particular, and which the merchant's agent, still lurking about the town in his groom's clothes, heard repeated with great enthusiasm for a week afterward, until the merchant himself began to wonder whether his debtor might not be worth pursuing more energetically than he had planned.

Duplessis left the garrison rather suddenly, some weeks later, for a post I understood to be considerably less comfortable than the one he had held.

I still have Marat's old bridle, the one I rode that night — the captain of dragoons gave it to me afterward, along with several opinions about my care of borrowed horses that I have chosen not to record. It hangs now beside the rope ladder and the bullet-holed hat, and when visitors ask about it, I tell them it commemorates the finest ride of my career.

I do not always mention that it also commemorates a debt collector's evening off.

Chapter Six — The Portrait

Vanity, my friends, is a door that opens both ways. A man who is proud of his face will let almost anyone through it who offers to admire that face properly, and I have never claimed to be an exception to a rule I have watched ruin better men than myself. I offer you the story of the portrait, which cost me nothing but my dignity, and very nearly cost the Emperor a great deal more.

It was the autumn of 1809, and I was quartered for some weeks in a small town in the Tyrol, recovering from a wound that was, I will say plainly, less serious than the surgeon believed and considerably more serious than I permitted anyone else to believe, since a hussar recovering slowly is an object of sympathy, and a hussar recovering quickly is merely a man who was never much hurt to begin with. I chose sympathy. I have never regretted the choice.

It was during this convalescence that I made the acquaintance of a painter — a soft-spoken, gray-eyed man named Herr Voss, who traveled the region with a case of brushes and a letter of introduction from a colonel I happened to know, and who asked, with great deference, whether the celebrated Brigadier Gerard might do him the honor of sitting for a portrait.

I want you to understand that I did not agree out of vanity alone. I agreed because a man in my position owes something to history, and history, as everyone knows, is chiefly a matter of portraits. Show me a great man from a hundred years past and I will show you a painting of him, usually looking a good deal braver than the historical record strictly supports. I intended that my own portrait should require no such generosity from the artist.

Herr Voss came each morning for eleven days. He was a careful, patient worker, and I was, I flatter myself, an excellent subject — I have been told my profile improves upon acquaintance, and I gave him every opportunity to discover this for himself. We talked a great deal during the sittings, since a man cannot hold a pose in silence for three hours without losing his character entirely, and I told him — I will not pretend otherwise — a good many stories about my campaigns, complete with the position of roads, the strength of garrisons, and the small, useful details a soldier remembers and a civilian never thinks to ask for. He listened with flattering attention. He asked, now and then, a question so precisely aimed that I sometimes wondered, in an idle way, how a painter had come to know so much about the placement of artillery.

I did not wonder very hard. A man being painted has other things on his mind.

On the twelfth day Herr Voss did not come. Nor the thirteenth. By the fourteenth I had begun to make inquiries, and it was the town's own garrison commander — a stout, unimaginative colonel of infantry named Berthier, no relation to the marshal, thank heaven for both of them — who told me, with an expression I did not care for, that Herr Voss had been arrested two days before at the Austrian border, in possession of a set of very detailed sketches of the town's defenses, disguised, with what I am obliged to call considerable artistry, as studies for the background of a portrait.

The portrait itself, still unfinished, was found in his case along with the sketches. It showed me from the chest upward, in half profile, one hand resting on my sabre hilt, my expression noble and thoughtful — and behind me, rendered in loving and exact detail, precisely the sort of general view of the valley, the river crossing, and the old fort on the hill that an artillery officer would have paid a fortune to possess and an enemy engineer would have paid rather more.

"You sat," said Colonel Berthier, not unkindly, "for eleven days, in front of the one window in the Tyrol that looks directly at our powder magazine, and told him, by his own notes, the exact number of men garrisoned there."

I considered several responses and discarded each in turn as unworthy of the moment.

"He admired my profile," I said finally, "with great sincerity."

"I do not doubt it," said the colonel. "It appears to have been the least of what he was doing."

I will not pretend this was my finest hour. But I will say this in my own defense, and I have said it many times since, to anyone who raises an eyebrow at the story: a man cannot be blamed for being magnificent in front of a spy, only for failing to notice the spying. And I did, in the end, notice — a full week before the border guards did, though I flatter myself that the noticing came about entirely by accident.

It happened that on the tenth day, restless, I had risen mid-sitting to examine the canvas myself, over Herr Voss's mild protest that it was not yet ready for a subject's eye. What I saw was, I confess, magnificent — my own face rendered with more nobility than I generally credit myself with in the shaving mirror — but what I also saw, in the background, half-finished, was a sketch of the valley so precise that I remarked, in an idle way, that he had captured the old fort's ditch more accurately than the fort's own garrison engineer had managed on the official survey I had seen the week before.

Herr Voss laughed, said something modest about a painter's eye for detail, and steered me gently back to my chair. I thought no more of it — until Colonel Berthier's news arrived four days later, at which point the conversation returned to me all at once, the way an insult sometimes does, hours after it has been delivered.

I went at once to the colonel and told him what I remembered: the border crossing Voss had mentioned once, in passing, as a place he liked to walk for the light; the two days each week he claimed for "sketching in the hills," always the same two days, always the days the supply convoys came through. It was not much. But the colonel's men rode out that same evening, and I am told — though I did not witness it myself, having by then done quite enough witnessing for one autumn — that they found, in a hollow tree not far from the crossing Voss had mentioned, a small tin box of papers that made my unfinished portrait look like the least of his sins.

The portrait itself I never received, on the grounds that army property does not return works of art to the men who nearly gave away the fortifications while sitting for them. I have often wondered where it hangs now, if it hangs anywhere at all, and whether whoever looks at it understands that the noble profile in the foreground was, for eleven mornings, providing excellent cover for the ruin of the town behind him.

I tell this story less often than the others. A man may laugh at his own vanity, but there is a version of vanity that comes near to costing lives, and I do not care to make a comedy of that part, even for an audience that expects one from me.

I will say only this: I have sat for two portraits since, and on both occasions I chose the wall behind me myself.

Chapter Seven — The Night March

I have told you a good many stories in which I come out well, because I generally do come out well, and it would be false modesty to pretend otherwise for the sake of variety. But there was one night, in the terrible winter of 1812, when I came out alive and very little else, and I have never once been able to make it into a comedy, though I have tried, because a story that will not turn into a comedy is usually the one worth telling straight.

We were coming back from Moscow by then — I need not describe that retreat to anyone who has read a history book, and I have no wish to describe it to anyone who has not, for their own sake. The snow does not stop. The cold gets into a man's boots and stays there, a permanent lodger, indifferent to eviction. Horses died standing up and fell over some hours later, as if the decision took that long to reach their legs. I had, by the third week, perhaps forty men left of the two hundred I had commanded at the Beresina, and I loved every one of the forty the way a man loves anything he has managed, against every reasonable expectation, to keep.

On the night I am telling you about, we had fallen some way behind the main column — a matter of a blocked ford and a stupid, unavoidable delay — and word came back along the line, from a dragoon half-frozen in his saddle, that a party of wounded had been left behind in a barn some two miles off the road, too far gone to walk, forgotten in the general chaos the way a man forgets, in a burning house, that there was a second door.

I want to tell you that I did not hesitate, because that is the sort of thing I generally do tell you, and because in this one case it happens to be true.

I took twelve men — all I could spare, all I dared spare, in country thick with Cossacks who by that stage of the campaign had grown bold enough to take an army's stragglers the way a fox takes a farmer's chickens, without particular hurry or particular mercy. We rode out from the column into a darkness so complete that a man could not see the ears of his own horse, guided only by the dragoon's memory of a track that he himself admitted, twice, he was not entirely certain of.

We found the barn by the smell of it before we found it by sight — smoke from a small, badly hidden fire, which told me at once that whoever was inside had given up trying to survive quietly and had settled instead for being warm, which is what starving, freezing men do when the arithmetic of their situation becomes plain to them. There were nineteen of them, from four different regiments, none able to march, two already past helping. I will not describe the two. I think of them still, some nights, more than I think of the men I have killed, which surprised me the first time I noticed it and does not surprise me now.

I had brought no wagon — there had been no wagon to bring — and so we did what soldiers have always done when the arithmetic will not balance: we put two wounded men across each spare horse, tied where they could not fall, and the men without horses walked, holding the stirrups of those who had them, and I rode at the rear with my sabre out, because the rear is where a commander belongs when the danger is behind, and because I did not trust anyone else with the position, which I will admit now was less about duty than about pride, though the two were, that night, difficult to tell apart.

We had gone perhaps a mile when the Cossacks found us.

I heard them before I saw them — that particular soft, many-footed sound a large body of horse makes on snow, which is nothing like the sound they make on a road, and which every cavalryman learns to fear the way a sailor learns to fear a particular color of sky. I gave the order to keep moving, at whatever pace the wounded could bear, and I turned Marat — for I had kept him, in the end, out of a stubbornness the dragoon captain never quite forgave me for — and rode back alone into the dark to see what we faced.

I will not pretend I was not afraid. I have said before, and I will say it once more so there is no confusion on the point: courage is not the absence of fear. Courage is what a man does with the two extra seconds fear gives him, when a foolish man would have already run.

There were perhaps thirty of them, strung out, moving without great urgency, the way men move when they believe the night belongs to them and there is no particular hurry about collecting what it contains. I did the only thing available to a single officer facing thirty horsemen with nineteen wounded men to protect: I made a great deal of noise. I shouted orders to squadrons that did not exist, in three directions, in a voice I have been told carries half a mile in still air, and I fired both my pistols into the dark at nothing in particular, and I trusted — as I have trusted my whole career — that men who cannot see clearly will always assume the worst about what they cannot see.

It worked, for perhaps four minutes, which does not sound like very long until you have spent four minutes waiting to discover whether it worked.

The Cossacks drew off to consider the matter, the way a cautious dog considers a hedgehog, and in those four minutes my little column gained perhaps half a mile, which does not sound like very much either, until you understand that half a mile, on that retreat, was sometimes the entire distance between a man's grave and his supper.

They came on again eventually, having decided, correctly, that thirty men need not fear the noise of one. I lost two of the walking men in the running fight that followed — I will not call it a battle, it was too ragged and too brief for that dignified word — and I took a lance graze along my ribs that I did not feel until the next morning, which is generally how the worst wounds announce themselves, politely, after the danger that caused them has already passed.

But we came into the column's rear guard a little before dawn, seventeen wounded men still breathing, eleven soldiers still walking, and one colonel of hussars considerably less handsome than he had been the previous evening.

The dragoon captain, when he had counted heads and understood what the count meant, said nothing to me at all. He simply removed his own cloak, which was drier than mine, and put it around my shoulders without a word, and walked away before I could either thank him or make a speech of it, which was, I have since decided, the correct judgment on his part regarding my likely behavior in either direction.

I do not keep a trophy from that night. There was nothing to keep — no bullet-holed hat, no lance-torn cloak worth the telling, nothing but seventeen men who are, so far as I know, still alive today because a track existed and a dragoon remembered it and I did not stop to calculate the odds before I rode out to follow it.

I have told this story perhaps four times in my life, always to men who had themselves seen something of the kind, and never once for applause. Some stories are not for polishing. This is one of them, and I set it down here only because a true account of a soldier's life should include the nights he does not care to talk about, or else it is not an account of a soldier's life at all — merely a very long toast, raised in his own honor, by himself.
