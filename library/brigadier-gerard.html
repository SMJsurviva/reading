Brigadier Gerard

After Arthur Conan Doyle’s Gerard stories (1890s) — retold in plain English.
Chapter one — more to come. New chapters are added to this same page.

A note before you begin. These are the adventures of Brigadier Gerard, written by Arthur Conan Doyle — the creator of Sherlock Holmes. Gerard is a French cavalry officer in Napoleon’s army, about two hundred years ago. He is brave, kind, lucky, and completely sure that he is the finest soldier in France. He tells his own stories as an old man, and he never notices when the joke is on him. Each chapter is a complete adventure.

Chapter One — How I Won My Medal

You wish to know how I won my medal. Everyone does, sooner or later. Old men ask me in the café. Young officers ask me respectfully, as is proper. Ladies used to ask me, and I would tell them the story slowly, because they deserved my best.

I always tell the truth. I won it by being the bravest man in the Emperor's army. The Emperor himself said something of the kind on the day he gave it to me — though he said it in his own way, and the Emperor's way with words was not always kind. Great men are like mountains, you understand: magnificent from far away, and very cold when you stand close.

Judge for yourself. Here is the story.

In the first weeks of the year 1814, France was a wounded lion, and the hunters were in the field. Russians, Prussians, Austrians — they had crossed our borders from three sides, more of them than there were rabbits in Champagne. Our army by then was boys and grandfathers, plus the last of the real soldiers, of whom I was one. We had been beaten several times that winter. I say this plainly, because it was in no way my fault.

I was a colonel of the Hussars of Conflans — the finest light cavalry in the world, which is to say, in France. We had two hundred men left, and I was the best of them, as any of the two hundred would have told you, once they had stopped pretending otherwise.

One freezing night, an order came down: Colonel Gerard to headquarters. At once.

I brushed my uniform, curled my mustache, and went. It is not every night that the Emperor sends for the best officer in his army, and I wished to look like the man he thought I was, which I was.

He was in a farmhouse full of maps and candle smoke, a small man in a plain grey coat, with his chief of staff at his elbow. Another officer was already waiting: Captain Charpentier of the dragoons — a big, slow, brave man who thought the way an ox walks: one heavy step at a time, arriving eventually.

The Emperor looked at the two of us for a long moment. There was something in his face that I took, at the time, for admiration.

"Gentlemen," he said, "I have chosen you two out of the whole army."

I bowed. I remember feeling briefly sorry for Charpentier, who was only there, I supposed, in case my horse went lame. But some missions are too large for one man's glory. The overflow must go somewhere.

There were two letters, the Emperor said, with the same words in each: for his brother, in Paris. We would ride that very night, by two different roads, through country that was crawling with Cossack patrols and Prussian horsemen. The letters must go through.

"Whatever happens," he said, "the letters must go through." And then, more quietly: "If you are taken — well. You are soldiers. These things happen."

There was a look in his eye when he said that. I did not understand it then. I understood it later. Wait, and you will understand it too.

He asked if my horse was fresh. My horse! I had Violette — the fastest mare in six brigades, a little chestnut lady who loved me nearly as much as I deserved, which is a great deal to ask of any horse. I told him that France herself was not fresher. He looked at me for a moment and said, "No. I imagine not." The Emperor was often brief with me. Great men fear, I think, to let their warmth show.

I rode out at midnight, with the letter sewn inside my boot, the moon on the snow, and glory ahead of me somewhere in the dark.

The first trouble was a Prussian picket at a bridge. They shouted a challenge. I answered in my best German — which consists of the word "Ja," said with confidence — and by the time they had finished thinking about it, Violette was over the bridge and gone. Pistols went off behind me. A ball passed through the top of my hat. I have the hat still. I show it to visitors.

The second trouble was a village full of Cossacks. The wise road went around the village. But it was long, and I was in a hurry, and besides, it was not magnificent. I went down the main street at a full gallop, sabre out, shouting my own name so that they would know whom to blame. I went through them like a needle through cloth. A lance ripped through my cloak, and I kept the cloak, too. My rooms, you will gather, are something of a museum.

The third trouble came near dawn, when I was half-asleep in the saddle. At a crossroads, quite suddenly, there were Russian hussars on every side of me — a dozen, then twenty, then more. Even the bravest man can be arrested by arithmetic.

They took my sabre, and my money, and my good opinion of that particular morning. They did not take my boots — which shows you they were young at this work — and they shut me in a barn to wait for an officer who spoke French.

I did not wait with them. Some hours into the night, my guard began to nod over his pipe, the way sentries do in the third hour. And outside in the yard, Violette whinnied. She was calling me. What could I do? A lady was waiting.

There was a loose plank; there was a quiet minute; there was a sentry who woke up too slowly, in the hay, some time after I had gone. I regret his headache to this day. I regret nothing else.

I came into Paris on the evening of the second day, having slept four hours out of forty-eight, been shot at, stabbed at, captured, and escaped — and I placed the Emperor's letter in the minister's own hands. I was frozen, starving, and filthy, and I was still — I say it with all modesty — the finest-looking soldier in that courtyard.

Ten days later, when the army had shifted and I had rejoined my hussars, I was summoned to the Emperor once more. I confess I had given some thought to what he might say, and to how I would refuse a promotion twice before accepting it.

He was reading when I came in, and he let me stand while he finished — which is a thing great men do, to remind you that you are furniture. Then he put down the paper and looked at me with eyes like two grey stones.

"Colonel Gerard," he said. "How, exactly, did you reach Paris?"

I told him. Modestly. It took some time.

While I spoke, his face did not move at all, and the chief of staff behind him wore the expression of a man watching a house burn down. When I had finished, the Emperor was silent for a moment.

Then he told me — in a voice you could have skated on — the thing that you, my friend, have perhaps already guessed.

The letter was false. Every word of it. It had been written to be captured — so that the enemy would read it, and believe he meant to fall back on Paris, and hurry their armies the wrong way, while he swung behind them and cut their supply road. He had needed two brave officers who would be certain to be caught, and he had chosen us for it. Charpentier — "who is a sensible man," said the Emperor — had been captured before midnight, as arranged.

"And you—"

He did not finish the sentence. Perhaps there were no words in French for it.

The room was very quiet. I confess it: for one entire minute, I felt like an ordinary man. It had never happened to me before, and I have taken care that it should never happen again.

It was the chief of staff who leaned down and murmured something — and I learned afterward what it was. The Prussians had taken Charpentier's copy after all. The trick had worked in spite of me. No harm was done, except to three Cossacks and one sentry's head.

The Emperor's face changed slowly, the way ice decides, in the end, to melt. And then he laughed — once, short, like a door shutting — and he opened a drawer, and took something from it, and came around the table to me.

He pinned the medal on my chest with his own hands. And he said, to the whole room, in that voice that could make a hundred thousand men cheer:

"Gentlemen — this officer has the thickest head and the stoutest heart in my army."

People have repeated that sentence to me all my life, thinking to tease me with it. Let them consider two things.

First: the Emperor of the French never said such a sentence about any other man. I have asked; he did not.

Second — and I invite you to notice it — both halves were superlatives.

That is how I won my medal. I have others. But that is the one I polish first.
